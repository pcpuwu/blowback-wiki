    Blowback S2E3 — "New Normal"¶

    📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: 1:16:18 Thesis: The revolution's first two years (1959–60) — how an initially moderate government, pushed by US hostility, radicalized into a socialist one; the birth of the Miami counter-revolution and the US covert/terror campaign; and Cuba's drift into the Soviet orbit, ending in the embargo.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Obvious speech-to-text slips corrected against context.

    Cold open [00:00:00]¶

    • May 1959 — CBS Radio's "Is Cuba Going Red?" (later revealed, per ex-CBS newsman Robert Tabor, to have been produced under CIA supervision) calls Cuba "a communist beachhead" — the start of the long demonization. [00:00:00]

    The mob shut out [00:02:34]¶

    • The revolution's cleanup of the despised mob casinos doomed the mafia's racket. Trafficante tried to infiltrate the new government, was arrested for heroin dealing — "I thought the Marines were going to straighten everything out." The mob would have to wait to strike back. [00:02:34]

    The war-crimes trials [00:05:11]¶

    • The rebels tried Batista's worst torturers/killers in public (stadiums), executing 500+ (e.g. a naval officer who roasted six alive). The US press (NYT, New Republic) and politicians (a "bearded monster… Castro will be like Stalin") were horrified — hypocritically, given the ~1,000 killed after the US-backed 1933 coup and the thousands Batista's forces had killed. Fidel actively restrained vigilante justice ("it is the duty of a government, not vigilantes"). Even Dulles shrugged: "when you have a revolution, you kill your enemies." [00:05:11]

    Governing a bankrupt island [00:10:43]¶

    • The rebels inherited a drained treasury (capital flight, frozen funds, Batista's embezzlement); Che was put in charge of the National Bank. The government was strikingly young (average minister 33) and improvisational, yet enacted price controls, 50% rent cuts, forced the US-owned electric ($300M, 90% of power) and phone companies to cut rates, and made taxes progressive — "the business classes asked for honest government and were surprised to get it." [00:10:43]
    • Moderate → radical [00:14:00]: President Urrutia (moderate) vs. radicals; Feb 1959 Fidel becomes PM; July 1959 Urrutia resigns (after anti-communist noises); Nov 1959 Che → National Bank. Cuba wasn't yet communist (Dulles said so), but its tolerance of communists set it apart, while the moderates drifted toward anti-communism — drawing opposite lessons from Guatemala 1954. [00:14:00]

    Land reform — the point of no return [00:18:22]¶

    • May 17, 1959 — the Agrarian Reform Law: holdings over ~1,000 acres expropriated; landless families guaranteed ~66 free acres; compensation in 20-year peso bonds (not convertible to dollars); the powerful agency INRA built homes, stores, cooperatives. Within a year ~60% of land changed hands. Less generous to owners than the US's own postwar Japan land reform — but here US landowners (United Fruit) were the ones told to cough up, so the US denounced it and it juiced talk of intervention. [00:18:22]

    The counter-revolution is born [00:22:51]¶

    • The Miami exile wave came from Cuba's upper/middle classes, driven by what one psychologist called "the anticipation of loss." Cuban sociologist Marta Núñez Sarmiento (who stayed) describes her elite Ruston Academy emptying after land reform and the Oct 1959 urban reform — and how seeing poverty disappear made her a revolutionary. [00:22:51]
    • Batista's failed counter-revolution [00:29:24]: from the Dominican Republic and Florida he funded operations (the Biltmore Hotel HQ; mobster Norman Rothman running guns and firebombing missions over cane fields; the Pedraza Plan invasion, landed Aug 1959 and smashed). Lacking exile respect, Batista shut it down and faded into wealthy European retirement. [00:29:24]

    The US hardens [00:35:00]¶

    • April 1959 — Fidel's US trip; Eisenhower golfed and sent Nixon, who concluded Castro was a communist "to be watched." Land reform hardened Washington into demanding "speedy, efficient, just" compensation ("fuck you, pay me"). [00:35:00]
    • Oct 31, 1959 — a joint State/CIA program to support anti-Castro elements "while making Castro's downfall seem to be the result of his own mistakes" — rebranding a US-vs-Cuba confrontation (rooted since 1898) as a Cold War story. The US cut crop-duster sales, technical assistance, and credit, and threatened the sugar quota. [00:41:00]
    • Soviet caution [00:39:00]: the USSR sent KGB man Alexandr Alexiev only in Oct 1959 ("I could not understand what kind of revolution this was"). Fidel teased him — "in 42 years we'll also be bourgeois" — and stressed his real fear: US economic strangulation within two years. [00:39:00]

    The covert war takes shape (1960) [00:45:00]¶

    • March 1960 — Dulles, Bissell, Nixon et al. formalize the covert operation. The Frente (exile umbrella) is assembled; the leader-search is grim (Prío "would be great if he had morals"; Tony Varona — a CIA asset who in Aug 1960 cut a deal with Lansky to reopen the casinos post-Castro; Artime's MRR). A 500-exile paramilitary trains in Guatemala (ready Nov 1960). Spies inside Cuba included future Watergate burglar Frank Sturgis. Propaganda via Radio Swan (Honduras). [00:45:00]
    • Operation Peter Pan [00:52:24]: a CIA disinformation campaign (bogus "patria potestas" documents claiming the state would seize parental rights) led right-wing Catholic groups to ship ~14,000 Cuban children out of the country — many never returned. (Interviews: Pedro Pan kid Nelson Valdez; Marta, whose own school principal, James Baker, helped run it.) [00:52:24]
    • Assassination plots [00:57:53]: poison, LSD-laced cigars to humiliate him on air, thallium to make his beard fall out — then the serious route via the mafia (Maheu → Roselli → Giancana, with Howard Hughes backing it for his own post-Castro casino dreams); Giancana chose poison. Fidel survived by staying elusive (often at Celia Sánchez's apartment, guarded by women militia volunteers). [00:57:53]

    La Coubre & the Soviet embrace [01:02:32]¶

    • March 4, 1960 — the French freighter La Coubre, carrying Belgian arms, exploded in Havana harbor (~100 dead). Cuba (and a Belgian expert) concluded sabotage; Fidel blamed the CIA — and at the next day's memorial, the iconic Che photo was taken. Fidel predicted the US's four steps: more terror, a break in relations, sanctions, and an overt attack. [01:02:32]
    • The Soviet deal [01:06:16]: Mikoyan's Feb 1960 trade deal (1M tons of sugar/year for oil, machinery, factories, credit); after La Coubre, the USSR agreed to arm Cuba (100,000 rifles, ~30 tanks, no payment). [01:06:16]
    • The escalation spiral [01:08:00]: May 1960 US U-2 shot down over the USSR; Soviet oil arrives June, US refiners (Standard, Texaco, Shell) refuse to refine it → Cuba nationalizes the refineries; July Eisenhower cancels the sugar quota (700k tons, $93M) → the USSR buys it; Sept Cuba nationalizes electric, phone, banks, mills; the Declaration of Havana (Sept 2, "Cuba sí, Yankee no"); Fidel and Khrushchev first meet in Harlem (UN); Oct 19, 1960 — the embargo. [01:08:00]

    The balance sheet [01:11:00]¶

    • For all the chaos, two years brought real gains: rural housing/electrification/medical services, year-round employment and pensions for farm workers, desegregated clubs, the coming 1961 literacy campaign. Maurice Zeitlin's Princeton study found 2/3 of workers backed the regime years in (a copper miner: "Cuba is a cup of gold to me"; a former bootblack: "now I have three sons with a future"). Marta: the "jubilee" joy gave way to "we have to defend this." [01:11:00]
    • Che (to Look, 1960): "US pressure on Cuba has made necessary the radicalization of the revolution… to know how much further Cuba will go, ask the US government how far it plans to go." [01:14:30]
