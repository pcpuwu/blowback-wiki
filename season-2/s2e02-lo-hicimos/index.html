    Blowback S2E2 — "Lo Hicimos"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: 1:20:06 Thesis: The Cuban Revolution — traced from Spanish colonialism and the sugar-and-slavery economy through the US neocolony, the Batista dictatorship and its "gangsterismo," to Fidel's rise and the rebels' improbable victory on Jan 1, 1959, setting Cuba on a collision course with the United States.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Obvious speech-to-text slips corrected against context.

    Cold open: Nixon's verdict [00:00:01]¶

    • On his 1959 US trip, Fidel met VP Nixon for ~90 minutes; Fidel explained Cuba's planned social changes. Immediately after, Nixon sent Eisenhower a memo declaring Fidel a communist who "had to be eliminated." [00:00:01]

    America's century-old appetite for Cuba [00:03:00]¶

    • Quotes establishing the obsession: Jefferson (1820, the US "ought… to take Cuba"), Senator Stephen Douglas (1858, "it is our destiny to have Cuba"), vs. José Martí, godfather of Cuban nationalism: "I have lived inside the monster and know its entrails." [00:03:00]

    Spanish colony → sugar & slavery [00:04:11]¶

    • Spain arrived in the 16th c. and nearly wiped out the indigenous Taíno/Guanahatabey (the chief Hatuey as the first resister Fidel would later invoke). As the 18th→19th c. turned, French refugees fleeing the Haitian Revolution turned Cuba into a slave-plantation agribusiness — "the greatest slave-importing colony in the history of the Spanish Empire." [00:04:11]
    • Sugar became the trap: a one-crop economy (two-thirds of income), brutal cane-and-mill labor (axes kept by the rollers to hack off caught arms), enriching Europe and giving nothing back. The US, meanwhile, bought up Cuban land cheap (and offered $100M to buy Cuba in 1848). [00:07:51]

    1898: the US takes over [00:10:43]¶

    • 1895 — the final independence war; Martí dies; Spain's concentration camps (the origin of the term) starve civilians — Fidel later called it "the Vietnam of the 19th century." [00:10:43]
    • Feb 8, 1898 — the USS Maine explodes in Havana harbor (~260 dead) → the pretext ("Remember the Maine"). The Spanish-American War: Teddy Roosevelt's Rough Riders, Hearst's propaganda, the US seizing Cuba, Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines — Secretary of State John Hay's "a splendid little war." The pivot of US empire from the continent to the world. [00:16:00]
    • The neocolony (1898–1902) [00:14:43]: the economy "securely in American hands" (Goodyear, P&G, Texaco, etc.); racist election rules; a segregated paramilitary instead of a real army; the Platt Amendment (jammed into Cuba's 1902 constitution) formalizing the US right to intervene at will; and Guantánamo Bay. Marines returned in 1906, 1912 (after a massacre of thousands of Afro-Cubans), and 1917. [00:14:43]

    The republic of lackeys (1902–1933) [00:17:00]¶

    • A "lackey government" for US capital; tariff cuts juiced the sugar boom (the post-WWI "Dance of the Millions," 5¢→22.5¢/lb) until prices collapsed in 1920, taking Cuba's monocrop wealth with it. Enter US "viceroy" Enoch Crowder, dictating orders from the cruiser Minnesota parked in Havana harbor — "a colony in all but name." Ambassador Earl Smith later told Congress the US ambassador was "the second most important person in Cuba." [00:17:00]
    • Gerardo Machado (from ~1925) — a Wall-Street-beloved "tin-pot dictator" who crushed labor, students, and communists; ousted (negotiated out by the US) in the 1933 uprising. A "Sergeant's Revolt" then brought up Fulgencio Batista. [00:20:53]

    Batista & gangsterismo [00:22:31]¶

    • A pragmatic, non-ideological strongman; his progressive 1940 constitution was PR for a government whose real constituents were US capital — and the mafia. The "golden age of gangsterismo": Meyer Lansky built the Havana casino/hotel empire; Lucky Luciano (who lived at the Hotel Nacional and worked with US Naval Intelligence) eyed Cuba as a narcotics hub; the fabled 1946 Havana Conference of mob bosses. Batista took a 1944 "sabbatical" in Daytona Beach. [00:22:31]
    • The conditions [00:36:00]: read out via Fidel's later speech — 25% of farmers under eviction threat, most productive land foreign-owned, "we export sugar to import candy," 2.8M without electricity, 90% of rural kids riddled with parasites. In 1951, opposition figure Eddie Chibás told listeners to "sweep away the thieves" — then shot himself on air (Fidel was at his hospital bedside). [00:28:00]

    Batista's coup & the US embrace [00:29:24]¶

    • March 1952 — facing electoral defeat, Batista seized power in a 77-minute coup (Carlos Prío fled, reportedly leaving a coke stash). After assuring the US that Cuba would be "safe for private capital and dangerous for communists," the US recognized him, outlawed the Communist Party, and cut Soviet ties. Nixon visited in 1955 (toasting Batista, comparing him to Lincoln, urging a harder crackdown); CIA chief Allen Dulles helped set up Batista's anti-communist repression bureau (BRAC); US military aid jumped from $400k to $3M/year. [00:29:24]

    Fidel: Moncada to Mexico [00:37:00]¶

    • A well-off cane-grower's son, insatiable reader, lifelong protester and lawyer for the poor; an aborted 1947 plot against Trujillo; ran for Congress in 1952 until Batista's coup ended electoral politics. Realizing protest and politics were dead ends, he led the July 26, 1953 attack on the Moncada barracks — a failure; survivors not shot were tried (Lt. Sarría saved Fidel by telling him not to give his name). His courtroom defense ("History Will Absolve Me") laid out a revolutionary program; 15-year sentence, released 1955 under popular pressure (the July 26th Movement), then exile to Mexico. [00:37:00]
    • In Mexico, the Castros trained a guerrilla cadre under Spanish Civil War vet Col. Bayo; Fidel met Che Guevara (radicalized by the CIA's 1954 Guatemala coup). [00:42:00]

    The Granma & the guerrilla war [00:43:00]¶

    • Late Nov/Dec 2, 1956 — 82 men crammed into the dilapidated yacht Granma for a disastrous landing (ambush at Alegría de Pío); only ~12 reached the Sierra Maestra. Fidel: "now we have won the war." [00:43:00]
    • 1957 — why the comeback worked [00:46:00]: the rebels treated the campesinos humanely (paid cash, built schools taught literacy) and enforced a harsh justice code popular with peasants long abused by the rural guard; the eastern Oriente's landless workers were an ideal base; the El Uvero victory (May 1957). [00:46:00]
    • The urban underground [00:52:46]: strikes, sabotage, spying — and the women who ran much of it (per historian Michelle Chase): transporting weapons, propaganda, safe houses, intelligence (telephone operators eavesdropping) — Celia Sánchez, Haydée Santamaría, Vilma Espín. The beloved organizer Frank País was murdered by Santiago police in July 1957, sparking a huge general strike. The Communist PSP was initially standoffish toward the armed-vanguard strategy. [00:52:46]

    1958: the turn [00:59:46]¶

    • 1958 opens with Nixon's car attacked by crowds in Caracas (the US dispatching forces from Guantánamo to rescue him) — while in Cuba, campesinos identified US-made bombs killing them. [00:59:46]
    • The April 9, 1958 general strike failed — Batista unleashed retaliatory terror and morale cratered. But the rebels recovered, the communists and July 26th merged under Fidel as General Secretary, and Che and Camilo Cienfuegos marched west through swamps and hurricanes toward Las Villas. Eisenhower kept hoping for a "third force"; the CIA tried to block Fidel — but ~95% of Cubans backed Castro. [01:01:00]
    • The Battle of Santa Clara [01:11:00]: Che trapped Batista's armored train (gasoline trucks + dynamited tracks + bazookas + Molotovs), capturing $1M in ammo; civilians joined in; the city fell Dec 29–31, 1958. The war was effectively over. [01:11:00]

    Triumph — Jan 1, 1959 [01:14:30]¶

    • Dec 31, 1958 / Jan 1, 1959 — at a New Year's party, Batista heard the island was rising and fled to the Dominican Republic (having moved $300–400M abroad). Fidel's general strike secured the victory against any coup. Crowds smashed the mob's casinos; Fidel's Jan 3 Santiago speech: "this will not be like 1895… 1933… 1944 — this time it is truly the revolution." At Camp Columbia, a white dove landed on his shoulder. [01:14:30]
    • The reactions that set up the season: Trafficante and Lansky figured it would "blow over"; Dulles said Fidel wasn't a communist but was "suspicious" of Che; Camilo Cienfuegos: "We did it" — and Lansky told the FBI Cuba would "soon be communistic" and offered the US his help. [01:18:00]
