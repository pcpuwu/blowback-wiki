    Blowback S4E2 — "Bleeders and Dealers"¶

    ← S4E1 — Snake Eater

    S4E3 — The Trap →

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:10:00 Thesis: The deep prelude to the war — Afghanistan's ancient empires, three British invasions, the modernizing kings, decades of Soviet aid vs. American/Pakistani/Saudi sabotage, and the 1978 communist (Saur) revolution that curdled into chaos — until, on Christmas Eve 1979, the reluctant Soviets invade. The recurring pattern: foreign powers (Britain, then the US) fanning religious revolt against modernizing Afghan governments; and Brzezinski's "Afghan trap" to give the USSR "its Vietnam."

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Daoud ≈ "Dowd," Hafizullah Amin, Taraki ≈ "Tauriki," Brzezinski ≈ "Brzynski/Bersinsky," Khalq ≈ "Kalk," Parcham ≈ "Partchum").

    Old Afghanistan [00:02:30]¶

    • For 10,000+ years the crossroads of Persia, China, Central Asia, India — divided by the Hindu Kush (Pashtuns south; Tajiks, Uzbeks, Hazaras north). Islam arrived 651 CE; survived even Genghis Khan. Ahmad Shah Durrani (elected king 1747, Kandahar) founded the modern Afghan empire, which fell apart after his 1772 death. [00:02:30]

    The British invasions [00:04:52]¶

    • The 19th-c. opium trade (British monopoly to China) doubled poppy output in western India/Afghanistan — a foreshadowing of America's 2001 takeover. Afghanistan was also the great prize of the Anglo-Russian "Great Game." First Anglo-Afghan War (1839, ~21,000 troops to install the puppet Shah Shuja) — a secret East India Company war with forged documents about a Russian threat; by 1842 the British fled Kabul, ~20,000 dead, Shuja assassinated. Second War (1878) — Britain takes control of Afghan foreign policy for eight decades. 1890s — the Durand Line drawn (later the Afghanistan–Pakistan border); Afghans ignored it, the British enforcing it with "butcher and bolt" air raids. [00:04:52]
    • Britain jump-starts holy war [00:10:11]: long before the US, the British (and the WWI Germans, who planned a Muslim uprising) exploited religious revolts to topple uncooperative Afghan rulers. The Third Anglo-Afghan War (1919) — King Amanullah challenged the Durand Line and opened talks with the new Bolshevik USSR (the first government to do so). [00:10:11]

    The modernizing kings [00:12:45]¶

    • King Amanullah — Afghanistan's most radical reformer until the communists (outlawing oppression of women, slavery, honor killings; secular education, a constitution). His reforms provoked a conservative revolt — which Britain secretly backed (as Germany had planned, as the US later would) — and he fled to Italy (late 1920s). His successor reversed the reforms with British approval; assassinated 1933; the throne passed to the 19-year-old Zahir Shah (uncles ruling in his stead). [00:12:45]
    • Post-WWII [00:15:03]: the US-led Helmand Valley Project (1946) was a corruption-ridden irrigation disaster (the Morrison-Knudsen company drained Afghanistan's dollar reserves into floods and dead crops). Britain's partition of India (1947) created Pakistan amid mass ethnic cleansing (~1M+ dead); Afghanistan was the only country to vote against Pakistan's UN admission and renounced the Durand Line. [00:15:03]

    Daoud, the communists, and the Sino-Soviet context [00:16:19]¶

    • Prime Minister Mohammed Daoud (1950s) — the real strongman under the young king — wanted to "light my American cigarette with a Russian match," modernizing with Soviet aid as Pakistan turned hostile. A student "enlightened youth" movement birthed the future socialist leadership (Nur Muhammad Taraki, ex-US-embassy interpreter, co-founded the Afghan Communist Party). Strikes spread; by the mid-60s the small but hardcore party had hatched. The USSR needed Afghan allies amid its widening split with Maoist China. US analysts conceded by the mid-50s "the opportunity to win Afghanistan had been lost" — Soviet aid was "immensely successful." [00:16:19]

    CIA spycraft & Hafizullah Amin [00:19:29]¶

    • What the US lacked in diplomacy it made up for in spycraft — the Asia Foundation funneling CIA support to religious extremists (continuing the British playbook). And the curious case of Hafizullah Amin — sent to Columbia University (where Brzezinski then taught), president of the CIA-sponsored Afghan Student Association, his teacher-training school a CIA front. A 1977 internal Afghan Communist Party transcript records Amin admitting (then weaseling out of) CIA ties. The precedent: Brzezinski boasted of wrangling China to back Pol Pot's Khmer Rouge — was Amin "Afghanistan's answer to Pol Pot"? A CIA asset, a communist playing the Americans, or a free agent? Either way, viewed with suspicion in Moscow and Kabul. [00:19:29]

    The Safari Club & the 1973 coup [00:24:16]¶

    • 1973 — with the economy languishing under conservative King Zahir (80,000+ dead in the 1971–72 drought/famine), Daoud returned via coup, declaring a republic. At this moment foreign-backed Islamic soldiers were activated: Pakistani Gen. Babar brought Rabbani, Massoud, and Hekmatyar to Peshawar for secret guerrilla camps; the ISI took the failed-uprising leaders under its wing. The CIA's direct intervention also dated to 1973. Pushed right by Islamist and socialist pressure, Daoud took $2B from the Shah of Iran — who was part of a multi-country covert plot against Afghanistan. [00:24:16]
    • The Safari Club [00:29:31]: after Watergate hamstrung the CIA, a club of intelligence services (Iran, Saudi Arabia, France's de Marenches, China, Sadat's Egypt, even young Saddam) formed to outsource Cold War dirty work — confirmed by Saudi intel chief Prince Turki at Georgetown. Under CIA director George H.W. Bush, covert ops were delegated offshore and off-book; Saudi intel transformed BCCI into "the biggest clandestine money network in history" (its founder a friend of Pakistan's new dictator Zia). The Shah's SAVAK and Saudi money flowed to Afghan religious extremists. [00:29:31]

    The Saur Revolution (1978) [00:34:39]¶

    • By the late 70s Daoud was a paranoid lame-duck autocrat (cabinet packed with cronies, opposed to land reform). The murder of a leading communist brought tens of thousands into the streets; as Daoud cracked down, Amin's Khalq faction moved. April 1978 — the Saur Revolution: Daoud and his family shot dead; Taraki made president. The US blamed the USSR, but (per Braithwaite) the Soviets had no part — they'd have backed the gradualist Parcham faction, not Amin's Khalq; their Kabul embassy was "caught in the crossfire," Brezhnev learning of it from the press. The party's program: literacy, women's equality, ending ethnic discrimination, abolishing feudal relations — "declared war on the power of landowners and mullahs." [00:34:39]

    Carter, the bleeders, and the dealers [00:38:10]¶

    • A communist revolution in Asia was a tough pill for Carter. French Le Figaro (July 1979): the US wanted to use Afghanistan "as a lever" to rally Muslim states against the USSR. Sig Harrison's framing: the "bleeders" (hawks wanting to go for the Soviets' jugular) vs. the "dealers" (favoring détente). The bleeders won, led by Brzezinski — who had already worked to "destabilize the Soviet Union" by stoking nationalism/religion in its republics (CIA propaganda "above all to Ukraine"; distributing Wahhabi Korans). He sold Carter the false premise that the USSR engineered the 1978 coup as a march to the Persian Gulf (Peter Dale Scott: really the US "setting up an American pathway to the Caspian Basin" for oil). The dealers — Secretary of State Cyrus Vance and Ambassador Adolph Dubs (who saw Amin as a flippable nationalist) — were sidelined. The Chinese were enlisted to train Hekmatyar (Avrakotos: "a marvelous con job… Chinese communist guns to kill Russians"). [00:38:10]

    The revolution unravels [00:43:39]¶

    • The communists inherited a wreck (economy underwater, 40% undernourished) and their reforms were "unrealistic… Marxist intellectuals in a hurry" (Anwar). By 1979 protest curdled into religious-tinged revolt; mullahs spread "poisonous propaganda"; the party leaders prepared to "liquidate each other." Even Moscow protested the regime's Stalinist terror against the countryside — to which the party replied that what worked for Stalin would work in Afghanistan. A new Soviet-Afghan treaty allowed Kabul to request Soviet military aid — an article Amin would invoke a year later, "himself to become the first victim of the invited Red Army." [00:43:39]

    1979 — the year of fire [00:45:00]¶

    • Iran's revolution (the Shah's fall) ended the post-war Middle East order. February 1979 — Ambassador Adolph Dubs kidnapped and killed in a botched Kabul rescue (Brzezinski "saw it as a massive opportunity"). Khomeini returned to Iran. Washington's nightmare: the USSR reaching through an anti-American Iran to the Persian Gulf — via red Afghanistan. [00:46:51]
    • Herat [00:50:01]: March 1979 — locals rose against girls being sent to school, killing communist officials and Soviet advisors (~800 dead per Anwar; Western accounts inflated it). The panicked Afghan communists asked Moscow for troops. [00:50:01]

    The Soviet refusals [00:50:43]¶

    • The Politburo decision-makers (Gromyko, Kosygin, Ustinov, KGB chief Andropov) repeatedly refused troops: Andropov — "tanks could not solve what was essentially a political problem"; Gromyko — it would undermine détente and be "a splendid present for the Chinese." Brezhnev ruled it out; Kosygin told Taraki the Vietnamese had defended themselves without foreign soldiers. Through spring/summer 1979 — uprisings, mutinies (Bala Hissar in August), repeated troop requests, all denied ("In no circumstances," said Ustinov). [00:50:43]
    • The bleeders escalate [00:54:04]: after Herat, Brzezinski ordered increased support to militants in Pakistan; the CIA Islamabad station chief pledged US support to "a known religious fanatic and drug trafficker — Gulbuddin Hekmatyar." [00:54:04]

    Amin kills Taraki [00:56:00]¶

    • Sept 1979 — the KGB first floated removing Amin. Sept 14 — a meeting at the palace turned to gunfire; Amin then had three reluctant officers execute Taraki (officially "a brief and serious illness"). Andropov, "mortified," was determined to get rid of Amin — but Soviet influence in Kabul was near zero. Amin's rule executed ~27,000 in one prison alone; he controlled maybe half the country, courted the US ("we want… more assistance"), and reached toward Hekmatyar and Zia — "doing a Sadat on them." [00:56:00]

    The decision to invade [01:00:00]¶

    • Braithwaite: "step by step, with great reluctance… the Russians slithered toward a military intervention because they could not think of a better alternative." A KGB October survey warned an invasion "would be opposed by the entire country." Nov 4 — the Iran hostage crisis; Nov 20 — the Grand Mosque seizure in Mecca; Nov 21 — the US embassy burned in Pakistan. The KGB brought their chosen successor, Babrak Karmal, to Moscow. Dec 8–12 — the Politburo decides; Dec 10 Ustinov orders a 75,000–80,000-troop plan (Gen. Ogarkov, against it, was "surprised and angered"). [01:00:00]
    • The "Afghan trap" [01:03:11]: Soviet generals later asked why the US issued no warning — concluding it was a deliberate trap. Brzezinski confirmed the "Afghan trap" in his 1998 Le Nouvel Observateur interview ("we now have the opportunity of giving the USSR its Vietnam War") — later claimed a mistranslation, but corroborated by CIA's Chuck Cogan (Brzezinski doubled down at a funeral) and Bill Odom (Brzezinski shot a fist in the air: "They have taken the bait"). Negotiator Paul Warnke: had the dealers been heeded, "there would [not] have been any Afghanistan invasion" — the Politburo voted ~six times, five against, until the hardliners said "you're getting nowhere with the United States" (Salt II unratified). [01:03:11]
    • Christmas Eve, 1979 — 80,000 Soviet troops enter Afghanistan. Their target: Hafizullah Amin. [01:09:02]
