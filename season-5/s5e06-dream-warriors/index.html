    S5E7 — Less Than Zero →

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:10:00 Thesis: 1973–75 — the bloodiest year (the most savage bombing of Cambodia, climaxing the secret war that became "the Southeast Asia Division of Watergate"), Nixon's fall (and the impeachment article over Cambodia that Congress abandoned), the Khmer Rouge's radicalization and break from Hanoi, and the twin collapses: Phnom Penh and Saigon both fall (April 1975). The Khmer Rouge enter the capital — silent, not smiling.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Khieu Samphan ≈ "Q Sampan," Ieng Sary ≈ "Yang Sari," Neak Luong ≈ "Neklung," Zhou Enlai ≈ "Joe N-Lai," Drinan ≈ "Drennan/Dronin").

    Cold open: Kissinger ascends as Nixon falls [00:00:00]¶

    • Fall 1973 — Kissinger threatened to quit unless made Secretary of State; Nixon, weakening, gave him both jobs (SoS + NSA). By spring 1973 Congress was as scandalized by the ongoing Cambodia bombing as by Watergate; Nixon agreed to end it by August 15. [00:00:00]

    Watergate timeline [00:04:11]¶

    • What felled Nixon was domestic, not foreign — "dirty tricks" and the June 1972 Watergate break-in. March 1973 — burglar James McCord's letter to the judge (the trail leads to Nixon); April — Haldeman, Ehrlichman, Dean ousted; May — Senate hearings; June — Dean testifies Nixon ordered the cover-up; July — the White House taping system revealed; and July — the Senate Armed Services Committee convened hearings on the secret bombing of Cambodia. [00:04:11]

    "The Cambodian cover-up" [00:06:43]¶

    • Nixon's repeated lie (the US had "scrupulously observed" Cambodia's neutrality) collapsed: Hersh's reporting and Senate testimony exposed all of Operation Menu — the falsified records, the burned paper, Kissinger running it himself. Columnist Mary McGrory: "the Southeast Asia Division of Watergate." Ex-major Hal Knight (who'd burned the records) and other airmen testified to the falsification ("I was in the position of falsifying a report to someone who had… told me what to put in it"). "Hidden history of U.S. moves in Cambodia emerging." [00:06:43]

    1973: the bloodiest year [00:11:24]¶

    • After Paris, the Vietnamese withdrew most troops, leaving the Khmer Rouge to fight alone. The returning Hanoi-trained old guard were viewed with suspicion — "Vietnamese minds and Khmer bodies," the body-snatcher phrase encapsulating Khmer Rouge xenophobia (the same jingoism as Lon Nol's, but aimed at "a far more receptive audience, the peasantry"). Lon Nol's government hit its nadir (a general strike, all opposition arrested incl. Sirik Matak; a pilot bombing the palace, killing 43, then defecting to the Khmer Rouge). [00:11:24]
    • The last theater [00:14:22]: with Laos's bombing ended (Feb 1973), Cambodia was "the only game in town" (CIA's Colby) — or, per a State official, "the re-election of the president." The Khmer Rouge refused Lon Nol's ceasefire (to keep their advantage); Hanoi cut their supplies to force it (Khieu Samphan: "Hanoi has dropped us"). Zhou Enlai warned the French: "the longer the war… continued, the more extreme and harsh the final victory would be" — but Kissinger wasn't listening (still believing the Khmer Rouge were Hanoi's puppets). [00:14:22]
    • The Neak Luong bombing [00:18:49]: at its height, 81 bombing runs/day (exceeding Vietnam's 60). In August an errant B-52 strike leveled the town of Neak Luong (the embassy called the damage "minimal"; a soldier: "All my family is dead… Take my picture. Let the Americans see me"). In Cambodia the US dropped three times the tonnage dropped on Japan in WWII (atom bombs included); Phnom Penh swelled to 2.5 million. Becker: "I cannot exaggerate how unprepared Cambodians were… 'why is there fire coming from the sky?'" — the bombing gave the Khmer Rouge an "outsized sense of invincibility." [00:18:49]

    How the Khmer Rouge fought [00:24:00]¶

    • They lost a dozen men for every Lon Nol soldier; Lon Nol's troops mythologized them as marijuana-fueled demons. They hugged enemy lines to avoid the bombs, and won "the Rice War" — equitable distribution in their cooperatives vs. Phnom Penh's corrupt, starving misery (officers fleecing refugees at roadblocks, buying virgins for brothels). But their zones became garrison states ("miles-wide tracks of no-man's-land"; people who crossed "were not seen again"). The choice: "Risk starvation as a refugee in the Khmer Republic, or give your soul to the Khmer Rouge in exchange for the assurance of being fed." [00:24:00]

    The pilots' revolt & the bombing's end [00:28:32]¶

    • Airmen rebelled — Captain Donald Dawson refused to fly; a federal judge (Orrin Judd) issued an injunction barring the bombing. The case bounced to the Supreme Court; Justice William O. Douglas, from his Washington cabin, ruled for Dawson (citing Truman's unconstitutional steel seizure: "if Truman could not seize the steel… I do not see how any president can take life in violation of the Constitution") — but the DOJ got a phone poll of the justices to reimpose a stay. The bombing ended anyway in mid-August 1973 per the congressional deal. Rep. Robert Drinan (a Jesuit) introduced the first impeachment resolution (July 31) — over Cambodia. [00:28:32]

    China's realignment [00:33:09]¶

    • The Sino-Soviet contest flipped: the USSR went more revolutionary under Brezhnev, while China courted "a politically eclectic cast" (Haile Selassie, Mobutu, the Shah) to build an anti-Soviet third-world front — abandoning the Maoist groups it once funded, recognizing Pinochet, and backing the pro-apartheid side in Angola ("the last straw in the eyes of many"). This drove China further from Hanoi (Soviet-backed) and closer to the anti-Vietnamese Khmer Rouge (Khieu Samphan presenting Zhou Enlai a grenade launcher at an April 1974 banquet, ruling out all compromise — "a critical moment which symbolized an important shift in Chinese policy… toward a military end"). [00:33:09]

    Nixon's fall [00:36:14]¶

    • The Cambodia impeachment push (Drinan → Conyers, Article 4) threatened to "indict an entire system of policymaking" — and was resisted by Democrats who'd known about the bombing. Nixon's White House crumbled anyway (Agnew resigned; the Saturday Night Massacre; the War Powers Act passed over his veto — I.F. Stone: "Nixon's bitterest defeat in foreign policy"). The Cambodia article was abandoned 26–12 (Rep. Hungate: "it's kind of hard… to impeach a guy for tapping telephones and not for making war without authorization"). The Supreme Court ordered the tapes released; Aug 8, 1974 — Nixon resigned. The night before, he and Kissinger prayed on their knees ("Henry, please don't ever tell anyone that I cried"); a drunken late-night call ("you must stay on for the good of the country"). Gerald Ford took over. [00:36:14]

    The end approaches [00:44:00]¶

    • Ford, "gun-shy" and hemmed by Congress, did nothing as Hanoi tested him (taking a province in Dec 1974). By late 1974 the Khmer Rouge shelled Phnom Penh "less like the Cuban rebels fighting for Havana and a lot more like the Afghan Mujahideen shelling Kabul." Phnom Penh rotted — opium parlors, brothels, slave boys, starving children (Schanberg from Neak Luong: "every child… is in some stage of malnourishment"); conscription gangs grabbing boys from movie theaters; 250% inflation; 96% of income from the US (and unlike Soviet aid to Cuba, "none of the rice… was provided for free"). [00:44:00]

    Pol Pot-ification [00:48:53]¶

    • As Phnom Penh's squalor grew, so did Khmer Rouge radicalism (refugees citing US bombing in 1973, then Khmer Rouge violence in 1974). Pol Pot purged Sihanoukists (Sihanouk to Oriana Fallaci: they'll "spit me out like a cherry stone"). In the zones, moderate reform gave way to sweeping dictates — money abolished, religion suppressed, flirting punished, everyone in black pajamas. Becker on the distinct terror: unlike China/Vietnam's open political executions, Khmer Rouge terror was secret ("the work of God"), "a bottom-up anarchistic execution of a top-down authoritarian dogma." The party was known only as Angkar ("the organization"). [00:48:53]

    Saigon and the failed peace [00:52:21]¶

    • The Paris Accords ended direct US involvement (591 POWs released) but not the contest. Thieu violated the ceasefire; Hanoi planned to win; Washington distanced itself. Kissinger made "no effort to end the war in Cambodia" ("Henry shot the dove off his shoulder… I don't want to hear about Laos-type compromises"). The French diplomat Étienne Manac'h tried to broker a Sihanouk-led coalition — but Beijing had shifted to a military solution (Khieu Samphan's grenade launcher), and a leaked communiqué (terms resembling Kissinger's unacceptable ones) killed the last chance. [00:52:21]

    The fall of Phnom Penh [01:01:04]¶

    • March 1975 — the Khmer Rouge took Udong (forcing 20,000 into the countryside, executing "class enemies"). The US ambassador: "I've got orders to fight to the last Cambodian." Sihanouk's deputy met George H.W. Bush's office (retrieving Sihanouk's old films); Kissinger, desperate, offered Sihanouk the head-of-state role "no conditions" — too late, the prince declined. The last US aid convoy came and went; Lon Nol fled; the US embassy evacuated; the city's defenses were overrun. The Cambodian war's toll: ~150,000 from US bombing (Kiernan), 275,000–300,000 overall. [01:01:04]

    The fall of Saigon [01:02:24]¶

    • Congress refused Ford's $1B aid ("we would just be wasting the money"); Thieu resigned; the North's final assault launched April 29. The chaotic embassy evacuation (Kissinger alone in "an eerie silence"); April 30 — the communists seized the palace; Saigon became Ho Chi Minh City. Kissinger, the only survivor of Nixon's inner circle (escaping the docket), could "savor an eerie silence." Shawcross's ledger: the end of American deaths, the China opening, détente, SALT — against "the continuing, extended war in Indochina where no real peace had ever been sought," the Bangladesh atrocity, the Allende destabilization, the tilt to apartheid Africa. [01:02:24]

    The silent victors [01:08:43]¶

    • April 17, 1975 — the Khmer Rouge entered Phnom Penh to euphoric crowds ("No more rockets… neighbors dancing and singing"). But "within a few hours, the atmosphere of celebration fizzled out. Something was off about the incoming soldiers. They did not smile. They did not speak." The resistance radio's call for "law and order" cut out; a new voice: "We are not coming here to negotiate. We are entering the capital through force of arms." Then the radio went dead. [01:08:43]
