    Blowback S5E3 — "Listen to the Thunder"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:11:00 Thesis: The Americanization of the Vietnam War (1955–68) and Cambodia's effort to stay out — Sihanouk's abdication-to-rule masterstroke and tightrope neutrality; the US building Diem's South Vietnam against Geneva; the Sino-Soviet split; the secret war in Laos; the Khmer Rouge driven into the jungle; the Gulf of Tonkin fabrication; Tet, 1968, and the massacres (My Lai, Speedy Express); and Nixon & Kissinger's treasonous sabotage of the 1968 Paris peace talks to win the White House — buying "four more years" that would doom Cambodia.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Diem ≈ "Ziem," Khieu Samphan ≈ "Q Sampan," Anna Chennault ≈ "Anna Chenow," Thieu ≈ "Tew/Hugh").

    Cold open: Kissinger in Saigon [00:00:02]¶

    • 1965–66 — Harvard professor Kissinger toured Vietnam; publicly he affirmed US goals, but his private diaries called the Army's optimistic reports "eyewash" ("a war which we knew neither how to win nor how to conclude"). Intoxicated by Saigon's intrigue, he began to see Vietnam as "one grand puzzle to be solved, and who better to solve it" — confiding: "If I were a dictator here, I would not know where to begin." [00:00:02]

    Sihanouk's masterstroke [00:05:32]¶

    • March 1955 — Sihanouk abdicated the throne to rule as a politician (his Sangkum party) — "to conservatives, still the god-king; to radicals, the Democrat who won independence and gave up the throne." He left Cambodia "on autopilot" (a feudal kingdom of barons paying tribute), with a Marxist-vocabulary "royal welfare" socialism and a personality cult ("his photograph on every page of every Cambodian newspaper"). [00:05:32]
    • Neutrality and US pressure [00:09:10]: at Bandung Sihanouk affirmed Cold War neutrality and befriended Zhou Enlai, refusing to join SEATO — for which US-aligned neighbors "nibbled at Cambodia's flanks" (Thai border incidents, South Vietnamese airspace violations, blocked Mekong convoys). Allen Dulles found him "overconfident"; Nixon found him "vain and flighty." [00:09:10]

    Two Vietnams [00:11:15]¶

    • The North focused on feeding itself (Saigon blocked north-south food trade; famine loomed) and a violent but successful land reform (driving ~1M middle-class/Catholic people south) — "much more focused on getting its own house in order." The South was chaos that "suited the United States just fine": Washington built Diem's anti-communist police state against Geneva's reunification elections (Eisenhower: "possibly 80% would have voted for Ho Chi Minh"). Diem rigged elections, refused the vote, and his forces arrested ~25,000, devastating the southern communist movement — while Hanoi still "clung to peaceful reunification." [00:11:15]

    The Sino-Soviet split [00:17:43]¶

    • Khrushchev's "peaceful coexistence" (dropping the inevitability of capitalist-socialist war; aid to develop states rather than back revolutionaries) clashed with Beijing (non-nuclear, bloodied in Korea, seeing the USSR "sacrificing revolution in the third world for stability in Europe"). The late-1950s Sino-Soviet split "will be a major factor in the wars to come over Laos, Vietnam, and Cambodia." [00:17:43]

    The South fights back [00:20:17]¶

    • A militant Hanoi faction (Le Duan) rose as Diem's crackdowns intensified and elections died; Hanoi authorized southern bases and assassinations, birthing the Ho Chi Minh Trail and (Dec 1960) the National Liberation Front (the "Viet Cong"). [00:20:17]

    The secret war in Laos [00:21:32]¶

    • The first Cold War crisis in Indochina came from Laos (a majority-minority country). The US fought to block even a neutral government (CIA bribes and a right-wing coup); after the 1962 Kennedy-Khrushchev Geneva deal (ignored), the US began its own secret war — recruiting Hmong militias and a decade-long air campaign that "destroyed hundreds of thousands of lives." [00:21:32]

    Cambodia's holding pattern [00:24:06]¶

    • Sihanouk's neutralism abroad didn't soften his anti-communism at home (barring leftist teachers — incl. arresting Saloth Sâr's brother; breaking strikes; coining "Khmer Rouge"). Cambodia's left was weak and unbacked (the Soviets refused them; Beijing hadn't yet engaged) — its future leaders refining their ideas (Khieu Samphan's moderate 1959 Sorbonne thesis; later beaten and jailed). The party's first Congress met "in an empty rail car." [00:24:06]

    JFK's escalation [00:27:53]¶

    • Le Duan hoped the Laos deal would bring a Vietnam settlement; instead Kennedy (post-Bay of Pigs, unwilling to look weak) escalated — "Project Beefup" doubling aid, tripling "advisors," approving defoliants, herbicides, and napalm, and the brutal Strategic Hamlets forced-resettlement program. The Viet Cong's victory at Ap Bac (Jan 1963) shattered the delusion. Diem's brother opened secret channels with the North → the US backed a coup (Nov 1963) in which Diem was shot; 20 days later JFK was assassinated in Dallas (having just ordered a secret troop-withdrawal start). [00:27:53]

    Cambodia's merry-go-round [00:32:05]¶

    • Sihanouk, paranoid after Diem's killing, swung between right and left ("at first the prince favored the right… then in 1963 he turned to the left… then in 1966… reinstate the right"). Student riots (un-organized by the communists, who paid the price anyway) produced an enemies "list" — and the future Khmer Rouge fled Phnom Penh (some in car trunks): newly-elected party secretary Saloth Sâr, Ieng Sary, Son Sen — to a Viet Cong base in the jungle. [00:32:05]

    The Gulf of Tonkin [00:34:36]¶

    • LBJ wanted no showdown with China but believed covert ops + air power could win cheaply — yet he resisted "13 separate attempts" to get him to authorize force, while Hanoi "actively sought openings for a negotiated settlement" (no US response). August 1964 — after raids on Tonkin islands, an Aug 2 attack was reported and an Aug 4 attack fabricated (Pentagon analyst Daniel Ellsberg on his first day; LBJ on tape doubting it: "maybe they hadn't fired at all"). The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution flipped public opinion to 2-to-1 support; no peace moves before the election. [00:34:36]

    The Khmer Rouge ferment [00:40:47]¶

    • At "Office 100" the Khmer guerrillas privately endorsed armed violence against Sihanouk (against Hanoi's pro-Sihanouk line; the Vietnamese gave them very little aid — hence their watchword "self-reliance"). Their ideology took Maoist "mind over matter" to an extreme, increasingly denying a role for the working class (by 1965 refusing factory workers as "infiltrated") — raising eyebrows among the orthodox Vietnamese. Sihanouk, betting the North would win, let the Viet Cong use eastern Cambodia and shipped them supplies via Sihanoukville, then broke relations with Saigon ("the fate of Vietnam appears to me to be sealed"). [00:40:47]

    LBJ's war unravels [00:44:27]¶

    • Christmas/Feb 1965 NLF attacks → Operation Rolling Thunder (March 1965) and a troop surge (125,000 → 200,000+); Hanoi mobilized hundreds of thousands and Chinese non-combat troops arrived. The war "refused to go America's way," cracking LBJ's coalition (the first big SDS peace protest, 20,000 in DC). In Cambodia, Sihanouk let the 1966 election run free — it lurched right, making Lon Nol ("Black Papa," the racist Buddhist mystic who'd told the US military "there is a point beyond which the military will refuse to support the chief of state") prime minister; Sihanouk retreated into amateur filmmaking. [00:44:27]
    • The Samlaut uprising [00:49:13]: April 1967 — a peasant revolt in Battambang over rice seizures; Sihanouk ordered Lon Nol to "liquidate the rioters" (villages razed, peasants clubbed to death); Khieu Samphan and the remaining socialists vanished (believed dead for years). Sihanouk renounced US aid: "look what happens when you put your trust in the free world." [00:49:13]

    Phoenix and the dark methods [00:50:32]¶

    • 1967 — Saigon rigged its election (Thieu, 35%); the US leaned on the Phoenix Program (CIA-led "neutralization" of civilians — torture, detention, assassination; quotas of 1,800/month; atrocities staged to look like the enemy's), which spread into Cambodia (a captain putting a bullet between his Cambodian informant's eyes). US military spending hit a post-Korea peak (9.4% of GDP). [00:50:32]

    Tet and the massacres [00:56:12]¶

    • Jan 31, 1968 — the Tet Offensive (assaults on 100+ cities) collapsed US public support (50% → 33%). March 1968 — LBJ announced he wouldn't seek re-election; MLK and RFK were assassinated; the chaotic Chicago Democratic Convention. And the massacres: My Lai/Son My (500+ civilians killed, raped — stopped partly by pilot Hugh Thompson; covered up for a year), and Operation Speedy Express (a secret Pentagon probe: up to 7,000 civilians killed — "a My Lai each month"; Westmoreland quashed the investigation). [00:56:12]

    Sihanouk reverses [01:01:12]¶

    • Impressed by the communists' Tet casualties (and prodded by Lon Nol), Sihanouk reversed his bet — opening to the US if it recognized Cambodia's borders. But the Americans were already crossing those borders (CIA mercenaries, "civilian irregular defense groups," and the US-created anti-Sihanouk Khmer Serei under Son Ngoc Thanh). Meanwhile the Sino-Soviet split squeezed Hanoi (the USSR now its biggest backer; China demanding Hanoi snub Moscow and reject peace talks). The time was ripe for negotiations — Paris talks were prepared. [01:01:12]

    Nixon's treason [01:04:08]¶

    • Kissinger ("of all the men running, Richard Nixon is the most dangerous") had dug up Nixon's "shit files" for Rockefeller — then, his candidate out, switched to Team Nixon, offering to spy on the Paris peace talks for them. Nixon (a lifelong escalation hawk) saw his chance: peace before the election would help the Democrats. His message to Saigon: "Hold on… Your real friends are coming." The hotline was DC socialite Anna Chennault (China Lobby grande dame), passing word to South Vietnam's President Thieu ("I would much prefer to have the peace talks after your election"). [01:04:08]
    • The sabotage works [01:09:03]: Oct 31, 1968 — LBJ announced the bombing halt and Paris talks ("peace was near"); but Nov 1, Thieu publicly opposed the talks. "The Nixon-Chennault conspiracy had won." Hitchens: it meant "four more years of an unwinnable, undeclared, and murderous war, which was to spread" — "and those four more years spelled the end for neutral Cambodia." [01:09:03]
