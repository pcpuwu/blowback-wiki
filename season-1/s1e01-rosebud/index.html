    Blowback S1E1 — "Rosebud"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: 58:18 Thesis: Before 9/11 and the 2003 invasion, trace the origin of America's relationship with Iraq — British colonialism → Cold War → the rise of Saddam — and the central irony: the US built Saddam into an ally before nominating him "global supervillain." Did Saddam turn on us, or did we turn on Saddam?

    Notes follow the episode's narration order so you can read along while listening. [hh:mm:ss] = jump point in the audio. Real-world dates in bold. Obvious speech-to-text date slips are corrected against context (e.g. the narrator's "Sept 22 1921" = the 1980 invasion).

    Cast introduced¶

    • Saddam Hussein — born near Tikrit; country boy, 21 at the 1958 revolution. The "Joker." [00:14:02]
    • Ahmed Chalabi — born Baghdad 1944, 9th child of a wealthy Shia banking family, 14 at the revolution; lifelong obsession with restoring (and ruling) a lost, idealized Iraq — the "Rosebud" of the title (Citizen Kane reference). The "Batman." [00:16:40]
    • Donald Rumsfeld — the through-line: Nixon protégé → Saddam's interlocutor → architect of the 2003 war.

    Cold open: the Rumsfeld throughline [00:00:02]¶

    • 1971 — Nixon, in the Oval Office, advises young ex-wrestler-turned-bureaucrat Rumsfeld: "there's nothing you can do about the Middle East." [00:00:02]
    • Dec 20, 1983 — Yet 13 years later Rumsfeld strides through Baghdad's Republican palace to shake Saddam's hand, offering US support for Saddam's war on Iran. "He's not a madman… he's a strong man, an intelligent man." Iraqi cameras film it. [00:00:56]
    • 2003 — During the war buildup, CNN replays that footage to Rumsfeld; he bristles ("Where did you get this video?"), then days later makes the WMD case from the White House podium. [00:01:31]
    • The framing question [00:02:08]: how did Rumsfeld get from coffee with Nixon (1971) → Saddam (1983) → showdown (2003)? And the bigger one — how did Saddam become a US friend and ally, only to be cast as global supervillain? Did he turn on us, or did we turn on him? And what does that reveal about why we really went to war in 2003? Rumsfeld, shown the old footage, didn't look ashamed — just "Isn't that interesting? There I am." [00:02:42]
    • Why "Rosebud": before getting to 9/11 and the Bush administration, the hosts want to trace the origin of America's "perverse relationship" with Iraq — a story of British colonialism, Cold War politics, and the rise of Saddam. Two characters emerge from the coming 1958 revolution: Saddam, and Chalabi — the self-appointed arch-nemesis who flees in the '50s vowing to return with an American army. [00:04:48]

    British Iraq & the oil order [00:06:32]¶

    • 1920 — Churchill, complaining of an uprising in British-ruled Iraq, writes: "I am strongly in favor of using poison gas against uncivilized tribes." Mustard gas wasn't available that time, so the British just bombed Iraqis — men, women, children — "until it was quiet again." [00:06:32]
    • 1921 — The British stage an election; Faisal I is installed as king as a single candidate with 96% of the vote. Hosts' aside: the British Empire "did everything first… but very deadpan" — chemical attacks, divide-and-rule — vs. the more earnest American style ("Jim and Pam get married and Iraq gets to be a real democracy"). [00:07:18]
    • ~pre-WWI ("the Great Unrest") — Churchill brokers a secret sweetheart oil deal (the company → eventually BP) and converts the British Navy from coal to oil. Analytical point: switching to oil was, like automation today, a labor-control move — it stripped leverage from striking coal workers (Churchill was also the man putting down those strikes). [00:08:14]
    • early 20th c. — Western oil firms form a cartel and create the Iraq Petroleum Company — which gives zero shares to Iraq itself; the companies effectively run the country. [00:09:05]
    • after WWII — Even before the war ended, US elites fixed on the USSR as the real enemy (a bigger threat to them than the Nazis ever were). A now-superpower America starts eyeing Iraq as a Cold War bulwark against communism and socialism. [00:09:36]
    • late 1950s — The Soviets repeatedly propose a Middle East non-interference pact (arms embargo, troop withdrawal, base closures); the West listens politely and says "how about no." Point: the Cold War framing was opportunistic. [00:10:18]
    • State of Iraq by 1958 [00:10:46]: a British outpost + Cold War front + oil-cartel holding. The wealth was real but flowed to British/American oil firms; Iraqis lived in squalor — infant mortality ~250/1,000, no social services.

    The 1958 Revolution & the Ba'ath [00:11:33]¶

    • July 1958 — Revolution: the Free Officers under Gen. Qasim topple the British-backed monarchy. King Faisal goes down; PM Nuri al-Said is murdered — his body run over by trucks "until it was a fine pink paste." Backdrop: Nasser of Egypt, the avatar of anti-colonial pan-Arabism, whom Qasim is imitating. Eisenhower & Nixon hold their breath and prep a possible intervention to block the Iraqi Communist Party (which had real support — "extremely well organized"). [00:11:33]
    • Meet the two characters [00:13:47]: out of this revolution emerge "the Batman and Joker."
    • 1957 — A year before the revolution, Saddam (21 in '58) joins the Ba'ath Party. Aside on the Ba'ath: a hodgepodge ideology (nationalism + pan-Arabism + a little socialism) whose real glue was anti-communism — never a mass movement; an elite/military cadre that ends up serving its leaders. Saddam said he became a nationalist, not a communist, because class divisions where he grew up "were not that significant." [00:14:02]
    • Chalabi [00:16:40] — born 1944 into wealth, 14 at the revolution; biographer Aram Roston describes him as "pampered, happy, and studious," blind to the squalor around him. Watching his idealized Iraq "come crashing down" sparked a lifelong obsession to restore it and rule it. A relative: "If you're looking for Rosebud, like Citizen Kane, look to the 1958 revolution." He didn't get his American invasion that year — and spent the rest of his life working on it.
    • Qasim's problem [00:18:09]: he pleased nobody — angered the US/UK by nationalizing oil, angered the Ba'ath for not being revolutionary enough, and unsettled everyone with communist allies. Also begins fighting the Kurds (intro to who the Kurds are: a minority across Iraq, Syria, Iran, Turkey — for now allied with everyone against Qasim).
    • Oct 7, 1959 — A "dashing" 22-year-old Saddam, machine gun in hand, is part of a botched 6-man hit team sent to kill Qasim. Amateur-hour ("the JV team of coup plotters"): guns jam, they shoot one of their own, Qasim survives (wounded), Saddam takes a bullet in the leg and flees to Cairo via Syria. [00:19:19]
    • In Cairo — Per journalist Said Aburish, Saddam makes contact with the local CIA station to advance schemes against Qasim. The CIA, the Ba'athists, and other Arab leaders all wanted Qasim gone. [00:21:13]
    • Spring 1962 — JFK asks the CIA to plot Qasim's overthrow. Point man: Archie Roosevelt Jr., brother of Kim Roosevelt (who ran the 1953 Iran coup). The CIA's real "Health Alteration Committee" sends a poisoned handkerchief. [00:21:53]
    • Feb 1963 (Ramadan) — Ba'ath-allied officers stage a coup and execute Qasim on live TV (corpse shot up, buried in a shallow grave). The CIA question: a Ba'ath secretary later said "we came to power on a CIA train"; CIA Middle East chief James Critchfield called it "a great victory"; an anecdote of Kurds joining the Ba'ath for a CIA stipend. Verdict: CIA presence at least confirmed; Saddam likely a junior asset. Then the anti-communist purge: death squads "winkle out the communists" — ~5,000 killed, ~15,000 detained, prisoners sent to camps run with the Iraq Petroleum Company. A US diplomat: "We were very happy. They got rid of a lot of communists… This was a great development." [00:22:52]
    • Saddam returns from Cairo and climbs the party ladder (myths of him running torture rooms — unverifiable); marries his wife Sajida, starts a close family. [00:25:53]
    • US backing — Within hours of the coup the US recognized the regime; in April it began shipping arms (used to "hack away at the Kurds"). Irony: the USSR went to the UN and called the Ba'ath a "fascist regime" committing "genocide" — the very charge the US would insincerely level decades later. [00:26:48]

    Saddam's rise, the Shah & the 1970s [00:27:43]¶

    • 1968 — The Ba'ath retakes power; in the US, Nixon wins and brings in Kissinger. [00:27:43]
    • 1970 — Nasser dies; Sadat succeeds him in Egypt and expels the Nasserites; pan-Arabism is on the wane. The story becomes about what America does, and America needs "boys." [00:28:02]
    • The Shah of Iran [00:28:40] — the US's ideal regional partner from Kennedy to Carter (a "secular monarchy and dictatorship, thanks to our 1953 coup"); a "huge piece of shit" who tortures and kills opponents.
    • 1970s — Kissinger's triangulation [00:29:46]: play Iran and Iraq off each other, and arm the Kurds against Baghdad (money, guns, encouragement). The Shah loved this — it tied down his Iraqi rival.
    • early 1975 — the Algiers Agreement [00:30:36]: Saddam (now the de facto strongman, officially #2) and the Shah, US-mediated, settle the Shatt al-Arab border and agree to stop sniping. A win for everyone except the Kurds, whose US/Shah benefactors abruptly said "oops, gotta go" and left them to slaughter.
    • 1970s — a genuinely successful Ba'ath state [00:31:39]: oil wealth + well-administered quasi-socialism made Iraq "probably the most dynamic and progressive state in the Arab world." Iraqi activist Ra'ed (interviewed): blooming economy, one of the region's best education and health systems — a post-colonial success story (alongside real political dictatorship). Saddam visibly wrenches Iraq's oil reserves from Western firms — a hugely populist move he'd dine out on forever.
    • Chalabi in the 1970s [00:33:10]: math professor at the American University of Beirut → flees the Lebanese Civil War → takes up his banking "birthright," founding Petra Bank in Jordan. Assets balloon $40M (1978) → $400M (1982); he builds a mansion and a Rolodex of Western journalists, think-tankers, and politicians. One insider sensed "something was not kosher" (payoff next episode).
    • July 1979 — Saddam takes full power [00:34:38]: he eases out his cousin Hassan al-Bakr ("you look ill, let me cover the presidency"), then stages the infamous Ba'ath Party Purge — a crowded, videotaped assembly where a visibly tortured man confesses to a "Syrian-backed plot" and reads out names of "co-conspirators" in the room, who are hauled away as the rest weep and pledge loyalty.

    The Iran–Iraq War (1979–1988) [00:36:40]¶

    • Jan 16, 1979 — The Shah flees Iran; the Islamic Revolution (Shiite clerics atop a broad coalition) takes power; Khomeini returns from exile, denouncing the foreign advisors who "ruined our culture and took our oil." [00:37:01]
    • Sept 22, 1980 — Saddam, seeing an opening, invades Iran after weeks of skirmishes. [00:38:48]
    • Stated cause: control of the Shatt al-Arab waterway — the conduit to the Persian Gulf and the oil-shipping routes that dictate both economies.
    • Deeper driver: Saddam (Sunni ruler of a Shia-majority country) feared a spillover Shia revolution. There were real stirrings — cleric Mohammad Baqir al-Sadr (Moqtada al-Sadr's father-in-law) called for an uprising; Saddam put him to death — an early note in the multi-generational al-Sadr thread. [00:40:38]
    • US posture: a Shia power center outside US control terrified the Gulf monarchs. Whoever won would dominate world oil — so the US wanted Saddam to move with at least quiet acquiescence. At the Sept 1980 UN Security Council, Western powers blocked any condemnation of Saddam; Iran (correctly) sensed everyone's thumb on the scale. [00:41:05]
    • The war bogs down [00:42:39]: Saddam hoped for an Israeli-style six-day Blitzkrieg, but his air attacks failed and Iran — having studied Israel's mistakes — halted his 100,000-man ground offensive. By Oct 1980 it was a stalemate; a month later Iran ran a naval blockade. It became "the longest conventional war of the 20th century," the closest thing to a second WWI. Iranian-backed Kurds opened a northern front. [00:42:39]
    • June 1981 — Israel bombs Osirak [00:44:41]: with Iraq pursuing a bomb (Italian/French help), Israel destroys the reactor. French historian Pierre Razoux's irony: only after Osirak removed the nuclear question did Washington and Moscow reconsider re-engaging Baghdad.
    • The US tilts to Iraq [00:45:44]:
    • 1981 — Diplomat William Eagleton cables Reagan's new government: "a greater convergence of interest with Iraq than at any time since the revolution of 1958." King Hussein of Jordan signals a Western softening; Moscow resumes delivering fighter planes.
    • 1982 — State Dept removes Iraq from the state-sponsors-of-terrorism list. [00:46:07]
    • Chemical weapons [00:46:34]:
    • 1983 — Iraq begins gas attacks on Iran (mustard gas).
    • 1983–84 — Rumsfeld's two visits to Iraq (the cold-open handshake); a March 1984 UN report confirms Iraqi CW use — and days later the US re-establishes full relations anyway. The US wasn't just okay with it — it would provide targeting intelligence to make the gas more effective, and sold precursor materials. [00:47:01]
    • mid-1980s — where the capability came from [00:48:30]: a "short list" of Western suppliers — W. Germany, Spain, Britain, France, Norway, Italy, Switzerland, Austria, the Netherlands. W. Germany sold CW materials to Iraq and protective gear to Iran. The US Commerce Dept rubber-stamped anthrax/insecticide shipments (exposed by a 1990s Senate Banking Committee report). A UK arms scheme later toppled John Major's government and opened the door for Tony Blair.
    • The tangled logic [00:49:40]: US arms/intel/CW to Iraq vs. Iran; Iran-Contra (selling Iran arms to fund Nicaraguan death squads); Israel bombing Iraq yet the US feeding Iran intel — not actually contradictory, because the US goal wasn't for either to win but for both to end up exhausted, weak, and under American domination.
    • 1987 — the decisive US tilt [00:50:19]: US satellites spot an Iranian buildup aimed at a gap in Iraq's lines (a breakthrough could've taken Basra and the oil fields). A colonel later recalled a message from Reagan himself: "An Iranian victory is unacceptable." After the US mapped targets, Saddam launched a sarin attack — it worked. Two-thirds of all Iraqi CW use came after 1987.
    • The Kurdish front & Anfal [00:51:24]: the Kurds, "re-weaponized whenever convenient," became a focus. In 1987 Saddam launched the Anfal campaign — widely called genocide; 50,000–100,000 Kurds dead — the latest in a Ba'athist drive to "Arabize" minorities (Kurds, Assyrians, Yazidis). An Iraqi voice: "To us [enemies] is okay… but why to his own people?"
    • March 16, 1988 — Halabja [00:52:30]: in response to an Iranian push, Saddam orders his cousin Hassan al-Majid ("Chemical Ali") to gas the Kurdish town of Halabja — napalm, sarin, mustard, tabun; 3,000–5,000 dead. Halabja finally pushed the international community to call for the war to end.
    • July 3, 1988 — Iran Air Flight 655 [00:53:51]: amid the "tanker war," the USS Vincennes shoots down an Iranian passenger plane — 290 dead. The US initially blamed the Iranian pilot; in fact Capt. Will Rogers III had taken the Vincennes into Iranian waters — and was later awarded a medal. GHW Bush (campaigning): "I will never apologize for the United States. I don't care what the facts are."
    • Summer 1988 — the war ends [00:55:03]: ~180,000 Iraqi + ~500,000 Iranian dead, ~1.5M wounded. Iraq emerges with a 1M-man army (5× its 1980 size) but ~$100B in debt. Kissinger's line: "It's a pity they both can't lose" — but, the hosts note, they both did, and Iraq "would never be the same."
    • Closing aside — the USS Stark [00:55:58]: back in May 1987, Iraqi missiles hit the USS Stark, killing 37 US sailors — yet Saddam sent a quick apology, submitted to a brisk inquiry, and Reagan demanded no compensation. The point: by now Iraqi lives, Iranian lives — and even American troops' lives — "don't matter, because it's just business." The episode ends on Reagan eulogizing the Stark dead over the "lifeblood" of Gulf oil. [00:55:58]
    • Cliffhanger: next episode → the Gulf War, "the final blow." [00:56:04]
