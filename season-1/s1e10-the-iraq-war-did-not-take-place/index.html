    Blowback S1E10 — "The Iraq War Did Not Take Place"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: 1:05:26 · (Series finale) Thesis: The wind-down — the botched "success" of the surge, the withdrawal, the rise of ISIS as the war's direct offspring, and a closing argument that "blowback" isn't karma — it's a feature, the self-perpetuating "algorithm of empire."

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold.

    Cold open: Trump, 2016 [00:00:00]¶

    • A 2016 GOP-debate Trump: the Iraq War was "a big fat mistake… they lied. They said there were weapons of mass destruction. There were none, and they knew there were none." (The episode's frame: the war became something everyone disowns.) [00:00:00]

    The surge's "success" and the human ledger [00:02:47]¶

    • Dec 2008 — 58% of Americans call the invasion a mistake. The surge "worked" only in a paltry, Batman-v-Superman sense: per the Iraq Body Count Project, civilian deaths fell to ~25/day in 2008 (from ~70/day in 2006–07) — but that was the same rate as the occupation's first two years. Iraq remained an occupied, over-policed ruin. [00:02:47]

    Bush's farewell and the shoe [00:05:07]¶

    • Dec 2008 — Bush's farewell Iraq trip; to the troops, "failure" was never an option. Then, at a Baghdad press conference, journalist Muntadhar al-Zaidi hurled both shoes: "This is a goodbye kiss from the Iraqi people, you dog." He got 9 months (and was tortured), and became an Arab-world folk hero. His later essay: "more than a million martyrs… five million orphans… a million widows" — the invasion "divided brother from brother." [00:05:07]
    • Aside: Biden had pushed a plan to partition Iraq into three ethnic segments. [00:09:39]

    Pressure, drawdown, and SOFA [00:11:36]¶

    • After the 2006 midterms, Democrats forced the issue: Bush vetoed a war-funding bill (mid-2007) because it carried a withdrawal timetable. After Petraeus's rosy Sept 2007 report, Bush announced the first real drawdown (20,000 troops). [00:11:36]
    • The SOFA (Status of Forces Agreement), mid-2008 [00:14:07]: set a hard full-withdrawal deadline of Dec 2011. The US felt able to hand over because the surge had bought reduced violence and a fragile-but-workable Shia political coalition — enough for a lame-duck Bush to take the exit. [00:14:07]
    • Foreshadowing ISIS [00:16:37]: beneath the "improvement," 2007 saw the Yazidi atrocities — the stoning of Du'a Khalil Aswad, a bus massacre of Yazidis near Mosul, and Aug 2007 car bombs that killed ~800 (the war's deadliest such attack). [00:16:37]

    The 2008 election [00:18:25]¶

    • The twin issues: Iraq and the economy (the crash hit that fall). GOP primary: Giuliani flopped. Democratic primary: Hillary Clinton (who'd voted for the war) vs. Obama (who'd opposed it). Hillary's brain trust settled on the Kerry-style non-apology — and she refused to actually apologize ("an apology would be a gimmick"); the "3 a.m. phone call" ad. The lesson of 2004 (don't nominate a war voter) cost her the primary. McCain/Palin won the GOP nod — McCain a war critic only for not going far enough (the neocons' and Chalabi's man all along). [00:18:25]

    Obama, withdrawal, no reckoning [00:26:33]¶

    • Jan 2009 — Obama inaugurated; ~150,000 troops still in Iraq. He largely kept the withdrawal timeline (last troops out mid-Dec 2011) — a signature "change" deliverable — while escalating Afghanistan and breaking the Guantanamo promise. [00:26:33]
    • No prosecutions of Bush officials for the war or for torture: Rahm Emanuel — "this is not a time for retribution… it's time for reflection." No one went to the Hague. [00:29:55]
    • Obama instead recalibrated the war on terror — drones, special forces, a quieter empire (the Yemen catastrophe via Saudi Arabia, Libya, Syria) that Americans didn't have to watch. [00:55:03](reflection)

    Maliki and the birth of ISIS [00:32:22]¶

    • Nouri al-Maliki consolidated power (the Baghdad Operations Command bypassing normal hierarchy), ruling corruptly and sectarianly; he crushed the 2011 Arab-Spring protests. Mass deprivation persisted (~20% of Iraqis under $2.20/day, 2007–12), masked by high oil prices. [00:32:22]
    • ISIS [00:35:56]: led by Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, built from former al-Qaeda-in-Iraq fighters pushed into Syria (2013), bulked up in the Syrian civil war, renamed Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant. They took Fallujah and Ramadi (Jan 2014) and then Mosul (summer 2014) — forcing Obama to send troops back. Their weaponry was largely American (captured or given to insurgents/Syrian rebels); their leadership was familiar Sunni faces (a cousin of Saddam, ex-Ba'athists running the "finance ministry"); and their leaders had forged ties in the US prison Camp Bucca. The direct line: Zarqawi's organization → ISIS. [00:35:56]
    • Death toll: generally estimated 600,000+ killed from the invasion onward (not even counting the 1990s sanctions). [00:50:50]

    The present & the oil [00:43:03]¶

    • Jan 2020 — Trump killed Iran's Qasem Soleimani in Iraq, triggering huge anti-US protests (and a parliamentary vote to expel US forces). Iran emerged as the open counterweight to US influence (Soleimani had even been necessary to the SOFA withdrawal deal). The hosts' point: if Soleimani was "a murderer and a terrorist," multiply that for Petraeus and Rumsfeld. [00:43:03]
    • Oil update [00:46:43]: after the surge, ExxonMobil/Shell/Total got back in — but only via profit-share contracts; Iraqi oil stayed a state-run enterprise, and the Iraqi parliament repeatedly blocked the privatizing oil law (written by consultancy BearingPoint). The century-old goal of fully privatizing Iraq's oil was never achieved. [00:46:43]

    Where are they now [00:49:48]¶

    • Bush ($350M, palling with Ellen) · Cheney ("sign my waterboard") · Rumsfeld (released a Solitaire app, $150M) · Hillary Clinton ($45M) · Condoleezza Rice (boards of Chevron/Charles Schwab; Hoover Institution director) · Colin Powell (Salesforce board, Kleiner Perkins; $44M) · Muqtada al-Sadr (still a power broker) · Ahmed Chalabi — "the heart of the show," died of heart failure at home in Baghdad, Nov 2015; to the end he blamed others (Wolfowitz, Zinni) and never became a US citizen. [00:49:48]

    The closing argument [00:54:51]¶

    • Rumsfeld "won": his vision — a light-footprint, low-visibility imperial model — became the bipartisan norm (Obama's drones/special forces; Trump running "the same playbook"). The US is historically obsolete but not politically obsolete — a rotting empire that still holds the most power, having lost the ability to use it competently (the Iraq War as "the last gasp of imperial hubris"). [00:54:51]
    • "Blowback" is not karma — it's a feature [01:00:55]: not an unwanted boomerang but a feedback loop the empire likes — every problem solved creates a new problem and a new opportunity (help the Ba'ath → Saddam → arm him vs. Iran → bomb him → sanctions → 9/11 pretext → invasion → insurgency → contractors → ISIS → …). "It's not a bug. It's a feature. It's the algorithm of empire." And the architects "never face any consequences" — many remain near the levers of power. Closes on Jeremy Corbyn's pre-war warning that the invasion would "set off a spiral of conflict… that will fuel the wars… of future generations." [01:00:55]
