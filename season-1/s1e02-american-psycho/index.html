    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: 1:01:21 Thesis: The Gulf War never really ended. Through the 1990s the US bombed, sanctioned, and ran covert ops against Iraq — deliberately grinding "a troubled but still viable modern society" down into a failed state, priming it for easy invasion in 2003. Two threads: how Saddam was demonized into the obvious "Big Bad," and how Iraq was brought low enough to conquer.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold.

    Cold open: running out of demons [00:00:05]¶

    • April 1991 — After driving Saddam from Kuwait on the "highway of death," Joint Chiefs chairman Colin Powell "confesses" he's running out of villains: "I'm down to Castro and Kim Il-sung." With the Cold War ending and the USSR collapsing, the US message is: no more alternatives — everyone gets in line and joins the global marketplace. [00:00:05]
    • But the swagger didn't last: Saddam "began dancing in our nightmares," surviving as a loose end and bleeding into pop culture as the archetypal scary dictator. Until the US came back to "finish the job," Iraqi civilians would pay the price. [00:01:46]
    • Framing: you can't understand 2003 without seeing (a) how Saddam was demonized, and (b) how badly the US "brought Iraq low" across the 90s — priming the pump for the later war. [00:04:48]

    Chalabi's fall and CIA rebirth [00:06:00]¶

    • Summer 1989 — Ahmed Chalabi flees Jordan in the middle of the night, claiming "vacation." Reality: his Petra Bank (2nd largest in Jordan) had cooked its books and helped trigger a financial crisis. When Jordan's central bank required private banks to deposit 35% of foreign holdings, Chalabi stalled, then ran to Syria. The Guardian's tally of the fraud: ~$80M bad debts, ~$20M unsupported balances, ~$60M simply missing. Jordan's government had to cover the ~$200M owed to depositors. [00:06:00]
    • Twice-exiled (Iraq, then Jordan), Chalabi resettles in London, spins the scandal to his Western journalist friends ("I was framed by Jordan to hand me to Saddam"), and lines up a new income stream: the American government. [00:07:46]

    Saddam's debt trap → Kuwait [00:08:49]¶

    • After the Iran–Iraq war, Saddam is publicly victorious but privately broke: ~$25B owed to Saudi Arabia, $10B to Kuwait (remember that one), ~$40B to the US/Europe — and the West, having used him against Iran, refused any debt rescheduling. Oil ~$17/barrel put Iraq's revenue near half its 1980 level. "Fake friends." [00:08:49]
    • Kuwait then overproduced oil (driving prices down when Iraq needed them up) and Saddam accused it of slant-drilling into Iraqi oil. Aside on Kuwait: a censorious royal family that dissolved parliament (1986), banned parties, ran slavery/human-trafficking and torture — "a piece of shit government," but oil-rich. To a desperate Saddam it "looked like a big turkey leg." [00:10:32]

    America in 1990: the peace-dividend problem [00:11:55]¶

    • 1990 — GHW Bush (Hunter S. Thompson: "a hyena with a living sheep in its mouth") faces every president's nightmare — the peace dividend: with the Cold War over, pressure to cut a ~$270B/yr military "with no major enemy." His post-Panama (Jan 1990) 80% approval was sliding toward 60%. Domestic politics decides what happens to a country like Iraq. [00:11:55]
    • 1989 (a year before the invasion) — US/UK/French firms were still arming Saddam (rockets, aircraft systems — illegally); the Bush administration supplied helicopter engines, nuke-plant pumps, bacteria, and unrefined sarin precursor. Deals were brokered by Kissinger Associates, whose alumni included Bush's future Secretary of State and NSA — i.e. the men who'd "kick Saddam's ass" were, one year earlier, helping arm him. Bush also funneled money via the criminal BCCI network. [00:13:46]
    • Aburish sums up the record: Bush "had been a Saddam supporter," his diplomats defended Saddam's chemical-weapons use and human-rights abuses, backed his loans, and resisted censure. [00:18:04] (quote at [00:18:04] in transcript)

    The green light and the invasion [00:15:53]¶

    • Heavily indebted, Saddam felt entitled to do to Kuwait what the US had backed him doing to Iran. As Iraqi forces massed on the border (mid-1990), the US showed "no real concern." July 1990 — US ambassador April Glaspie tells Saddam the US has "no position" on the Iraq–Kuwait border dispute (echoed in a State Dept cable, at Baker's direction); a US diplomat tells Congress there's no defense commitment to Kuwait. [00:15:53]
    • Aug 2, 1990 — Iraq invades Kuwait. The administration abruptly "flips out": "This will not stand." [00:16:39]
    • Was Saddam set up? (per William Blum's Killing Hope): in May 1990 Saddam offered to negotiate the border; the US encouraged Kuwait to cold-shoulder him; repeated warnings of the buildup were ignored while Kuwait stayed demobilized; Bechtel's George Shultz warned his people to leave Iraq; a Kuwaiti diplomat's invasion warning was ignored, and his post-war press conference was broken up by force. [00:17:55]

    Building the war: bribes, threats, and a fake witness [00:18:57]¶

    • Desert Shield — the 82nd Airborne deploys to Saudi Arabia to "prevent an invasion of Saudi Arabia" that no one (per Woodward and even Judy Miller) actually believed was coming — but it gave the defense budget something to do and pushed Bush's approval to ~74%. [00:18:57]
    • UN Resolution 661 slaps a full trade embargo on Iraq; these sanctions would outlast the war itself. The "noble coalition" myth: in reality assembled "the old-fashioned way" — Egypt got billions in debt forgiven; Syria/China/Turkey/USSR got aid, loans, or sanctions relief; human-rights complaints went quiet. Yemen voted no and lost all US aid — Baker called it "the most expensive vote he ever cast." [00:20:01]
    • Nayirah testimony (~Oct 1990) — the Kuwaiti government paid PR firm Hill & Knowlton >$10M; their AstroTurf group "Citizens for a Free Kuwait" staged a 15-year-old "Nayirah" tearfully telling Congress that Iraqi soldiers threw babies out of incubators. It was fabricated — and "Nayirah" was secretly the daughter of the Kuwaiti ambassador to the US. [00:22:07]
    • Bush starts calling Saddam "Hitler" on TV. Saddam's Aug/Oct peace feelers (Gulf access + oil fields for sanctions relief; releasing foreign hostages as goodwill) are ignored; the US sets a Jan 15, 1991 withdrawal deadline. Arab diplomats said Saddam was ready to pull out with face-saving guarantees; the US, with half a million troops in place, wasn't interested. [00:25:33]

    Desert Storm [00:27:08]¶

    • Jan 17, 1991 — Air war begins. A CIA officer's framing: "convince Saddam the Holocaust was coming." Part of the policy was simply trying to kill Saddam (strikes on "command and control centers" = wherever he might be); the wider aim was the destruction of Iraq itself. [00:27:08]
    • Atrocities cataloged: civilian areas hit (markets, bus stations, apartments); the baby-food factory dismissed as propaganda — then verified as real; depleted uranium; LA Times: US fired on Iraqis waving surrender flags; the highway of death (cluster-bombing fleeing soldiers and civilians while blasting the William Tell overture), after Saddam had been calling for ceasefire for five days — Powell privately uneasy: "we're killing literally thousands of people"; the Amiriyah shelter bombing (400+ women and children — BBC eyewitness account of fused bodies and "one inch thick … melted human fat"). [00:27:56]
    • Deliberate infrastructure destruction for post-war leverage — a UN brief said Iraq was bombed into "a pre-industrial age"; the Washington Post quoted planners admitting targets were hit "to create post-war leverage," to "amplify the … impact of sanctions." Per the Cockburns' Out of the Ashes: electricity to 4% of pre-war level, water to 5%, sewage/medical/agriculture wrecked → gastroenteritis, typhoid, malnutrition exploded. Human toll: PBS ~10–12k combat air deaths + up to ~10k in the ground war; true totals unknown — and uncared for. [00:31:17]
    • The CNN war — the first war on live TV; CNN (then a genuine 24-hr news powerhouse) gave Americans a simple story: Saddam = bad guy, maybe Hitler — omitting that the US had helped him and helped corner him. Footage was military-fed, gruesomeness obscured, and the war was "scripted for cable," prime-time "like watching your friend play CoD" — imagery the same producers would reuse in 2003. Censorship returned for the first time in decades. [00:33:36]

    The 1990s: betrayal, covert ops, and Chalabi the asset [00:37:39]¶

    • End of Feb 1991 — major combat ends. Saddam briefly loses 14 of 18 provinces to a post-war uprising. Bush urged Iraqis to rise up — then refused to back them; per the Cockburns the US even stopped rebels from seizing abandoned arms, shipping those weapons to the Afghan Mujahideen (future Taliban/al-Qaeda). A northern no-fly zone is set up (April), lasting until 2003. [00:37:39]
    • Chalabi → CIA asset — in London he'd rebranded (a credit-card-fraud-detection business, friendships with John McCain), posed as the Iraqi opposition. After the war the CIA cold-calls him (handler alias "Bill Ryder"); first payment $50,000, then access to a $40M anti-Saddam program — even as he's wanted for fraud and hunted by Interpol (the CIA "took care of" that; convicted in absentia in Jordan, 1992). In 1993 he moves to CIA-protected Kurdistan. [00:39:02]
    • Chalabi's 1995 plan flops — his scheme (Kurdish forces + covertly flipped generals/cities → overwhelm Baghdad) is a humiliating bust; he then spreads rumors blaming CIA officer Bob Baer (the Syriana George Clooney character), getting Baer in trouble. Worse, Chalabi's INC was riddled with Iranian agents — he was effectively a double agent, preferring his Iranian partners. The CIA shifts to Ayad Allawi (whose own 1996 coup also fails). A furious CIA officer to Chalabi: "If I ever see you again, I'll fucking run you over." [00:40:51]
    • Weapons inspections, co-opted — per the Cockburns, the UN monitoring system was used to relay Iraqi military comms to US intelligence, vindicating Saddam's claim the inspectors were spies. And the sanctions goalposts moved: originally tied to leaving Kuwait + WMD, Bush re-defined them to last "until Saddam Hussein is out of there" — killing any incentive to cooperate; Albright continued the policy (bipartisan immiseration). [00:43:47]
    • Hussein Kamel — Saddam's son-in-law defects to Jordan (says the WMD were destroyed after 1991), proves to have little to offer, then is lured back by Saddam and slaughtered: Chemical Ali stands over the body — "this is what happens to those who deal with the midget [King Hussein]" — shoots him, and puts meathooks in the brothers' eyes. [00:45:37]
    • The fake assassination plot (1993) — the US accuses Saddam of plotting to kill ex-president GHW Bush; Clinton launches a missile strike on "compelling evidence." Almost certainly false (debunked by Seymour Hersh; the source was the same Kuwaiti minister behind Nayirah; 2003 found nothing in Iraqi files). What did happen: CIA + Allawi ran bombings inside Iraq 1992–95 that killed civilians — Bob Baer recalled one that "blew up a school bus." [00:47:08]

    The sanctions as the great crime [00:49:11]¶

    • By end of 1996 Saddam had survived every plot — killed Kamel, foiled Allawi's coup and Chalabi's uprising, smashed the Kurds, driven the INC out — while his people starved under US sanctions. The hosts' argument: the 1990s sanctions were as great a crime as either war that bracketed them. A Ba'ath government rationing system (praised by a survey as among the world's most efficient/equitable) is what saved the population from outright famine. [00:49:11]
    • A profiteer class (smugglers, contractors) and a well-paid "UN colonial class" emerged; reduced Iraqi oil output kept ~3M barrels/day off the market, propping up world prices for the Saudis/Kuwaitis. Oil-for-food (proposed 1991, finally accepted 1996) was hamstrung — caps too low, "holds" on water/sanitation contracts a "prime reason for increases in sickness and death," approved items useless without unapproved complementary parts. A coordinator's verdict: charity was a band-aid; "the only real solution was to lift the fucking sanctions." [00:50:36]
    • The death toll — the famous figure: 500,000 Iraqi children. Even if (per an LSE paper) Saddam juiced the data, everyone believed it and the US did nothing. Madeleine Albright on 60 Minutes: asked whether half a million dead children was worth it — "we think the price is worth it." The New England Journal of Medicine (independently) showed a tripling of child mortality after the war; Sarah Zaidi's estimate: excess deaths between 228,000 and 500,000. The core point: this was intentional policy, not a mishap — infrastructure was destroyed precisely to make sanctions bite (WaPo planner: the attacks were "to accelerate the effect of sanctions"). [00:52:50]
    • March 1997 — Albright heckled at a CNN town hall in Ohio; Iraqis interviewed by the AP saw it as a "healthy sign" that the American public was waking up. [00:55:36]

    Setting the 2003 stage [00:59:11]¶

    • 1998 — the Iraq Liberation Act — passed unanimously by the Senate, it made regime change official US policy (no war funding yet) and opened the money spigot to Iraqi dissidents — i.e. Chalabi's INC. Chalabi lobbied so hard it was nicknamed "Ahmed Chalabi's law." The State Dept gave him the PR firm BKSH — formerly Black, Manafort, Stone & Kelly (yes, that Paul Manafort and Roger Stone). [00:59:11]
    • Closer: a late-90s Bill Clinton speech warning of a mad Iraqi dictator with WMD and terrorist ties — "eerily similar" to what his Republican successor would say. [00:59:34]
