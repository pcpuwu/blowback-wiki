    Blowback S3E6 — "National Smile Week"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:03:08 Thesis: MacArthur reverses the war (the Inchon landing, Sept 15) and then drives it past the 38th parallel toward the Chinese border — while the US home front, fed by openly racist war coverage, mobilizes into a Red Scare. Beneath it all: a deeper agenda among MacArthur and US hardliners to widen the war into a nuclear showdown with China and the USSR, and the quiet truth that Soviet and Chinese restraint — not American skill — kept the peace they kept breaking.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Inchon ≈ "Inchin/engine," Uijeongbu, Antung/Andong, Wonsan ≈ "Wansan").

    Cold open: Inchon from the air [00:00:01]¶

    • September 1950 — British correspondent Reginald Thompson flies over Inchon: gray warships, naval guns puffing "like indolent fingers," and "the smoking ruin of all that was left of the port." After being humiliated for months, MacArthur had struck back. [00:00:01]
    • Episode roadmap: MacArthur engineers the reversal; captured Gen. William Dean watches from the other side; the war is covered at home through the lens of the Red Scare; and a deeper elite agenda emerges — MacArthur and others wanting to broaden the war into a nuclear standoff with China and the USSR. [00:01:11]

    The home front makes the Cold War real [00:02:56]¶

    • Masuda Hajimu: ordinary Americans co-created Cold War reality — NY/Boston longshoremen refusing to unload "Soviet red crab meat," San Franciscans debating whether post-office murals were communist propaganda. Most letters to Truman never mentioned Korea at all — they were about Stalin and the Kremlin, assuming Soviet forces had invaded. Nearly two-thirds of Gallup respondents thought the US was already in WWIII and expected Soviet A-bombs on US cities; 70% backed higher taxes for a bigger military. Rep. Jacob Javits: "the American people are way ahead of their leaders." [00:02:56]

    Racist war coverage [00:05:14]¶

    • ~300 journalists flooded in, mostly embedded with US troops. Keyes Beach (Chicago Daily News): "I have a feeling that I have just witnessed the beginning of the Third World War." NYT military editor Hanson Baldwin cast North Koreans as the "hordes of Genghis Khan" who'd "taken a leaf from the Nazi book of Blitzkrieg," and elsewhere as "locusts." Papers ran the slur "gooks" in headlines (traceable to the US occupation of the Philippines, carried through WWII, and later into Vietnam). [00:05:14]
    • Nuremberg chief counsel Telford Taylor: "individual lives are not valued so highly in Eastern mores." MacArthur's intelligence chief: "half men with blank faces"; a battalion commander: "trained monkeys." The communist Liberation Daily: "the Americans do not recognize Koreans as human beings." [00:07:11]
    • The surprise [00:08:19]: Cumings notes the NYT found a "queer… ring of passion" in NK statements, "as if they really believed what they were saying about American imperialism." After five weeks of retreat, AP's Hal Boyle reported GIs now rated "the gooks" first-rate fighters; one soldier couldn't believe he was even in Korea. Cumings: "it did not dawn on most Americans that anti-colonial fighters might have something to fight about." Baldwin then flipped — urging the US to copy the Nazis' anti-partisan tactics to "win friends and influence people" among "these simple, primitive, and barbaric peoples." [00:08:19]
    • Censorship from the press [00:09:57]: editors demanded Europe pull "its fair share" of "this UN show"; the Washington Post editorialized for MacArthur to impose censorship (fearing front-line images would spoil the war's appeal). MacArthur brushed it off — planning for a moment he'd want the media around. Dissent was thin (a "Peace in Korea" rally broken up in NY's Union Square). [00:09:57]
    • The honest correspondents [00:12:05]: Reginald Thompson (Daily Telegraph) — Marines "never spoke of the enemy as though they were people, but as one might speak of apes." Alan Winnington (Daily Worker) — accurate on US/ROK war crimes, vindicated decades later. Wilfred Burchett (Australian radical) — branded a spy, banned from his own country 20 years; wrote that "American imperialist prestige can never live down… the humiliating military and political defeat" of the war's first two months. [00:12:05]

    Gen. Dean, prisoner [00:13:53]¶

    • Captured Gen. William Dean is taken to a commandant who interrogates him ("Where is Syngman Rhee?" — no idea; "Would you continue to fight us?" — "yes… I can do better next time and kill more of you"). The kicker: the commandant had been a political prisoner in that very jail when Dean inspected it as military governor — "I'm going to treat you better than you treated me." Dean had no comeback. [00:13:53]

    The Inchon gamble [00:15:29]¶

    • By August the Allies had a huge material edge (5× the tanks, air control, ~140–150k men, ~60% American) but were stuck in a guerrilla war of attrition at the Busan perimeter. In mid-August MacArthur conceived an amphibious landing at Inchon to put a force behind NK lines. The Joint Chiefs were skeptical (Bradley had testified the A-bomb might have ended amphibious invasions forever); others wanted to wait till October. But the great tides forced a mid-September date — weeks away. Halberstam: "the enemy would have to be completely asleep for it to work." So obvious it was nicknamed "Operation Common Knowledge" — yet that very obviousness was MacArthur's cover. [00:15:29]
    • The landing, Sept 15 [00:18:24]: the KPA hadn't mined the harbor; Inchon was piers, not beaches. 230 ships, ~40,000 troops landed against Kim's mere ~2,000 defenders — "a gem of military precision" (the bombardment stopped 15 seconds before the Marines hit). A GI: "this looks like the beginning of the end." [00:18:24]
    • The pincer [00:20:26]: the next day the UN command broke out from Busan, trapping the KPA — in 96 hours, ~50,000 Inmin'gun were caught between the jaws. The campaign "went not merely as MacArthur had planned, but as he had dreamed." But the "Road to Seoul" was real combat; MacArthur's pet general Ned Almond drove the Marines so hard his orders were disregarded — Halberstam: the pressure reflected "an obsession with public relations… the constant need from MacArthur's headquarters for glory." [00:20:26]
    • Stone's verdict [00:22:03]: a psychological turning point — "had Russia wanted war, this was the time." Soviet air/sea power could have pushed the Busan defenders into the sea; the North could've done it alone but for being starved of supplies. Yet MacArthur reported to the UN the opposite — massive USSR/China munitions and manpower, on scant evidence. Stone: an impartial UN body "would have reported that there was no evidence of Russian or Chinese military intervention." [00:22:03]

    China watches [00:22:56]¶

    • At first Korea wasn't China's top concern (Mao slammed the US over Taiwan/Formosa — the naval blockade an "act of aggression"). But by July Mao recognized the threat; in August the long-awaited plan to take Taiwan was scrapped ("in a twinkling, Mao's dream… was shattered"). Mao moved anti-aircraft units across the Yalu to guard bridges, and Zhou Enlai shifted the Fourth Field Army to the border (resolution passed the same day MacArthur got the UN keys). Mao warned the North: don't push the Americans into a corner — "you are fighting with the boss of imperialism… examine seriously the possibilities of retreat." The KPA had over-advanced, leaving its rear vulnerable — exactly what Inchon exploited. Neither Kim nor Stalin took the warnings seriously. [00:22:56]
    • Border raids near the USSR [00:25:25]: Aug 12, a 500-ton US raid hit the NK port of Rashin — ~17 miles from Siberia, ~110 from Vladivostok — followed by strafing along the Korea–Manchuria border. The US Air Force first denied, then admitted an F-51 "might have strafed" Andong on Aug 27 — later conceded as a "mistake." [00:25:25]

    Rollback [00:26:31]¶

    • Inchon stimulated American "thrust northward" thinking — especially Dulles (who'd floated going past the parallel as early as June) and Dean Rusk. By early October the NYT reported major fighting below the parallel had ceased. MacArthur's clashes with Truman were sharpening (over army size, his support for Chiang and Rhee, the rollback against State Department wishes). [00:26:31]
    • The green light + NSC-68 [00:28:18]: Sept 29, over lunch and a map at Blair House, Truman, Acheson, and Marshall gave MacArthur the go-ahead to cross the parallel ("we want you to feel unhampered tactically and strategically to proceed north"). That afternoon they approved NSC-68 — "the American blueprint for waging the Cold War," the charter for the 1950s military buildup. Crucially, Truman had rejected it months earlier: Hajimu — "it would have gone nowhere had it not been for the Korean War." [00:28:18]
    • The fall of Seoul [00:29:51]: Thompson describes a "hideous inferno"; the flag raised on the Capitol at 3 PM. But it wasn't the triumph claimed — the AP reported liberation on Sept 25, though hard fighting ran to Sept 28. US officers: the destruction was driven by "international politics" — the city could've been spared, but "a triumphal entry… was needed as soon as possible." One commander said the attack "accomplished absolutely nothing of military value." Thompson: "few people have suffered so terrible a liberation." [00:29:51]

    "Neither war nor peace" [00:31:10]¶

    • Stone (with a soft spot for Truman, "a good human being… exasperatingly inadequate"): Truman "did not want war, but… did not want peace, and in a sense could not afford peace." MacArthur, by contrast, had no doubt: cross the parallel, kick communism back over the Yalu into China and the USSR. [00:31:10]
    • MacArthur & Rhee [00:34:11]: Thompson watched MacArthur lead Rhee "by the upper arm as a headmaster might lead a pupil," read a "purple speech" with tears, and intone the Lord's Prayer — a man who "nursed his dreams of the conquest of Asia… Genghis Khan in reverse." As Rhee spoke of restored hopes, "the prisons had filled… hundreds faced the firing squads." [00:34:11]
    • The peace that wasn't wanted [00:36:50]: Stone — Chinese/Soviet self-restraint again made the American victory possible (neither retaliated for cross-border strikes). "Anxiety increased in Washington with the possibility of peace." Sept 21: a $11B Air Force budget pitch (48→70 groups); Marshall sworn in as Defense Secretary, broaching unpopular universal military training; Bradley warned the West might "let down its guard after victory." State pushed Japanese non-disarmament and West German rearmament without consulting allies — and these were the moderates. Meanwhile Soviet deputy FM Jacob Malik publicly said "yes" to US–USSR peace talks (Stone: a diplomat arranging to be asked if his government wants peace "must be anxious for peace talks"). [00:36:50]
    • The UN vote and the rigged logic [00:39:24]: Sept 28, the UN approved crossing the parallel but also cleared a path to elections that "would eventually favor the communists" — "a unified Korea would go communist within four or five years." An unacceptable outcome for an administration under fire at home for being soft on communism. Stone: "Truman wanted something which was neither war nor peace… Indecision made Truman at best an irresolute superior, at worst a passive collaborator in MacArthurism." Plus the Japan motive: the longer the war and the more devastation to North Korea, "the larger the market would be for a resurgent, US-friendly Japan." [00:39:24]
    • The planned withdrawal [00:41:55]: KPA documents show Kim was making an "effective withdrawal"; a captured officer called the retreat to the Yalu a deliberate trap to draw UN troops thin. But the US knew a drive past the parallel meant facing Chinese troops too (Baldwin's September dispatches flagged China's sensitivity about the Yalu dams). [00:41:55]

    Military Keynesianism [00:43:14]¶

    • A Biden White House blog (July 2021) on Korea-era inflation sits, fittingly, beside the story: Congress's Defense Production Act (Sept 1950) was "the printer that produced MacArthur's blank check." Michael Brenes: Korea offered "growth liberalism, or military Keynesianism" — Truman used national defense for full employment, repurposing Fair Deal language to grow the economy "without having to redistribute the pie." [00:43:14]

    Stalin's cold calculation [00:45:06]¶

    • Oct 1, past midnight, Stalin got a panicky cable: Kim and Pak Hon-yong begged for direct Soviet military aid (or help organizing "international volunteer units" from China). Stalin dictated a telegram telling Mao to "move immediately at least five or six divisions to the 38th parallel," reasoning the US wasn't ready for a big war and would have to "give in." He was wrong — but realpolitik either way. Zubok & Pleshakov: Stalin "was willing to swallow a serious regional defeat and even the loss of a socialist regime… rather than risk a military clash." Stalin to Khrushchev: "So what? If Kim Il-sung fails, we are not going to intervene… Let the Americans be our neighbors in the Far East." US officials correctly suspected the USSR would stay out. [00:45:06]

    The "imaginary line" [00:47:42]¶

    • Truman had said crossing the parallel was the UN's call (Sept 21), then said he couldn't say publicly whether MacArthur would cross — Stone: the word "publicly" gave it away; Truman had already authorized it. The US had called the parallel an international boundary (its whole justification for the war); now its UN ambassador called it an "imaginary line." Sacrosanct when North Koreans crossed it, meaningless when Americans did. [00:47:42]
    • The unconditional-surrender proclamation [00:48:49]: Oct 1, MacArthur demanded the North lay down arms and hand the whole peninsula to Rhee — guaranteeing nothing good for surrendering NK soldiers, i.e., the best possible reason to keep fighting. Stone listed the peace options foreclosed (negotiate, countrywide elections with observers, a UN trusteeship). But 15 minutes before the proclamation, South Korean troops crossed the parallel under MacArthur and the US 8th Army, with US air/naval cover — UN approval rammed through after the fact. The NYT (Oct 4) called the ROK advance "almost a sightseeing tour" against no opposition: not military necessity. Oct 7, the UN voted to "neither approve nor disapprove" — the US deciding for itself it was justified (a parallel to the Iraq War's reading of old resolutions). [00:48:49]
    • Near-WWIII over Vladivostok [00:51:43]: Oct 8, two US F-80s strafed a Soviet airfield ~60 miles over the border near Vladivostok. The USSR protested; State refused to acknowledge it (should be addressed to "the United Nations"); MacArthur's HQ denied it happened — his army "both America and not America." The Soviets swallowed the affront; FM Vyshinsky offered to meet the US halfway. Stone: had fighting spread, "the United Nations would have found itself drawn toward war with the USSR through… a unified command which operated in its name but was not under its control." [00:51:43]

    The drive north [00:53:22]¶

    • ROK units rolled up the east coast, taking Wonsan on Oct 10; a South Korean major couldn't understand why the North kept "giving up beautiful natural defenses" — because they weren't fighting. [00:53:22]
    • The Wake Island meeting [00:54:18]: Oct 10 Truman announced he'd fly 6,000 miles to meet MacArthur — their first and only meeting. Oct 15, Wake Island, 90 minutes (MacArthur didn't salute his commander-in-chief; no newsmen). Four days later MacArthur conceded the Vladivostok attack as "navigation error and poor judgment." Truman later claimed MacArthur had assured him China would not enter the war. [00:54:18]
    • The Pyongyang occupation [00:56:13]: Thompson on the advance — "every enemy shot released a deluge of destruction. Every village… was blotted out." Cumings: "the Pyongyang occupation was a disgrace" (no utilities, no order, food shortages). The British noted official policy to "hunt and destroy communists and collaborators" was becoming "an international scandal of a major kind"; Dean Rusk admitted "many cases of atrocities." The US knew, reprimanded and sometimes replaced ROK units — but didn't stop the advance. By November the South claimed ~56,000 "vicious red-hot collaborators" rounded up (Cumings: probably an undercount). [00:56:13]
    • Dean again [00:59:19]: another commandant asks Dean why Americans "bomb innocent women and children… bomb children while they're swimming." Dean: "Americans never knowingly harm women, children, or non-combatants." [00:59:19]

    MacArthur arranges the trap [01:00:00]¶

    • Thompson: MacArthur wanted "to establish himself as the Napoleon of the East," "a blind, ridiculous, but immensely powerful Samson, capable of pulling down the world." Oct 24 the US announced a 40-mile buffer zone from the Chinese border — and MacArthur publicly defied it, ordering his troops simply to "clear Korea, period." His HQ knew Chinese troops had already entered and clashed with Americans but stayed silent. Stone: "if MacArthur had been a New Dealer instead of a right-wing darling, he would have been suspected of covering up for the Reds." [01:00:00]
    • The deliberate widening [01:01:41]: Oct 31, HQ finally conceded the Chinese were fighting — Stone's reading: MacArthur downplayed them to prolong the clashes and make a wider war more likely. US troops pushed to the Yalu and hit the Changjin (Chosin) Reservoir, exactly where Chinese forces were concentrated. Nov 6, MacArthur called China's entry "one of the most offensive acts of international lawlessness of historic record," accusing them of a "privileged sanctuary." Stone's verdict applies to the whole intervention: "This was not a matter of the US falling into a trap, but of the US arranging one." The way to avoid the trap was easy — don't send troops where the Chinese were. "But in the troops went all the same." The AP foresaw a "greatly expanded version of the first days of the war" — a slow fighting retreat back down the peninsula. This is precisely what would happen. [01:01:41]
