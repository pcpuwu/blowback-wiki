    Blowback S3E10 — "The Host"¶

    📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:36:05 Thesis: A "speed run" of Korea since 1953 — the armistice that never became peace; the US nuclearizing the South; Rhee's fall and the cycle of military juntas (Park, Chun) building a capitalist-but-dictatorial South; the North's postwar "miracle," then its post-Soviet famine and isolation; and the two nuclear crises in which every US administration from H.W. Bush to Biden sabotaged or bypassed the recurring chances for peace. The unfinished war as "a nail stuck in the flow of history."

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. The season's wide-ranging finale; speech-to-text slips corrected (Yongbyon ≈ "Nyangbon/Nyeongban," Gwangju ≈ "Guangzhou/Kwangju," Chun Doo-hwan ≈ "Chundu Wan," chaebol ≈ "chibals").

    Cold open [00:00:00]¶

    • A US official on North Korea: "We're going to make sure we can control them… We're not going to legitimize you… stronger and stronger sanctions." Spliced with The Host line: "He who can destroy a thing controls a thing." [00:00:00]
    • Roadmap: the armistice that never became peace; the falls of Rhee and McCarthy; military juntas and the Kim line extended; the North's rebuild; the South's rise to capitalist powerhouse-under-dictatorship; spy missions, assassination plots, mutinies, near-war; nukes (not from whom you'd expect); and the long arc of sabotaged reconciliation. [00:00:47]

    The armistice that wasn't peace [00:02:17]¶

    • July 27, 1953 — a purely military document, no nation a signatory. The North took it as a way to stop the bloodshed; Rhee, the South's right, and much US brass saw it as a disaster. June 9 a 500,000-strong Seoul crowd demonstrated against it; June 18 Rhee released ~25,000 anti-communist NK POWs to "jam up the armistice process." Two weeks before signing, the US floated using A-bombs if the truce failed. Three lasting components: the Neutral Nations Supervisory Committee; the DMZ (with its Joint Security Area; ~2.5 mi wide, ~160 mi long); and Article 2 ¶13(d) barring new weapons on the peninsula — which the US would soon violate. [00:02:17]

    The US nuclearizes the South [00:06:21]¶

    • Eisenhower/Dulles's "New Look" leaned on "massive retaliation" — and Dulles judged it was Rhee, not the North, most likely to restart the war (Nixon visited Nov 1953 to extract a no-restart promise). Dec 1956 Eisenhower agreed to cut US divisions but modernize them with atomic weapons (some missiles designed by ex-Nazi Werner von Braun). Stephen Lee: with Chinese forces withdrawing and the North only building defensively, this was escalatory — and preceded even the Jupiter missiles in Europe. "Neither confirm nor deny." [00:06:21]

    McCarthy dies, McCarthyism lives [00:08:56]¶

    • McCarthy died of liver failure (1957), censured, disgraced — but Nixon, Dulles, and HUAC carried McCarthyism forward; the Rosenbergs were dead. [00:08:56]

    Two Koreas, two class systems [00:09:48]¶

    • The North swept away its landlords by revolution; the South by Rhee's tepid land reform — in whose place rose new entrepreneurs (e.g. Chung Ju-yung, who trucked supplies to US bases → Hyundai). The wealth didn't trickle down: Cumings on orphan bands, beggars, a boy cut by razor blades scavenging the dump for meat — against politicians chauffeured in war-surplus jeeps with white linens. [00:09:48]
    • Rhee's fall [00:12:23]: Rhee was a genuine nationalist (wanting industrialization like the North's) who "sucked billions out of the American teat" while running a police state (rigged trials, youth-gang terror, the National Security Law jailing hundreds of thousands). In 1960, after ballot-stuffing and the discovery of a tortured schoolboy's body, a student-led revolt erupted; police killed hundreds; the Americans told Rhee to resign. April 29, 1960 — Rhee flew to Hawaii. [00:12:23]
    • The flash of hope [00:16:18]: a brief opening — free press, diverse Assembly, calls for reunification (Kim Il-sung floated a confederal proposal Aug 1960; students planned to meet Northern counterparts at Panmunjom). "And this, simply, could not be allowed to happen." [00:16:18]

    The juntas [00:17:54]¶

    • May 16, 1961 — Gen. Park Chung-hee's coup snuffed out the democracy; the US held back. Park (a peasant once flagged as a leftist) built an anti-communist developmental state — arresting thousands, labeling all socialist states "enemy states." Military + business ran the show; the KCIA grew (and gave Sun Myung Moon his start with the Unification Church). The South sent ~320,000 troops to Vietnam (most of any country but the US). 1965–71 saw rapid growth and "relative freedom." [00:17:54]

    1968 — the year of raids [00:21:50]¶

    • Jan 21, 1968 — a 31-man NK commando raid on the Blue House to assassinate Park; nearly all died. Lee: this capped the North's aggressive 1960s strategy, paralleling US/ROK aggression in Vietnam (the Tet Offensive that same January). Jan 23 — North Korea seized the spy ship USS Pueblo, taken to Wonsan. The South fumed at US inaction (its PM asked what the US would do "if Cuba raided the White House"); Park drank heavily, urged blowing up NK training sites. The US ran Operation Combat Fox (300 F-4s, three nuclear carriers, nuclear B-52s) and reportedly threatened atomic weapons — then, after Soviet back-channel talks, apologized, got the sailors back (Dec 1968), and later retracted the apology. [00:21:50]
    • Unit 684 [00:26:03]: the South's own squad trained to assassinate Kim Il-sung (men died in training, were executed for desertion/rape). Told in Aug 1971 the mission was off, Unit 684 mutinied, killed 18 handlers, hijacked a bus to Seoul, and died in a standoff (some blowing themselves up with grenades; survivors executed). Covered up for decades. [00:26:03]

    The North's postwar miracle [00:28:03]¶

    • Despite being leveled, the DPRK "roared back" — sending food aid to the South in those days. Cambridge economist Joan Robinson (1965) called the recovery "a miracle" — Pyongyang rebuilt as "a city without slums," with free education/healthcare. By the 1970s: imported Western factories, Siemens medical equipment, Mercedes/Volvos, trade diversified toward non-communist countries. A 1975 Swedish envoy noted living standards "higher and more equitably distributed than in the South" — while finding the Kim personality cult "a bit much." [00:28:03]

    Détente and the axe murders [00:31:32]¶

    • Nixon's détente/China rapprochement cooled Asia; North and South pledged toward reconciliation. But August 1976 — a US/ROK crew cut down a tree in the JSA without permission; on Aug 18 they returned and didn't stop. Two American officers were killed with the axes. Lee: "a calculated provocation, repeated twice." DEFCON 3, nuclear bombers dispatched for Operation Paul Bunyan; a US analyst said it "blew their fucking minds." [00:31:32]

    The KCIA payoffs & crueler rule [00:33:43]¶

    • 1970s congressional scrutiny exposed the KCIA bribing ~90 members of Congress (and funding pro-Nixon rallies via Moon). At home a "rogue institution": Park rewrote the constitution to rule indefinitely (Emergency Decrees, Order No. 9 making any criticism a security crime); the KCIA "watch[ed] everyone everywhere," torture at Namsan. [00:33:43]
    • The economic machine [00:36:24]: the junta marched corrupt businessmen through the streets in dunce caps, then built a developmental paradise — negative-interest loans to chaebol in steel/electronics. "In South Korea, it really, really worked," sold as a "national family." [00:36:24]
    • Labor's other story [00:38:03]: unions were state-controlled (KCIA-managed). Sweatshops (a 1970 Quaker probe of the Peace Market: girls 14–16 working ~15-hour days kneeling, two days off per month, amphetamines to stay awake, afflicted with TB and anemia). Labor had to fight its way into politics year after year. [00:38:03]
    • Park's death [00:40:15]: Oct 26, 1979 — at a KCIA safehouse dinner, the intelligence chief shot Park ("how can we conduct our policies with an insect like this?"); unclear if murder or failed coup; the chief was executed. [00:40:15]

    Democratization and the Gwangju massacre [00:41:19]¶

    • The 1980s would (bloodily) end the cycle of military rule. Tim Shorrock: Chun Doo-hwan's three-stage coup (military → KCIA → government); the day after, Gwangju demonstrators were "stomped and slaughtered" by special forces; on the third day the army opened fire on tens of thousands — the Gwangju massacre. Shorrock's documents showed the US gave a green light to use force and released troops under Joint Command; a Carter-era White House meeting (Mondale, Brzezinski, Holbrooke) backed Chun's takeover knowing of the massacre. The story sparked protests at the US Embassy and turned Korean leftists against the US ("US interests… always predominate"). The June 1987 demonstrations forced free elections (Roh Tae-woo); the ROK opened ties with the PRC — though the KCIA merely rebranded (ANSP). [00:41:19]

    The North's golden age [00:46:24]¶

    • 1980s North Korea: a "more relaxed scene," "paradise stores" of imported goods, a golden age of cinema (Kim Jong-il's patronage; joint Italian/German productions — filmmaker Anna Barnowski). Cumings (visiting): "no squalor, no begging… extraordinary public civility"; Andrew Holloway: a "secure and happy existence"; one journalist likened it to "one big kibbutz." [00:46:24]

    The South's middle class & the 1997 crash [00:48:29]¶

    • 1990s democratization grew a genuine middle class (Jesook Song). Unlike the ex-Soviet bloc, East Asia boomed — until the 1997 financial crash: too much "hot money," bad chaebol loans, mass layoffs (1.5 million jobs lost in 1998; poverty up to 12%, ~15% today after IMF policy). [00:48:29]
    • The longest political prisoner [00:49:48]: Kim Sun-myung, jailed from Oct 1950, tortured (his father and sister executed to break him), 44 years' solitary — released in the 1990s, unrepentant, at 73. The National Security Act still jails people "as of this recording in 2022." [00:49:48]

    The North enters the wilderness [00:51:52]¶

    • Kim Il-sung died 1994; succeeded eventually by Kim Jong-il. The 1991 Soviet collapse wrecked the North's trade, compounded by new US sanctions, then mid-1990s floods and drought → a famine killing as many as half a million (Meredith Jung-en Woo). US "aid" was mostly heating-oil compensation (never >2% of energy needs) and food (slashed under Bush); China gave far more. Jimmy Carter: "the North Koreans have suffered because the United States has done everything we possibly could to destroy [their] economy… then we condemn them because their people are starving." [00:51:52]
    • The prison contrast [00:55:09]: the North moved resources to the military; ~100,000 imprisoned (half political). Cumings cites The Aquariums of Pyongyang (Kang Chol-hwan, 10 years at Yodok with his family — survivable, not the "ghastly… totalitarian repression" its French publishers wanted) — juxtaposed against the US "never-ending gulag" incarcerating ~25% of Black youth. [00:55:09]

    The first nuclear crisis [00:58:03]¶

    • The justification for all the hostility: the nuclear program. After the USSR fell, the US needed new villains (Colin Powell: "down to Castro and Kim Il-sung"); Leslie Gelb's 1991 NYT op-ed "The Next Renegade State." The US had put nukes in the South in 1958, ~700 by the 1970s (helicopters flying them near the DMZ); Park secretly pursued his own bomb; the South was a "renegade arms supplier." The North's Yongbyon reactor (1980s) was for energy — its fuel-load pattern inconsistent with a weapons program, publicly visible (if they wanted a secret bomb they'd go underground like Israel), and they invited the IAEA (which declined on a technicality). [00:58:03]
    • The squandered opening [01:03:31]: in a post-Soviet world Kim Il-sung planned to normalize with the US/South and marketize. Late 1991 H.W. Bush withdrew the nukes from the South and suspended Team Spirit; the North hosted six 1992 inspections, even showing undisclosed facilities. But Bush went hardline (inspections being a CIA-surveillance backdoor — the North's real objection, and accurate). Jan 1993 Clinton resumed Team Spirit (simulating nuclear strikes); STRATCOM retargeted ex-Soviet nukes on the North; Woolsey called the DPRK the greatest threat. The North announced NPT withdrawal (shrewdly timed) but paused it, holding out for a deal. [01:03:31]
    • Carter saves it [01:06:57]: a 16-month war scare (sanctions threats, the doctored "sea of fire" quote). Jimmy Carter flew to Pyongyang, met Kim Il-sung, and brokered the framework: freeze Yongbyon for light-water reactors — the basis of the 1994 Framework Agreement. [01:06:57]

    The Sunshine Policy [01:08:25]¶

    • 1998 — opposition figure Kim Dae-jung (whom the KCIA once tried to run over with a truck) became president and launched the Sunshine Policy: food aid, lifted business limits, calling for an end to the US embargo. The North hadn't tested a missile in six years. Yet US press kept alleging secret bomb-building (a site the North then opened — nothing found); a 1998 commemorative satellite launch (failed to orbit) became the Hawks' obsession (Rumsfeld's missile-defense task force). Threats continued: Powell's "charcoal briquette"; a Marine general's not-for-attribution rollback plan ("We'll kill them all"). Still, a thaw: the June 2000 Kim–Kim summit, then Albright's Pyongyang visit; Kim Jong-il even accepted continued US troop presence. Clinton's bags were packed for a Pyongyang summit. [01:08:25]

    Bush blows it up [01:12:43]¶

    • November 2000 — Bush wins; the summit is struck from Clinton's calendar. Bush's team junked the Framework Agreement; Bush lectured Kim Dae-jung ("Who is this guy? I can't believe how naive he is"), then named the DPRK part of the "Axis of Evil" (Jan 2002). An intel official (via Hersh): "Bush and Cheney want that guy's head on a platter… they're going to get this guy after Iraq." The second nuclear crisis: Bush accused the North of a uranium program (not a Framework violation, which covered plutonium); "without proof… decided the DPRK had a secret program" (Matray); Bush at his ranch: "I loathe Kim Jong-il." The six-party talks (2003) demanded the North surrender its one deterrent and trust "a government dedicated to its destruction"; Bush even misrepresented intel on a supposed NK–Libya transfer (as with Iraq's WMDs). By 2008 the North resumed testing, certain the US would seek regime change regardless (Bolton slapping The End of North Korea on a table: "That is our policy"). [01:12:43]

    Obama: "strategic patience" [01:19:53]¶

    • Obama bypassed diplomacy — "strategic patience" — predicting collapse (the internet would "inevitably penetrate"), not even issuing a statement on Kim Jong-il's 2011 death. Covertly: cyber/electronic strikes on the North's missile launches (rockets exploding mid-air, per the NYT), and new missile defense in the South. The only talk proposals came from Kim Jong-un — rejected (the North even offered to suspend tests if the US/ROK canceled joint exercises; rejected). Obama: "We could obviously destroy North Korea with our arsenals." [01:19:53]

    Moon, Trump, and after [01:22:50]¶

    • 2017 — South Korea's Moon Jae-in revived the Sunshine Policy (joint Olympic march, K-pop in Pyongyang); April 2018 the Kim–Moon JSA summit and Panmunjom Declaration; Moon spoke to ~150,000 in a Pyongyang stadium. Trump — after "Rocket Man" and "totally destroy North Korea" — produced the biggest breakthroughs in decades (Singapore, then Hanoi), savaged by both parties (Biden: "coddling dictators"; Warren: "exchanging love letters"). But hardliners ran his foreign policy (Pompeo, Bolton — who reportedly wanted the talks to fail). At Hanoi (2019) Trump rejected the North's offer to dismantle Yongbyon for sanctions relief and walked out; the North resumed testing; talks never recovered. By March 2020 the North said it had "lost all appetite for dialogue" over Pompeo's pressure. Biden (as of 2022): a review producing a path "somewhere in between" Obama and Trump — "something for something." [01:22:50]
    • Christine Ahn [01:23:23]: K-pop "invisibleizes" the undercurrent — North Korea's "struggle for life" under cut-off trade (no pandemic medical capacity), against the world's largest US base at Camp Humphreys (golf courses, water parks, $3B+/year) — "the legacy of the unresolved war." [01:23:23]
    • Baek Ki-wan [01:33:30]: the activist (who fought to oust Rhee, was tortured under Park, opposed the Iraq deployment, pushed for reunification) died 2021. He'd asked any funeral money go to the poor, and once wrote: "When the unified life of Korea is cut in two, our country will be like a nail stuck in the flow of history." [01:33:30]
    • Sign-off [01:35:12]: "I guess we learned not to do it again." Thanks to the season's guests (Suzy Kim, Bruce Cumings, Tim Shorrock, Monica Kim, Christine Ahn, Anna Barnowski, Michael Brenes, Elizabeth Beavers, Jeffrey Kaye, Thomas Powell). [01:35:12]
