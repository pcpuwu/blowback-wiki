    Blowback S3E3 — "The Blue House"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:06:39 Thesis: The two occupations (1945–48) — how the US military government in the South suppressed Korea's own people's committees and rebuilt the Japanese-collaborator order, while the North underwent a genuine social revolution (land reform, people's committees) — and how the US, not the USSR, drove the Cold War confrontation that hardened Korea's division.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Drawn from Suzy Kim, Bruce Cumings, and Hwang Sok-yong.

    Cold open: liberation [00:00:00]¶

    • Aug 15, 1945 — Japan surrenders; Koreans pour into the streets; the Soviet Red Army (which liberated Korea) disarms the Japanese police while Koreans spontaneously form self-governing people's committees. But days earlier, two US officials had drawn the line that would define Korea forever. [00:00:00]

    The Soviets wanted partnership [00:05:00]¶

    • Post-WWII the USSR was triumphant but devastated (27M+ dead, a third of US national wealth in losses) and counting on continued alliance with the West, not brinkmanship (Yalta, Potsdam; Litvinov/Maisky's peacetime-diplomacy planning; Molotov, Gromyko all confirm). The US instead moved toward confrontation — rebuilding West Germany and Japan (with former Nazis/fascists). On Korea, the Soviets took the path of least resistance and accepted the US-proposed temporary partition. [00:05:00]

    Truman & the Red Scare [00:12:30]¶

    • FDR's death brought Truman (a machine politician who replaced the progressive Henry Wallace in 1944). His "Fair Deal" withered; Democrats were trounced in the 1946 midterms amid the onset of the Red Scare (Taft-Hartley gutted unions). Plus the "reconversion" fight — the war lobby resisting peacetime spending cuts. [00:12:30]

    The Korean People's Republic vs. the US occupation [00:23:00]¶

    • Sept 1945 — Koreans declared the Korean People's Republic (land redistribution, nationalize Japanese property, punish collaborators) run by people's committees. The US (Gen. Hodge, in the "Blue House") refused to recognize it: "one of our missions was to break down this communist government." [00:23:00]
    • The South under US rule [00:28:00]: the occupation treated the South as enemy territory, kept the Japanese-era structures (≈75% of the police were former Japanese-service Koreans; Kim Sŏk-wŏn, who'd hunted Kim Il-sung, became an ROK general), banned strikes, imposed a "free market" on rice → famine, and jailed leftists. The 1946 strikes built into a 300,000-worker general strike; the Sept 30 railway strike was broken (1 dead, ~1,500 arrested) and the Daegu uprising (Oct 1946) met martial law and rigged elections. Korean gangsters ("street leaders") were deployed to crack heads. (Lt. Richard Robinson's critical report had to be burned and rewritten from memory.) [00:28:00]

    The North's revolution [00:41:00]¶

    • Kim Il-sung was the real deal — not a Soviet puppet or "imposter" (Cumings debunks the decades-long US/South propaganda); the Soviets were barely present at the local level (Suzy Kim), and a Sept 27, 1945 decree abolished the colonial institutions (vs. the US keeping them). [00:41:00]
    • Land reform (Feb–March 1946) [00:44:30]: free redistribution of Japanese/collaborator land, no compensation, abolishing the tenancy system (in which peasants had handed over 50–70% of harvests); production rose as much as 50%. Landlords fled south (forming the anti-communist base). Then the people's committee elections (1946–47, a coalition slate, mass turnout), a literacy campaign, and a women's-rights / everyday-life revolution. [00:44:30]
    • North Korea's structure [00:53:00]: the Korean Workers' Party (Aug 1946), nationalized industry, Soviet-model planning, Christians targeted (Rev. Sun Myung Moon imprisoned → later the "Moonies"), press freedom phased out, a self-criticism/meetings culture — but not a bloodbath (the collaborator minority simply fled south). Cumings: to its beneficiaries a vast improvement, to its victims a draconian denial of freedom — "both were probably right." The Soviet-NK relationship was nuanced, not master/servant (Stalin had deported 200,000 Koreans in 1937; Soviet advisers dropped from ~200 to ~30 by 1947). [00:53:00]

    The US Cold War machinery [01:00:00]¶

    • The South outlawed communism (Aug 1947) amid assassinations of leftists; Syngman Rhee (US-picked, a Wilson student, ex-OSS, ruthless) consolidated power (rival Kim Gu's coup failed; he was later assassinated). In the US: Truman's loyalty program (1947), Kennan's Long Telegram (1946), the Truman Doctrine (March 1947), the National Security Act (1947 — creating the Secretary of Defense and the CIA), the Marshall Plan (to deprive the USSR of German influence) → the Berlin blockade and Germany's division. [01:00:00]
    • The split hardens [01:05:00]: Aug 15, 1948 — the Republic of Korea declared (Rhee president); Sept 9, 1948 — the DPRK declared. The USSR withdrew its troops by the end of 1948; in 1949 North Korea's battle-tested troops (fighting alongside the Chinese communists) began returning home. [01:05:00]
