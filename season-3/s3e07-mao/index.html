    Blowback S3E7 — "Mao" (Mao's Poem)¶

    ← S3E6 — National Smile Week

    S3E8 — American Caesar →

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:04:27 Thesis: China enters the war. Against MacArthur's drive to the Yalu, Mao — over the objections of a divided Politburo and after Stalin's whiplash on Soviet air cover — sends the "Chinese People's Volunteer Army" across the river. Ill-equipped peasant soldiers drive the US out of North Korea in weeks, triggering "the worst crisis in US foreign relations between 1945 and the Cuban Missile Crisis": a national emergency, open talk of the A-bomb, and the rise of McCarthyism.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Peng Dehuai ≈ "Pung de Huai," Zhou Enlai ≈ "Joe N-Lai," Nie Rongzhen ≈ "Nie/Nihong Zhen," Unsan, Sinuiju ≈ "Sinuzi," Chongchon ≈ "Chongchung").

    Cold open: Thanksgiving 1950 [00:00:00]¶

    • US soldiers ate turkey and pumpkin pie shipped from home — not knowing they were surrounded by thousands of Chinese soldiers (the People's Volunteer Army) in the hills, each carrying only a bag of cereal mix, rifles, and grenades, wearing tennis shoes in -30°F. Within weeks they'd drive every American out of North Korea. [00:00:00]
    • The "forgotten war" — not in China [00:01:43]: a 1988 tea party of retired PLA generals began a wave of Korean War memoirs; in China the war is anything but forgotten. Roadmap: China's hotly-debated decision to enter; a stunning military reversal; MacArthur's escalation vs. Truman's panic. Cumings: the winter-1950 defeat "caused the worst crisis in US foreign relations between 1945 and the Cuban Missile Crisis… and essentially demolished the Truman administration." Stone: caution in September or November might have brought peace, but Truman pushed on — until December, when he said "it looks like World War III is here." [00:01:43]

    The warnings ignored [00:04:45]¶

    • Thompson watched MacArthur take Pyongyang amid his "private journalistic team," sensing it was "merely a beginning" (a general told him "on to the Yalu"). China had signaled clearly: a drive to the Yalu was unacceptable. Sept 30, Zhou Enlai warned the world ("the Chinese people can never tolerate any foreign aggression"); lacking US diplomatic relations, he had India's ambassador K.M. Panikkar relay that China would fight if the parallel was crossed. The Americans ignored it — or "positively welcomed the idea." Oct 2, South Korean then American troops had crossed in large numbers. [00:04:45]

    The Politburo debate [00:07:23]¶

    • Marshal Peng Dehuai rushed to a Central Committee meeting already in progress. Lin Biao was notably skeptical; many comrades, exhausted from decades of war, urgently wanted to "recuperate and rebuild" rather than fight "the strongest imperialist nation in the world." Mao: "When we are standing on the side just watching other people undergoing a national crisis, we feel terrible inside." Peng couldn't sleep that night — the beds too soft, then the war on his mind: "The tiger always eats people… It is impossible to make any concessions to a tiger." Mao agonized too (Nie: "he had racked his brain… many times"), but "the Americans had already brought the war to our borders." [00:07:23]
    • Thompson's outside read [00:10:36]: he thought it "absurd to expect the new China… to tolerate the United States or the United Nations on her Manchurian frontier" — not because it was communist, but because "great nations… do not permit these things if they can help it." [00:10:36]
    • "Volunteer forces" [00:11:21]: Peng was made commander of the Chinese Volunteer Army — the "volunteer" label (suggested by a non-communist liberal official) artfully dodged a formal declaration of war: if the US ran a "police action," these were merely volunteers. But the Politburo kept tugging Mao back; a solid majority (incl. Zhou Enlai and Lin Biao) still opposed entering — a "bloody distraction" from consolidating the revolution. On the other side: Mao, Peng, Nie — for whom Korea was another front "from which the United States would try to squeeze the revolution" (Mao's "recurring nightmare" of encirclement; false rumors of US support for a Chiang return, armed uprisings). [00:11:21]
    • The decision [00:13:52]: Mao declared that not fighting would swell the reactionaries' arrogance; North Korea had aided China in Manchuria and just made its own revolution. "When the lips are destroyed, the teeth feel cold." China cabled Kim Il-sung, who clapped and shouted "well done, excellent." [00:13:52]

    Stalin's whiplash [00:14:52]¶

    • As Chinese troops began crossing, a huge reversal: meeting Zhou Enlai at his Black Sea dacha, Stalin scratched the promised Soviet air support — air power the Chinese had factored into their plans. Zhou and Lin Biao haggled, salvaging a deal for at least heavy military aid (only Lin left the banquet sober). Back in Moscow, Molotov told Zhou the deal was off again — no air support, no equipment. Khrushchev: after Inchon, Stalin "became resigned" to North Korea's annihilation — "Let the United States of America be our neighbors in the Far East… We are not ready to fight." [00:14:52]
    • Mao wavers, then commits [00:16:56]: even Mao had second thoughts — "a sleepless 70 hours." But at an emergency Politburo session, "Mao had already made up his mind": the Americans wouldn't stop at the Yalu. Troops were going in. [00:16:56]
    • Stalin relents [00:17:47]: moved by Mao's cable reinstating the commitment, Stalin (reportedly near tears: "The Chinese comrades are so good") granted a bit of air support and discounted hardware — though the jets gave only minimal help and wouldn't fly offensive missions. China's rear would at least be better defended. [00:17:47]

    Crossing the Yalu [00:19:04]¶

    • Mid-October — deputy commander Hong Xuezhi sized up the matchup: the US had modern weapons, mobility, firepower, air/naval dominance; the Chinese had horse-drawn artillery, rifles, grenades — but "rich combat experience" (always beating well-equipped enemies), superiority in close-quarters/night/bayonet fighting, and flexible, outflanking tactics vs. an American army that "operated by books and regulations." [00:19:04]
    • The handicaps [00:20:42]: Bin Yu — preparation was "at best inadequate," hastened by the UN's rapid advance, with barely three months to get combat-ready and little knowledge of the enemy. The rank and file needed convincing: by mid-August only half the 30th Army actively supported intervention, 40% passively, 10% opposed (fearing US firepower and atomic weapons). Two months of training helped — and class hatred motivated many (a soldier's letter: "I am requesting to go to the Korean front to kill American devils"). [00:20:42]

    The First Campaign [00:22:50]¶

    • Oct 18 Peng crossed the Yalu; Oct 21 clashed with Rhee's "puppet troops" — the first campaign a surprise encounter with the South Koreans. On the UN side, GHQ labeled the crossing forces "Chinese Koreans" and insisted China wouldn't intervene; US advisors (Oct 22) claimed only "scattered pockets of resistance." But fresh North Korean troops then plowed through; Oct 26 combined Sino-Korean units "came roaring out of the mountains at Unsan," destroyed the ROK Second Corps, and crippled the US 8th Army's flank. Mao had the volunteers hit South Korean units first to gain experience. [00:22:50]
    • First contact with Americans [00:25:58]: Nov 1, the Chinese 39th Army attacked Unsan thinking it held South Koreans; the Americans, caught off guard, even approached to shake hands before the 39th opened fire at close range — battalions of the US 8th Cavalry fell. US mechanized units depended on roads, air, artillery, and supplies, and feared night/close combat — letting the Chinese cut them into pieces. Thompson: the enemy didn't outnumber the UN, and counting air/naval power "there could be no comparison in fighting strength. But the enemy had grasped the initiative." Chinese weaknesses also showed: inadequate firepower, thin supplies, no motorized infantry to pursue. [00:25:58]

    The Second Phase and the Korean role [00:28:20]¶

    • Crucially, Koreans were still doing much of the fighting — comparable numbers of renewed NK troops alongside the Chinese. Cumings: "the Korean contribution… has been completely missed in the literature"; the US was "outmaneuvered by the Korean People's Army generals." Thompson described central North Korea becoming a "no-man's land" where the enemy who'd struck so skillfully "had disappeared completely" — while the winter froze in and US supply gaps opened after the over-optimism of Pyongyang's fall. [00:28:20]
    • The Sinuiju bombing [00:29:57]: as the UN Security Council prepared to meet Nov 8 — a moment for negotiation — the US Air Force lifted its border-flight ban (it had already been operating there): 70+ B-29s and 300 fighters dropped 630 tons on Sinuiju, destroying "90% of the city." A surprise escalation at a fragile diplomatic moment. MacArthur, who'd earlier downplayed the Chinese, now painted the counterattack as "the armies of Mordor." [00:29:57]
    • Allied wavering [00:32:08]: even Britain wavered — 20+ Labour MPs (joined by Tories, even Churchill) wanted out and favored recognizing/trading with communist China. Useless: Britain wasn't running the show, nor was the UN. Nov 24, a communist Chinese delegation was allowed to join the UN on Korea; China released 100 POWs as a goodwill gesture — the very day MacArthur launched his "Home by Christmas" offensive. [00:32:08]

    "Home by Christmas" [00:33:30]¶

    • Nov 24 (day after Thanksgiving) — MacArthur's "outline for victory" ran in papers nationwide: a pincer to "compress the enemy against the Yalu," which "should for all practical purposes end the war… It is that for which we fight." He flew an unarmed plane along the Yalu, saw nothing, and said it'd take a massive snowstorm to hide troop movements. Manchester: "This is exactly what happened." Two days after Thanksgiving, Chinese and North Korean soldiers rushed down from the very mountains MacArthur said couldn't hold them — chaos (some GIs not even sleeping with boots on). Thompson watched MacArthur, "almost done for by the cold," totter up the gangway after telling a general, "Home for Christmas… don't make me a liar, feller." [00:33:30]
    • Truman: "The Chinese have come in with both feet." Sub-freezing mountains, no winter clothing; a British soldier described enemy in thick padding "like little Michelin men" — turning a body over to find a Red Star cap. [00:33:30]

    The two battles [00:38:09]¶

    • The Battle of the Chongchon River (west, 30+ miles north of Pyongyang): Thompson — "within five seconds of wild bugle calls, the attacks came in," seven of ten men "draped with percussion grenades." The 8th Army's right flank collapsed; jeeps froze solid to the ground. [00:38:09]
    • The Battle of the Chosin Reservoir ("Battle of Lake Changjin" — subject of the 2021 Chinese blockbuster, the highest-grossing film worldwide that year): the US 10th Corps driven back into South Korea. Brief terror of a total collapse/evacuation. [00:38:09]
    • A "phony war"? [00:39:43]: by early December the US press (Time, NYT) turned on MacArthur; he punched back, denying any error. Stone: MacArthur had functionally advertised his pincer in press briefings — "to read the November 26th NYT account… is to see Achilles pointing frantically at his heel" (the dispatch all but invited the Chinese into the wedge between the two UN armies — "a Victorian maiden could not have fluttered her eyes more unmistakably"). Truman ordered MacArthur to make no more public statements. Cumings: observers wondered why Americans retreated so fast, "often breaking contact with an enemy not necessarily pursuing them"; British attachés saw "a great hoax" or "phony war" — the US puffing up Chinese troop numbers the POW count couldn't support. The "disaster" was being turned into deadly political capital at home. [00:39:43]
    • The cost [00:43:05]: the second campaign pushed the line back to the 38th parallel and recaptured Pyongyang in nine days — but exposed Chinese weaknesses: frostbite hit 30,000 men (a quarter of the 9th Army), killing 1,000; UN airpower battered overextended supply lines. [00:43:05]
    • Mao's son [00:44:21]: Nov 25 — Mao Anying, 28, who'd volunteered (making Peng nervous that harm to him would cost the brass their heads), was killed by a US napalm bomb on Peng's command shed, his body identifiable only by a Soviet revolver from Stalin. [00:44:21]

    The A-bomb on the table [00:46:03]¶

    • Truman's force collapsing, the press conference where he said the US was prepared to use the A-bomb in Korea triggered worldwide alarm. The White House walked it back ("only the president can authorize… no such authorization has been given") — but consideration was "always implicit in the very possession of that weapon." On Guam sat atomic-capable B-29s under Curtis "Bombs Away" LeMay, head of Strategic Air Command. British PM Attlee rushed to Washington and extracted a promise of allied notification — which Acheson then got Truman to drop from the memo. Atomic weapons stayed "on the table, but only the province of America." Thompson recalled a GI's reaction ("Hell yes, use it") and the journalists slumping "as though we had been shot." [00:46:03]

    The state of emergency & McCarthyism [00:50:53]¶

    • The catastrophe pushed Truman to declare a state of emergency (Dec 16). The Democrats had lost 28 House and 5 Senate seats in November 1950; Truman's approval began its terminal slide. Though Truman had out-redbaited the redbaiters (the 1947 loyalty pledge, expelling radical New Dealers, smothering the Wallace Progressives), harder anti-communists in the GOP were winning — McCarthy now "boasts of the term McCarthyism." Richard Freeland: "the enormous gap between what the American people had been led to expect in Asia and what actually occurred there probably did more to make McCarthy's charges credible… than anything else, save… Alger Hiss." Food prices rose ~10% over the last eight months of 1950. Dec 15 Truman's "No Appeasement" Oval Office address; Acheson called China's entry "even more immoral" than the North's. [00:50:53]

    Censorship and the massacres [00:55:35]¶

    • Press controls came only now (Dec 1950) — and Su-kyoung Hwang notes the trigger was the news of civilian massacres. An AP reporter witnessed the execution of 20 ROK civilians (incl. an 18-year-old girl and a mother condemned for organizing women to make underwear for the communists) — the army then banned correspondents from executions. The Red Cross knew of ~35–40,000 prisoners' conditions but "looked the other way." As the South retreated, its repression machine surged: hundreds of DPRK prisoners summarily executed and hung "like rag dolls"; ~400 women and children burned alive in a warehouse; the Hongseong/Hwangji (Hongjiri) incident (Dec 15) — a British soldier, William Hilder, described prisoners roped in pairs, including two women and two children aged ~10 and 13, shot in trenches in batches of ten while American soldiers watched "disgusted." Hwang: the Americans documented executions "occurring on a daily basis for several months." [00:55:35]

    MacArthur unravels — and the meaning [00:59:25]¶

    • Thompson on Tokyo HQ: "ever more flamboyant statements emanated from the dictator in his ivory tower… There had been no mistakes." The "Chinese hordes" were swollen to nearly a million "though everywhere out of contact"; the briefings became "filled with the phrases of advertising copywriters." The one cheerful thought — that MacArthur couldn't survive this — proved wrong. [00:59:25]
    • Gen. Dean's interrogators [01:01:14]: asked why the Chinese came in, Dean blamed Stalin's orders; his interpreter retorted that if the Soviets wanted to attack they'd have done so when US troops reached their border — "Our Chinese brothers saw robbers in their neighbor's house. That is why they came to our assistance." [01:01:14]
    • The greatest danger [01:02:18]: Cumings — though the Cuban Missile Crisis gets the title, the defeat of rollback in North Korea "occasioned the greatest danger," bearing on two axes: the global communism-vs-capitalism conflict, and the internal US struggle between Truman/Acheson and the substantial pro-MacArthur "rollback" coalition. China and North Korea "had perfect aim" — reversing the first great attempt to displace a communist state and exploding the rollback coalition at home. As 1950 closed, Washington contemplated "nearly every weapon in the United States arsenal." [01:02:18]
    • The poems [01:03:34]: a 40th Army soldier — "The American imperialism is a ball of fire… China can be saved by helping Korea." Mao's "Two Birds" — "gunfire licks the heavens, shells pit the earth… This is one hell of a mess." [01:03:34]
