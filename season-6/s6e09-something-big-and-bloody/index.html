    Blowback S6E9 — "Something Big and Bloody"¶

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:12:00 Thesis: The climax, 1987–91 — the MPLA's Soviet-pushed Mavinga offensive (Operation Salute to October) fails, drawing South Africa's full intervention (Operation Modular) into a siege of Cuito Cuanavale, "the largest battle in Africa since WWII." Castro defies both Pretoria and a reluctant Gorbachev, surging Cuban troops, winning air supremacy, holding Cuito, and then driving a second front southwest to the Namibian border — forcing Pretoria to the table. The result: the New York Accords (Dec 1988) granting Namibian independence and Cuban withdrawal; Mandela's verdict that Cuito "destroyed the myth of the invincibility of the white oppressor"; Savimbi's PR collapse (Red Scorpion, the Red September/Chigunji revelations); and Cuba's 1991 exit, leaving Angola's civil war to the Bicesse Accords and the question: what will Savimbi do if he loses?

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Cuito Cuanavale ≈ "Quito Kwanavale/Guanavale," Gleijeses ≈ "Glehesas," dos Santos, Geldenhuys ≈ "Geldenheis," Risquet ≈ "Risket," Akhromeyev ≈ "Akromayev," Calueque ≈ "Calike," Chigunji).

    Cold open & roadmap [00:00:24]¶

    • Recap: Reagan's landslide, constructive engagement/linkage, the Savimbi PR machine. By the late 1980s three struggles converged — Namibian independence (SWAPO would win any free vote), apartheid's internal collapse, and the Angolan showdown — and would climax at a tiny village, Cuito Cuanavale, in "the largest battle in Africa since the Second World War." [00:00:24]

    Operation Salute to October [00:04:38]¶

    • Summer 1987 — on Soviet advice, the MPLA launched Operation Salute to October to crush UNITA's southeast and retake Mavinga (and threaten Jamba), staging from Cuito Cuanavale. The Cubans vehemently opposed it (it could invite an "overwhelming South African response"). The advance went well at first — but South Africa intervened to save UNITA ("totally incapable of halting the offensive on their own") with airstrikes and long-range artillery, turning the MPLA drive into a defensive action at the Lomba River. Despite Cuban warnings, the Angolan commanders refused to retreat. Botha approved a larger counterattack — Operation Modular — aiming at "the total destruction of the enemy forces north of the Lomba River and… possible capture of Cuito Cuanavale itself." The Johannesburg Star: "something big and bloody." [00:04:38]

    The 47th Brigade & open war [00:09:50]¶

    • Early Oct 1987 — South Africa annihilated Angola's 47th Brigade. The Cubans ("we were always against the Mavinga operation") were proven right; the Soviet mission head shrugged ("I'm just an advisor"). Then the other side got complacent — UNITA "dallied" ("the boers are here, so we don't have to do any fighting"), Savimbi bragging of a "magnificent victory" while the SADF flew 40 journalists to Mavinga. Nov 11, 1987 — General Geldenhuys publicly announced the SADF was fighting alongside UNITA against the MPLA, Cubans, and Soviets — an open declaration of war (he likened Botha's troop visit to US presidents in Vietnam: "the president wanted to score points"). The UN condemned the offensive, but the US vote was "pure theater" (a winked-at "necessary sop"; Crocker kept the resolution toothless — no sanctions, no aid to Angola). South Africa promised a Christmas withdrawal it never intended. [00:09:50]

    The bridgehead & the Cuba-Soviet rift [00:15:27]¶

    • South Africa planned tank attacks (with UNITA infantry) on the MPLA's bridgehead east of Cuito, intending to leave the captured town to UNITA so Savimbi could claim credit. But the Cubans stood in the way. Moscow-Havana relations were cooling: Castro disliked Gorbachev's perestroika and Moscow's new reluctance to arm Angola (the CIA judged Castro thought Gorbachev "embarked on a disastrous course"). Gorbachev prioritized US normalization over third-world struggles. Nov 15, 1987 — Castro, with Raúl and Risquet, decided to surge another wave of troops, planes, pilots, anti-aircraft weapons, and tanks to the Namibian border by March 1988 — to "force South Africa to agree to a political solution" by intensifying military pressure. As in 1975, this was decided without consulting the USSR. Castro's plan: not reinforce the suicidal southeast ("at the end of the world") but win air supremacy ("Raúl, we have to be the masters of the air") and open a southwest front. [00:15:27]

    Informing, not consulting, Moscow [00:20:01]¶

    • Cuba's deputy defense minister Ulises Rosales briefed Marshal Akhromeyev ("when I told him the first group had already left, he pressed the pencil so hard he broke the point") — Akhromeyev grasped that Cuba was informing, not consulting, and even admitted the Soviets had erred in pushing the Mavinga stand. A new Soviet defense minister accused Cuba of abandoning the offensive; Akhromeyev backed the Cubans. Then Gorbachev and Castro exchanged letters (echoing Kennedy-Khrushchev over the missile crisis): Castro disclaimed responsibility ("the responsibility belongs entirely to the Soviet advisors") and warned of "serious conflict with Cuban troops"; Gorbachev called the decision "a complete surprise… without consulting us." But Havana's decision was final. [00:20:01]

    The southwest offensive & Cuito won in the air [00:24:14]¶

    • The Cubans were emboldened by a post-Iran-Contra, "defanged" Reagan (Castro: "the danger is in Angola… the war is there, not here"). Crocker proposed a 3-year Cuban-withdrawal timetable; the Angolans found the Americans "getting warmer." Gleijeses: the Cuban deployment secured Cuito Cuanavale but did not win the war — by March 1988 the Cubans had won the air battle over Cuito (the South Africans, to their "almost panic," lost air control; their supply by air was cut), a defensive victory. What won the war was the Cuban offensive in the southwest — "the boxer that with the left hand holds the blow and with the right strikes." [00:24:14]

    Driving to Namibia [00:28:00]¶

    • Crocker (inspired by Nyerere: "Namibia is the key… begin in Namibia") had pursued linkage diplomatically — but change came on the battlefield. The Cubans/Angolans moved a major force into Kunene province (a South African "free-fire zone" since the 1984 Lusaka Accord), Castro filling it "with modern conventional combat power." Fearing Pretoria's nuclear arsenal (being finished "with Israeli help"), Cuban troops advanced at night in groups of ≤1,000, "always keeping in mind the possibility that the enemy might use nuclear weapons." By late May 1988 they held a 250-mile front within 12 miles of Namibia (11,000–12,000 of Cuba's best units), with joint Cuban-SWAPO operations. The linchpin: rebuilding the Cahama airfield in weeks (the Soviets had refused fuel tanks to extend jet range) to refuel fighters near Namibia. Breytenbach: "bloody Fidel Castro outwitted South Africa's generals." [00:28:00]

    Calueque & the psychological watershed [00:34:24]¶

    • 1988 negotiations resumed (a new, less warlike NSC Africa director, Herman Cohen). The Cubans, now credible, demanded a seat at the table ("diplomacy is missing a critical ingredient — direct Cuban participation"). Crocker (with Shultz's nod) met the Angolans and Cubans together (Jan/March 1988), then all parties in London. Cuba's two non-negotiable demands: implement Resolution 435 unaltered (Namibian independence) and South Africa cut Savimbi loose. Asked how he knew Cuba would stop at the border, Risquet: "I cannot give you a tranquilizer… tell the South Africans to get out completely of Angola." South Africa stalled at the Cairo summit (June) — Castro: "a proposal written by idiots… we flipped the tortilla." June 26-27, 1988 — South African artillery killed 10 Cubans at Calueque; hours later 10 Cuban MiGs killed 11+ South Africans and bombed the bridge over the Kunene. South Africa blew up a bridge but did not counterattack — "the scorpions took a deep breath and never touched one another again." A psychological watershed (amid a domestic crisis: 143 white South Africans refusing conscription; the ANC's ~200 attacks/year). The NYT: South Africa had "lost a reputation for invincibility." [00:34:24]

    The New York Accords [00:45:03]¶

    • Post-Calueque, South Africa changed its tune: New York talks (July 1988) set a date for Resolution 435 and a staged total Cuban withdrawal (a South African general even asked a Cuban colonel for "a cassette with Cuban music"). Aug 8, 1988 — a Geneva ceasefire; Aug 30 — the last SADF troops left Angola. The New York Accords were signed Dec 22, 1988: Cuban withdrawal over 27 months (66% in year one) ⇄ Namibian independence. The Angolan pledge to stop aiding the ANC was minor; the South African pledge to drop UNITA was "enormous" — Castro: "we're getting a ton and giving a kilogram." (The signing was overshadowed by the Lockerbie bombing the day before, which killed Namibia's UN Commissioner en route.) [00:45:03]

    What Cuito meant [00:49:48]¶

    • Castro "directed every aspect of the campaign… by telephone, from Havana" (Magnus Malan: "how you can do a thing like that, I wouldn't know"). Gleijeses' summary: there was no climactic single battle — Cuito's victory was two elements: the Cuban air victory and the defensive ground victory at the bridgehead, which saved key MPLA brigades and had "great psychological importance — South Africa's onslaught had been broken for all to see." Mandela: "Cuito Cuanavale was the turning point for the liberation of our continent." Antonio Tomás: Angolans built "a whole mythology" around it, crediting it with Namibian independence, South Africa's democratization, and Mandela's freedom. [00:49:48]

    Savimbi's PR collapse [00:54:43]¶

    • Everyone consented to the peace process except Savimbi — Crocker presented him a fait accompli (South Africa exiting, SWAPO coming to power). The 1988 tides had shifted Washington (Iran-Contra purged his fans; an election year without Reagan; the GOP's Southern Africa policy a "sore spot" for Bush). Savimbi's June 1988 US trip (still with an Oval Office Reagan photo-op) was "counterproductive" — protesters outnumbered supporters (Jesse Jackson called him "a blood-sucking vulture"); the MPLA hired its own PR firm branding him "South Africa's secret agent." Abramoff's Red Scorpion (Dolph Lundgren, "loosely based on Savimbi") was exposed as a South African military co-production filmed in Namibia (ads sought "light-skinned people to play the villainous Cubans") — a critical flop. Savimbi's allies (Jesse Helms) did block $150M in UN peacekeeping funds. [00:54:43]

    Savimbi exposed [01:05:03]¶

    • Spring 1989 — even sympathetic biographer Fred Bridgland published a damning portrait: a "brutal and terrifying cult of personality." Meredith: Savimbi purged rivals (and their families), the witch-burnings, choosing/sleeping with his officers' wives, seducing his teenage niece (her protesting parents executed). His foreign and interior ministers quit after he ordered the deaths of Tito Chingunji and his brother-in-law, "their children's heads smashed against a tree." James Baker demanded an account; Savimbi denied it and "rode out the storm." Supporters (Orrin Hatch, National Review's Radek Sikorski) acknowledged the cult yet kept advocating aid; the revelations had "singularly little effect" — Bush kept the military aid flowing. [01:05:03]

    Bicesse & the Cubans go home [01:09:27]¶

    • The New York Accords resolved the international war; the Portugal-mediated Bicesse Accords (May 1991) were to resolve the MPLA-UNITA civil war — a ceasefire, disarmament, and multiparty elections in 1992 (the US still backing Savimbi, having bribed Mobutu to keep arms flowing). For the first time since 1975 Angolans felt "relief, maybe even hope." The one open question: "What would Savimbi do if he lost?" May 1991 — the last Cuban troops left after 16 years; their mission to neutralize South Africa achieved at a high cost (thousands killed; mission head Arnaldo Ochoa tried and executed in 1989 for a Medellín-cartel deal and embezzlement). Castro's regret: withdrawing the troops meant withdrawing the aid workers too — "any Cubans left in Angola would be targets." Angola, the show warns, "was in for something even worse." [01:09:27]
