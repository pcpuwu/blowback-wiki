    Blowback S6E4 — "Zulu & Foxbat"¶

    ← S6E3 — The Stand

    S6E5 — Kicking the Dog →

    🎧 Listen here: Spotify · blowback.show 📄 Read the full transcript

    Hosts: Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · Runtime: ~1:15:30 Thesis: Independence year, 1974–75 — the Carnation Revolution topples Portugal; the three factions' fake unity collapses into civil war; the MPLA seizes Luanda while the CIA (Operation IA/Feature), apartheid South Africa (Operation Savannah — task forces Zulu and Foxbat), and Mobutu's Zaire back the FNLA and Savimbi; and Cuba (Operation Carlota) airlifts thousands of combat troops to save the MPLA, halting the South African blitz days before the Nov 11, 1975 independence.

    Narration order; [hh:mm:ss] = audio jump point; real dates in bold. Speech-to-text slips corrected (Caetano ≈ "Keitano," Neto ≈ "Netto," Chipenda ≈ "Chupenda," Gleijeses ≈ "Glehesus," Telepneva ≈ "Telapneva," Huambo ≈ "Hawaii," Vorster).

    Cold open: "this is a map of Africa" [00:00:03]¶

    • CIA director Bill Colby's actual first NSC briefing on Angola: "gentlemen, this is a map of Africa… here is Angola… the MPLA, they're the bad guys. The FNLA, they're the good guys. And there's UNITA and Jonas Savimbi — we don't know too well." (From CIA officer John Stockwell's later account.) [00:00:03]

    Recap & the Carnation Revolution [00:01:09]¶

    • Recap: the 1961–74 colonial war; the corrupt FNLA (Roberto), the city-bound but rising MPLA, and Savimbi's young UNITA (Ovimbundu south). Then April 25, 1974 — Portugal's Estado Novo fell to a near-bloodless military coup (the Armed Forces Movement); soldiers put red carnations in their gun barrels (200,000 in the streets, "a united people will never be defeated"). The young officers had "decided they were not going to win lucrative benefits from the colonial war"; industrialists saw a better future in democratic Europe than colonial Africa. Guinea-Bissau got swift independence (Sept 1974); Mozambique and Angola were harder. [00:01:09]

    The fake unity [00:06:44]¶

    • A four-way interim government (MPLA/FNLA/UNITA + Portugal) was set up, but the chances were slim. Meredith: the three factions "competed for power, transforming a colonial war into a civil war… drawing the Soviet Union and the United States into a perilous confrontation by proxy." Nov 1974 — Luanda a powder keg (a first clash left 50 dead); the FNLA stockpiled weapons from Zaire (Mobutu building it up with Chinese instructors). Neto rebooted the MPLA (a Sept 1974 conference, new Politburo) and impressed Lisbon — revolutionary Portuguese troops even helped the MPLA take oil-rich Cabinda. Savimbi quietly contacted South Africa (via right-wing Portuguese officers) in late 1974/early 1975, offering to help against SWAPO — "the suitor is Savimbi." [00:06:44]

    The Alvor Agreement → war [00:13:32]¶

    • Jan 1975 — Neto, Roberto, and Savimbi signed the Alvor Agreement in Portugal, setting Independence Day for Nov 11, 1975 with elections and a joint army. But it didn't cap each movement's own troops or barracks — so all three kept arming inside Luanda. A Jan 25 FNLA kidnapping reignited street fighting (a visiting Soviet mission registered as "experts in citrus fruit"). By spring the Eastern Bloc (Poland, East Germany the biggest humanitarian donor) backed the MPLA; the Soviets, after Neto's charm offensive, restored military support. [00:13:32]

    Kissinger & the US covert op [00:16:39]¶

    • Across the Nixon→Ford transition, the constant was Kissinger, who grasped that Portugal's colonies had toppled the dictatorship and feared Portugal "might be the first NATO country to go communist" (the Azores base again). Portugal became Kissinger's "obsession," the colonies a back burner — until the 40 Committee acted: CIA payments to Roberto rose to $10k/month (fall 1974), then $300,000 after Alvor (Savimbi got nothing because "Kissinger had heard of Roberto… but had never heard of Savimbi"). July 14–16, 1975 — the 40 Committee approved covert action ($25M, soon far more), dubbed IA/Feature — back the MPLA's enemies, hope for a pro-Western Angola. Mobutu (his copper economy collapsing) had goaded Washington with pro-PLO/North Korea statements and a fake CIA-coup claim; Kissinger got Ford to approve $50M for Mobutu. [00:16:39]

    John Stockwell's inside view [00:24:01]¶

    • Stockwell (Angola Task Force chief; his 1978 tell-all In Search of Enemies is the key inside record) assembled a team (Nick, Arnie with his 18-inch jungle knife, propagandist "Bubba" who wanted to call it "IA/Phoenix" after the Vietnam terror program, analyst Brenda — and no Black officers, "because of the South African involvement"). Visiting the field: Roberto's FNLA was a malnourished, dishonest "rabble" (Roberto a "cocktail party cowboy" in dark glasses); his men killed 15 MPLA activists in an undefended town — "the fate of Angola was cast, written in blood." Savimbi's UNITA was "a very different caliber" (Stockwell flown in on a Lear jet gifted by a London-Rhodesian mining firm betting on Savimbi): a "savvy operator" with "no profound ideology," who would take help from North Korea, China, the US, or South Africa alike. [00:24:01]

    South Africa enters [00:35:32]¶

    • Pretoria wanted to control southern Africa (directly in Namibia, via friendly regimes in Mozambique); an MPLA Angola would mean an anti-apartheid government sheltering SWAPO. From Feb 1975 the SADF and BOSS courted the FNLA/UNITA (Roberto, Savimbi, Chipenda promising to bar Namibian fighters for South African support). July 1975 — secret deals to arm them and launch an invasion "disguised as a mercenary operation." Savimbi: "if you are a drowning man in a crocodile-infested river… you don't question who is pulling you to the bank until you are safely on it." US-SA coordination was tacit — Stockwell: "without any memos… coordination was affected at all CIA levels." August — the SADF seized the Calueque Dam, its toehold. [00:35:32]

    Cuba commits — Operation Carlota [00:41:48]¶

    • The MPLA held Luanda and Cabinda, hoping to survive to November; the socialist bloc divided the labor (USSR, Poland, Bulgaria, East Germany's $2M). The decisive turn came from Havana: Cuba (advisors since 1965) sent 230 instructors in June, then — after the South African moves — Castro launched Operation Carlota (480 specialists building four training centers, 16 battalions). The first contingent flew in as "healthy tourists roasted by the Caribbean sun," machine guns in their briefcases. Castro's rationale partly mirrored America's (post-Vietnam/Watergate constraints); Cuba could not appear to ally with racist South Africa. Volunteers were so eager an engineer posed as a truck driver to get in; "no Cuban was forced to go." (Marquez's Operation Carlota is the source.) [00:41:48]

    Operation Savannah: Zulu & Foxbat [00:49:48]¶

    • By autumn the CIA op was in full swing (paramilitaries, French/Portuguese mercenaries, a stolen-plane air force, De Beers-borrowed aircraft). The cover cracked: abandoned FNLA crates bore "fresh U.S. Air Force shipping labels," and a NYT front page exposed millions/month to Portugal and Angola. A Sept 40-Committee transcript caught Kissinger's realization: "we've blown it, basically." Oct 14, 1975 — South Africa launched Operation Savannah with Task Force Zulu (~1,000 men, Chipenda's FNLA + ex-Portuguese Angolans, white SADF officers) and Task Force Foxbat (UNITA + SADF instructors). Zulu raced up the coast "like a Sunday drive" (festive music on the tanks) toward Benguela/Lobito; one mercenary column commander rode in a Honda sports car beside "a blonde film actress" — blown to pieces (her bag held a bikini and an invitation to Roberto's "victory celebrations"). Cuban commandos halted the blitz. [00:49:48]

    Independence on a knife's edge [01:00:11]¶

    • Even days before, Moscow, Portugal, and Tanzania's Nyerere still hoped for a three-way accommodation; the Republic of Congo urged no compromise ("Mobutu… a gigantic snake"). The Soviets finally unilaterally recognized the MPLA (Neto: "we have been understood… now we will certainly win"). Nov 2, 1975 — the MPLA and Cubans blasted the South African column with artillery (the first Cubans fell in combat). Nov 11, 1975 — Castro's commitment leapt to thousands of combat troops (taking the Soviets by surprise — too late to dissuade); crowds in Luanda raised the new MPLA flag (machete + factory cog) while Zairean guns pounded the northern suburbs and Cuban guns held the South African columns to the south. The CIA threw an Independence Day "wine and cheese" party in Langley expecting to hear Luanda had fallen — it hadn't. [01:00:11]

    The MPLA turns the tide [01:09:09]¶

    • After independence, reinforced by a 10,000-man Cuban seaborne army (outnumbering the invaders) and Soviet officers (so under-provisioned the Cubans shared their food), the MPLA struck back. Nov 13 — Zulu took Novo Redondo but was stopped by a blown bridge; Foxbat, with no supply line deep in-country, faltered and reversed. Dec 23, 1975 — South Africa decided to withdraw. Ford approved another $7M (CIA total >$30M), but Dec 19 Congress had balked at more covert funds (post-Vietnam/Watergate/Church Committee). "Washington would not abandon Angola — there was still the Safari Club" (the Shah of Iran, the Saudis, and Sadat funding Mobutu). The lines were drawn "for a new war, both international and fratricidal… that would last for decades, until Jonas Savimbi's death in 2002." [01:09:09]
