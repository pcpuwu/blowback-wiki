> **Thesis:** The road from the UN charade to the actual **invasion** of March 2003 — how the diplomacy was theater for a war already decided, how the "coalition" was bought, and what the invasion looked like on the ground.

> _Narration order; `[hh:mm:ss]` = audio jump point; real dates in **bold**._

## Cold open: Powell's last stand `[00:00:00]`
- **March 17, 2003** — UK Foreign Secretary **Robin Cook** resigns over the war ("with a heavy heart"). The contrast: **Colin Powell**, the one insider with the credibility to stop the war with a resignation, instead **gave the UN speech** for it. Per Woodward's *Plan of Attack*, Powell felt he couldn't "walk away" — loyalty to the president, the soldier's code, the troops. A career pattern: he'd covered up My Lai and Iran-Contra and prosecuted the 1991 war. The episode's title nods to Tim Burton's *Mars Attacks!*, whose Powell-like general says: "if I just stayed in place and never spoke up, then things are bound to happen." `[00:00:00]`

## The UN as PR `[00:05:23]`
- Polls: **64%** supported war — but only **33%** if no allies joined. Hence Powell's UN push (Andy Card: "you don't introduce new products in August"). Meanwhile war-planning had been underway since Nov 2001 under euphemisms like the "Office of Special Plans." `[00:05:23]`
- **The damned-if-you-do trap** `[00:08:22]`: Saddam invited weapons inspectors in *before* any resolution forced him — which the administration treated as a *setback* (and "evidence of guilt"). Press secretary **Ari Fleischer** (Dec 2, 2002): if Saddam says he has WMD he's lying, if he says he has none he's also lying. The point of any resolution was to control the language so Saddam could be declared in violation no matter what. `[00:08:22]`
- **Resolution 1441** (early Nov 2002, 15–0) `[00:10:00]` — extracted via threats/bribes: Bulgaria (NATO/aid), Guinea & Cameroon (market access), Mexico (Congress retaliation threat), Pakistan (aid + "nice insurgency you've got"), Syria (terror-list carrot), France (wine/water sanctions threat), Germany (pull US bases). Inspections began Nov 2002. `[00:10:00]`
- **"Slam dunk"** `[00:13:48]` — Dec 2002, the CIA pitched Bush the WMD case; even *Bush* said "nice try… not something Joe Public would gain confidence from," prompting **George Tenet**'s infamous "**it's a slam dunk case.**" A source told Woodward: "we don't want a smoking gun… the whole intent of 1441 was to keep the burden off us." `[00:13:48]`

## CIA in Kurdistan: the "rock stars" `[00:16:25]`
- The CIA flooded Kurdistan with so many $100 bills it caused local **hyperinflation**; its top informant was a suicidal alcoholic. It *did* recruit real assets ("rock stars") inside the regime — including a bodyguard of Saddam's son Qusay — via "Thuraya" satellite phones. Then a call came in under duress: "**We knew you were CIA.**" An asset appeared beaten on Iraqi TV; the base **never again heard from 30 of its 87 phones.** `[00:16:25]`

## Chalabi cashes in (and delivers nothing) `[00:19:32]`
- Chalabi courted post-invasion backers, dangling Iraqi oil reserves "greater than Saudi Arabia's," posing as the clean democratic alternative. His follower **Francis Brooke** said he'd back removing Saddam "even if it killed every single Iraqi." `[00:19:32]`
- His one real assignment — recruit a **~3,000-strong militia** to aid the invasion — produced **zero** recruits (the Pentagon scraped together 74). He got paid anyway. This fed the Pentagon's chronic **"not enough troops"** worry (Rumsfeld's light-footprint vs. what commanders wanted; a WMD-team officer's haiku: "Rumsfeld is a dick / Won't flow the forces we need / We will be too light"). `[00:19:32]`

## 2003 opens badly for the case `[00:24:21]`
- **Late Jan 2003** — UN inspector **Hans Blix**'s first report: Iraq cooperated "rather well," access granted to all sites, **no WMD found.** The administration spun it ("the problem with guns that are hidden is you can't see their smoke"). `[00:24:21]`
- Bush's **Jan 2003 State of the Union** still aired the debunked **Niger uranium** claim ("16 words") — pulled from an earlier speech but thrown back in. `[00:26:23]`
- **The second resolution** `[00:27:24]` — initially the US planned to rely on 1441 alone, but **Blair** (facing a possible no-confidence vote at home) begged for a second resolution for legitimacy; Bush, who respected Blair, told his people to try.

## Powell's UN speech `[00:28:47]`
- **Feb 5, 2003** — Powell tells the Security Council "every statement… is backed up by sources, solid sources," then recites the cooked intel: aluminum tubes (centrifuges), the Chalabi/Curveball **mobile labs**, **Zarqawi** (actually a bin Laden *rival* in territory Saddam didn't control), even the anthrax. Much of what he cited his own State Department had flagged "weak." It worked as an *emotional* appeal — the "**Powell buy-in**." Liberal columnist Mary McGrory: "he persuaded me, and I was as tough as France to convince." `[00:28:47]`

## The movement, and a leak `[00:31:54]`
- **Feb 15, 2003** — coordinated global protests; Guinness called it the **largest protest in human history** (millions across dozens of cities). The hosts note the US anti-war movement later withered after hitching itself to a Democratic Party that "doesn't give a shit about you" (Pelosi funding the wars post-2006). `[00:31:54]`
- **Mid-Feb** — Blix's fuller update: still nothing, no sign Iraq was tipped off or hiding things; he wanted a couple more months. `[00:33:48]`
- **Katharine Gun** `[00:35:24]` — a GCHQ translator leaked a memo showing the **US was spying on Security Council members to blackmail them** into backing war (revealed March 2, 2003). Charged under the Official Secrets Act; case later dropped to avoid a trial of the war's legality. `[00:35:24]`
- France's **Chirac** publicly refuses; the **second resolution dies.** `[00:36:54]`

## The "coalition of the willing" `[00:37:27]`
- Militarily a joke (Finland sent **two** troops). Categorized by what they got:
  - **"New Europe"** (Rumsfeld's term) — post-Soviet states (Albania, Bulgaria, Croatia, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Macedonia, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia), nearly all in line for **NATO** (and got in).
  - **Gulf autocracies** — Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE: small, oil-rich, reliant on US military protection.
  - **Silent partners** — **Saudi Arabia** (Prince Bandar was shown the battle plans and ran out jotting notes for Riyadh) and **Israel** (Peres warned of Saddam's nuclear path; **Netanyahu** told Congress toppling Saddam would have "enormous positive reverberations"). `[00:37:27]`

## Inside Iraq before the war `[00:41:49]`
- Saddam told his own generals he had **no WMD** — they were stunned. Iraqi voice **Ra'ed** rebuts the later myth that "Saddam pretended to have WMD": he publicly said on Al-Jazeera, "**Iraq is free of all weapons of mass destruction.**" Per *Cobra II*, Saddam was so sure the US wouldn't win the political debate that he **refused to mine the Persian Gulf** to avoid giving a pretext. `[00:41:49]`
- The human baseline (Anthony Shadid's *Night Draws Near*): by 2003, sanctions and the Gulf War had cut incomes to **one-fifth** of 1991, doubled infant mortality, dropped adult literacy from 90% to under 60%, left the country on a UN-described "semi-starvation diet." An Iraqi on the coming "liberation": "they're going to burn the forest to kill the fox." (Last time Iraqis heard "liberators": a **British** general in 1917.) `[00:45:53]`

## The invasion `[00:46:43]`
- **Mid-March 2003** — inspectors pulled out. **March 14**, Tommy Franks screens *Gladiator* to psych up the troops. **March 17**, Bush's 48-hour ultimatum. **March 19**, special ops cross from the west; an attempted decapitation strike on Saddam at **Dora Farms** (bad intel — 15 civilians killed, Saddam not there). A "deck of cards" of 55 wanted Iraqis. `[00:46:43]`
- **March 21 — "shock and awe"** `[00:48:18]`: the opening aerial salvo killed as many as **6,000 Iraqis** (none of the 55 cards). CNN anchors openly giddy. Ground forces (US V Corps + Marines; British to Basra) advanced fast. `[00:48:18]`
- Hard fighting: **Nasiriyah**; the expected southern uprising **didn't happen**; the fanatical **Fedayeen Saddam** (often teenagers, RPGs vs. tanks) shocked the Americans. **April 4**, an airstrike on a Basra house meant for **Chemical Ali** killed 17 innocents (he wasn't there). "Precision" bombs hit working-class Shiite markets; a bridge massacre of civilian families ("good people stay home"). `[00:49:53]`
- **Mid-April** — Basra then Baghdad fall; Saddam goes into hiding. **April 9** — the **staged toppling of the Saddam statue** in Firdos Square (a small crowd couldn't do it; a US armored vehicle did — a photo op for the Arab world). `[00:53:01]`
- **May 1** — Bush's "**Mission Accomplished**" carrier landing declares major combat over. `[01:01:33]`*(near end)*
- **Closer — Jessica Lynch** `[00:56:43]`: the war's first manufactured "hero." Her capture was spun into a Rambo fable; her "rescue" was **staged** (blank rounds, no Iraqi troops present, cooperative doctors). Lynch told Congress in 2007 it was all false: "it's sad that they want to make up stories… now it's kind of hard to believe them." `[00:56:43]`
