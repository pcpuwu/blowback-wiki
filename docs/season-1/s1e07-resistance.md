## Cold open `[00:00:00]`
- An Iraqi parable: "the little serpent [Saddam] has left, and the great serpent [the Americans] has come." Getting rid of Saddam didn't make the occupation good. `[00:00:00]`

## Muqtada al-Sadr & the Mahdi Army `[00:01:48]`
- Demographics: Iraq is ~2/3 **Shia**, but Saddam's Sunni Ba'ath had kept them on a tight leash (no pilgrimages, clerics controlled). With Saddam gone, the long-underground Shia religious networks surfaced — initially organizing anti-looting patrols and services while the US (per Rumsfeld) said "don't bother intervening." `[00:01:48]`
- **April 10, 2003** — the US-favored liberal Ayatollah **al-Khoei** is hacked to death by a mob in Najaf; Garner's team shrugged ("just them killing each other"). The mob was loyal to a then-obscure cleric under 30: **Muqtada al-Sadr** (religious royalty — Saddam killed his father and brothers in 1999). `[00:04:53]`
- **Sadrism** `[00:06:43]`: Islamic social norms + **social justice**, base among the poor. Its real population center was the Baghdad slum **Saddam City → renamed Sadr City** (2–2.5M people in ~20 sq km). The **Mahdi Army** earned support by returning stolen cars, running checkpoints, delivering kerosene/flour, paying salaries — "protection for people who can't go to the cops." `[00:06:43]`
- Al-Sadr was *straightforward* — anti-American, "what you see is what you get" — but the US insisted on misreading him (Bremer's liaison psychoanalyzed his "daddy issues"). His Iran trip got him funding to grow the Mahdi Army (Iran also backed **SCIRI**/Badr) — but his interests ≠ Iran's: he wanted an Islamically observant Iraq *free of the Americans*, not an Iranian client state. Excluded from the toothless **Iraqi Governing Council**, which only boosted his untainted legitimacy. `[00:09:37]`

## The Sunni insurgency begins `[00:16:31]`
- The invasion radicalized Sunnis inside Iraq *and* drew in foreign Wahhabi jihadists (from Egypt, Jordan, Saudi). **Aug 2003** — suicide bombings begin, loosely attributed to **Abu Musab al-Zarqawi**. By end of 2003, a couple hundred Iraqi civilians dead; the toll would skyrocket. `[00:16:31]`
- **March 2004** — suicide bombs hit a Shia Ashura ritual, killing 130+ in Baghdad and Karbala. `[00:18:04]`

## Bremer vs. al-Sadr `[00:18:21]`
- **March 2004** — Bremer shut al-Sadr's newspaper for 60 days (the offending article: "Bremer follows in footsteps of Saddam" — so he proved it wrong by closing a paper). The response: tens of thousands protested; the **Mahdi Army seized chunks of southern Iraq and expelled coalition forces from Najaf**; the "**Black Sunday**" ambush in Baghdad killed US soldiers. Sadr couldn't hold the territory, but proved two points: **Sadr City** became an unremovable stronghold, and Najaf an embarrassment that even drew Sunni fighters' admiration. `[00:18:21]`
- **June 2004** — Bremer hands off (a manila folder) to interim PM **Iyad Allawi** (the CIA ex-Ba'athist from Ep2). Ayatollah **Ali al-Sistani** (the senior Shia spiritual leader, a subtle power-broker) — whom Allawi foolishly tried to keep out of the country ("the turbans"). **Aug 2004** — a brutal Marine battle in Najaf leaves al-Sadr still standing, in control. `[00:21:23]`

## The Zarqawi myth & the PSYOP `[00:25:03]`
- Zarqawi (Powell's UN bogeyman) was an unremarkable Jordanian — Afghan-war veteran, jailed in Jordan in the 90s, **anti-Shia**, operating in a part of Iraq **Saddam didn't control**, and (per his own associates) a bin Laden *rival* who'd sworn no al-Qaeda oath. Saddam may not have even known he was there. The truth: **the invasion unleashed Zarqawi**, not the reverse. `[00:25:03]`
- **The smoking gun** `[00:30:08]`: Thomas Ricks (WaPo, **April 2006**) exposed Pentagon PSYOP slides — "**villainize Zarqawi / leverage xenophobia**," a "selective leak to Dexter Filkins" (NYT) — designed to "eliminate popular support for a potentially sympathetic insurgency." Gen. Mark Kimmitt called it "the most successful influence operation the army had run to date." Rumsfeld compared Zarqawi to **Hitler**. The point: blame foreign jihadists so you never admit an **occupied people are rising up.** `[00:30:08]`
- **The CIA's own finding** (WaPo, 2005): the insurgency was "newly radicalized Sunni Iraqis, nationalists offended by the occupying force" — not just Ba'athist dead-enders. And the vast majority of attacks targeted **coalition forces** (only ~4–5% on civilians). `[00:36:37]`
- **What actually fueled it** `[00:37:05]`: de-Ba'athification + the army's dissolution + occupation violence (night raids, killed kids) radicalized Sunnis who felt dispossessed by Saddam's fall and feared a Shia-American conspiracy — bound by familial/communal ties, not Zarqawi. Their signature: **IEDs / roadside bombs** on the nightly news. `[00:37:05]`

## Fallujah `[00:39:46]`
- **March 31, 2004** — four **Blackwater** contractors killed in Fallujah; two bodies strung from a bridge (footage rebroadcast endlessly). Insurgents had nearly killed CENTCOM chief Gen. Abizaid. `[00:39:46]`
- **First battle — Operation Valiant Resolve (April 4)** `[00:41:41]`: Mattis's order — "kill or capture… emphasis on **kill**." A spectacular failure; the interim Iraqi government forced a US pullout by **May 1**. The stopgap **Fallujah Brigade** (1,600 men, many ex-insurgents, US-armed) was a "fiasco." `[00:41:41]`
- **Second battle (Nov 7–Dec 2004)** `[00:44:57]`: ~8,000 US/Iraqi troops vs. ~3,000 insurgents; civilians fled. The single most combat-heavy event of the war: **36,000 of 50,000 homes destroyed**, plus 60 schools and 65 mosques; a hospital seized (patients/doctors bound on the floor); **white phosphorus** and **depleted-uranium** rounds used. US admitted ~1,200 insurgents + ~800 civilians killed; later estimates up to **6,000, mostly civilians**. A 2010 study found birth-defect/cancer rates **worse than Hiroshima**. The aftermath: a **police state** (DNA + retina scans, mandatory address badges, no cars, forced-labor battalions to clear the rubble the US created). The hosts: the US "knew what they were doing… the goal was to terrorize." Net effect: one step forward, five back — it **emboldened** the Sunni insurgency. `[00:44:57]`

## Abu Ghraib `[00:51:36]`
- Gen. McChrystal later: "**nearly every first-time jihadist claimed Abu Ghraib had first jolted him into action.**" `[00:51:36]`
- **Jan 2004** — Rumsfeld mentions the "incident" to Bush; an investigation is "underway." **April 28, 2004** — *60 Minutes*, followed by **Sy Hersh** in the New Yorker, broke it open with the soldiers' own photos. The **Taguba report** catalogued the torture (phosphoric liquid poured on detainees, beatings, forced nudity and sexual humiliation, sodomy with objects, dog attacks, naked human pyramids). Hersh and a WaPo report alleged worse — including the sexual abuse of boys — and **detainee deaths** in custody. `[00:53:14]`
- Soldiers said they were encouraged by military intelligence/CIA; the **Bush torture memos** (released 2009) linked the abuses to what the administration had explicitly authorized. Repercussions: a video of the **Nick Berg** beheading (claimed by Zarqawi); but punishments were light — **12 soldiers convicted, 2 colonels dismissed**; **Rumsfeld kept his job** (Cheney protected him). Bush's first response (Al-Arabiya, **May 5, 2004**) wasn't an apology — it was self-congratulation that, unlike "a dictator," the US investigates itself. `[00:57:36]`
