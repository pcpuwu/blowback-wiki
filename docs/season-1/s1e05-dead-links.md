# Blowback S1E5 — "Dead Links" (feat. Will Menaker)

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s1e05-dead-links.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Guest:** Will Menaker (Chapo) · **Runtime:** 1:01:48
> **Thesis:** The **media's** role in selling the war — how the press (above all the *New York Times* and Judith Miller) laundered the case for war, why the few who got it right were ignored, and a tour of the pundits who cheered it on.
>
> _This is the show's thematic "media" episode, so it's structured as the media machinery + a chronological pundit ledger rather than a dated event timeline. `[hh:mm:ss]` = audio jump point; real dates in **bold**._

## The seeding `[00:02:50]`
- **Dec 2001** — Cheney on *Meet the Press* claims a 9/11 hijacker (Atta) met an Iraqi intelligence officer in **Prague** ("pretty well confirmed" — it wasn't). Echoing **James Woolsey**'s day-one (Sept 12) finger at Saddam. `[00:02:50]`
- **Laurie Mylroie** (American Enterprise Institute) had long pushed the Saddam-did-it thesis — and **co-wrote a 1990 book on Saddam with NYT reporter Judith Miller.** `[00:03:55]`

## Judith Miller and the *New York Times* `[00:04:14]`
- Miller's beat was chemical/biological weapons + terrorism; after 9/11, editor **Howell Raines** (the worst modern Times editor, of Jayson Blair infamy) reportedly told her: *"go win us a Pulitzer."* `[00:04:31]`
- **The anthrax attacks (Oct 2001)** `[00:05:39]` — letters to media and government (incl. Mike Pence's office) freaked the country out as much as 9/11; **never solved**; the suspected al-Qaeda link was disproven. (Aside: the US sold Saddam anthrax in the 80s.) Andrew Sullivan's contemporaneous "flailing pigeons / Iraqi connection" post captures the hysteria.
- **Miller's blockbuster bylines:**
  - **Dec 20, 2001** — an Iraqi defector "tells of renovations" at WMD sites `[00:08:55]`
  - **Feb 3, 2002** — Kuwaitis lobbying for action (aided by neocon pundits like Fouad Ajami) `[00:09:01]`
  - **March 2002** — framing **OPCW chief José Bustani** as too soft on Saddam. The real story (per Mehdi Hasan): **John Bolton** threatened Bustani — *"We know where your kids live… you have two sons in New York"* — to force him out. Miller reported it as Bustani being a wimp. `[00:09:25]`

## "Black September" 2002 `[00:11:36]`
- **Sept 8, 2002** — the **aluminum tubes** story (Miller + Michael Gordon): tubes "only" for centrifuges → Saddam building a nuke. Then quoted by Cheney/Rice on the Sunday shows — the **echo-chamber laundering** trick (plant it in the Times, then cite the Times). `[00:11:36]`
- **Sept 13** — "Baghdad's Arsenal" listing Iraq's supposed weapons steps. **Sept 18** — "verification is difficult at best… maybe impossible," demonizing the inspections themselves. Miller **omitted** Hussein Kamel's 1990s admission that Iraq had destroyed its WMD. `[00:11:46]`
- **The inspectors as the "near enemy"** `[00:13:48]` — the media discredited Blix, **Scott Ritter**, and arms-control experts. The logic: *not finding anything proves how well it's hidden* — the exact **Team B** move (Wolfowitz, Perle) used on phantom Soviet doomsday weapons in the Reagan era, now run by the same people with a cozy NYT relationship. The Times' own editorial board, treating its reporting as fact, endorsed "the use of force." `[00:13:48]`
- **Why the press did it** `[00:18:14]`: 9/11 gave journalists "a sense of purpose"; they wanted to be part of "a new greatest generation" with a worthy villain — and to *prove they weren't the Vietnam-era press* by being the "good guys" who back a good war.

## The Chalabi pipeline & the mea culpas `[00:20:00]`
- Miller's 2005-revealed email admitted **Chalabi "has provided most of the front-page exclusives on WMD to our paper."** The "intel" was a circular game of telephone — Chalabi → Cheney/Wolfowitz/Rumsfeld *and* → Miller, everyone "drinking from the same river of sewage," telling each other what they wanted to hear. The only real mark: "the fucking sucker reading the New York Times." `[00:20:00]`
- **NYT's 2004 mea culpa** ("From the Editors: The Times and Iraq") admitted failures but never named Miller cleanly. The fuller accounting: Michael Massing's "**Now They Tell Us**" (NYRB, Feb 2004). Miller ended up at **Fox News**. `[00:21:36]`
- **Who got it right** `[00:26:08]` — the **Knight Ridder** Washington bureau (Landay, Strobel, Walcott) ran the mirror-image, accurate headlines ("Lack of hard evidence…," "CIA analysts split…") — and got no cable play. Proof the press *could* have done its job and chose not to. The *Washington Post* buried its skeptical stories off the front page (while Walter Pincus etc. reported them). `[00:26:08]`
- **Phil Donahue** `[00:31:36]` — the lone anti-war voice on cable, **fired by MSNBC** in the run-up (an internal memo feared his anti-war stance); replaced by a segment literally called **"Countdown: Iraq."** The pattern: the war-sellers **failed upward** (Goldberg, Frum still prominent); the people who were right stayed anonymous. `[00:31:36]`

## The pundit ledger (chronological) `[00:36:58]`
- **Jan 2002** — Kristol & Kagan (Weekly Standard): forget bin Laden, "the clock is ticking in Iraq." `[00:37:12]`
- **2002** — Michael Walzer (*Dissent*) scolds the left for reflexive anti-war "incoherence" (distinguishing "just" wars like Kosovo 1999 — the hosts' note: the first "good liberal war," done without UN approval, that worsened what it claimed to fix). `[00:39:36]`
- **Tom Friedman** `[00:42:06]` — "**suck on this**" (the war was about telling Iraqis "which part of this sentence don't you understand"); **July 31, 2002** column: an Iraqi "Thomas Jefferson," ramped-up oil, **destabilizing OPEC** — "a two-for-one sale… buy one, get one free."
- **Jonathan Chait** — "Why Liberals Should Support the War." **Nick Kristof** — "I'm a wimp… in favor of invading, but only if we can win easily." `[00:44:34]`
- **Fred Barnes** (Weekly Standard, **April 2004**) — full victim-blaming a year into the occupation: traffic jams = success, the only obstacle is "the attitude of many Iraqis," wishing for "an outbreak of **gratitude** for the greatest act of benevolence one country has ever done for another." `[00:46:10]`
- **David Brooks** `[00:48:36]` — "dream palaces" (April 2003: the anti-war left will "lose self-confidence and vitality") → 2004: "I never thought it would be this bad" → 2015: the **strangle-Hitler-in-his-crib / butterfly-effect** defense ("to erase mistakes from the past is to obliterate your world now").
- **The New Republic** (the liberal-hawk gold standard — Pollack, Beinart, Wieseltier, Peretz) `[00:52:01]`: a June 2004 editorial titled "**Were we wrong?**" — "we feel regret, but no shame," pivoting to "Iraqis are, in some meaningful sense, free." Kenneth Pollack's *The Threatening Storm* = the "serious" case. **Matt Yglesias**'s mea culpa: Pollack's argument was "invade and a **pony** shows up" — except "we're missing the pony." `[00:52:01]`
- **Andrew Sullivan** — conversion **Oct 31, 2006** ("many liberals are indeed my brothers and sisters"). **Friedman**, Aug 2006: "we are not midwifing democracy… we are **babysitting a civil war.**" `[00:55:48]`
- **Jeffrey Goldberg** (now editor of *The Atlantic*) `[00:57:48]` — his Oct 3, 2002 Slate piece "**Aflatoxin**": dismisses war critics as having "limited experience in the Middle East" (the hosts note Goldberg's own Mideast "experience" was as an Israeli prison-camp guard), concluding the invasion "will be remembered as an act of profound morality."
- **The throughline:** almost none of these people suffered professional consequences — they were promoted *because* they "took the hit for the team." Closes on Seymour Hersh: "It doesn't matter what I think… it matters what I can learn." `[00:58:59]`
