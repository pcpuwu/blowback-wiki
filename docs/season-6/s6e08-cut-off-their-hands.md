## Recap & roadmap `[00:01:01]`
- Recap: Reagan's first term, constructive engagement, South Africa's continued war and grip on Namibia, Angola leaning on the Cubans, the Lusaka deal (with SWAPO barred). This episode: that peace deal coming apart, Reagan's re-election, another assault on the Clark Amendment, a tighter embrace of Savimbi just before Iran-Contra, Angola's market reforms (like Vietnam's), and the turning of US public opinion against apartheid. `[00:01:01]`

## Reagan's term-two unleashing `[00:05:08]`
- After a rough start (10%+ inflation), the 1983 economic turnaround (inflation to ~3%) and a string of "hurrah" moments — the 1981 Libyan jet shootdown (later Top Gun's climax), arming El Salvador/the Contras, the 1982 Marines in Lebanon (barracks bombed, 241 dead, 1983), and the 1983 **Grenada** invasion ("expelled Vietnam syndrome") — carried Reagan to a 59-41 landslide over Mondale (Nov 1984). Hawks Casey and Weinberger, restrained in term one, were now "unleashed" under the **Reagan Doctrine** of rollback — its prize proxy: Savimbi. `[00:05:08]`

## Crocker between left and right `[00:09:58]`
- Crocker was attacked from both sides (a shield for apartheid to the left; a "lost China" appeaser to the right — "why is Chester Crocker trying to sell 20 million black Africans into slavery?"). The pro-Savimbi hawks were led by CIA director **Casey**; Chas Freeman described Crocker's counter-move: phoning Thatcher's office so "mommy" could tell "Ronnie, don't do this thing." Crocker and Shultz wanted *national reconciliation*, not a UNITA victory — Savimbi as leverage, not a winner — which annoyed both the right and Savimbi. `[00:09:58]`

## Lusaka collapses `[00:13:33]`
- South Africa kept acting militarily: **July 1984** — SADF commandos blew up Cabinda oil pipelines and mined ships in Luanda harbor (UNITA claimed credit; US officials knew it was Pretoria). **April 1985** — a 9-man SADF team was caught near Gulf oil tanks at Malongo (a prisoner confessed "we were attacking Gulf oil") — "the first time it had been caught red-handed." Angola scuttled the next phase of talks. Gleijeses: Luanda and Pretoria had "virtually incompatible priorities… the entire negotiation was built on quicksand." South Africa also moved to install a SWAPO-excluding puppet government in Windhoek. `[00:13:33]`

## US opinion turns on apartheid `[00:17:38]`
- Reagan didn't just avoid criticizing Pretoria — he praised Botha's "reforms" and loosened export controls (2,000+ shock batons sold in 1982; South Africa restarted its chem/bio program **Project Coast**, betting on US indifference). But the sham 1984 constitution (rights for Indians and "coloureds," not blacks) sparked township uprisings (~2,000 killed by 1986). **Late 1984** — "for the first time in U.S. history, South Africa became the subject of widespread debate." Daily anti-apartheid protests; **Rosa Parks** arrested at the embassy; **Desmond Tutu** won the Nobel and met Reagan (who brushed him off, writing in his diary the real problem was "tribal, not racial"). **Feb 1985** — **Mandela** rejected Botha's conditional-release offer (read by daughter Zinzi: "I will remain a member of the African National Congress until the day I die"). The township revolt impressed even the Cubans (Raúl Castro to the SACP's Joe Slovo: "tell us what we can do to help you"). The movement complicated constructive engagement and drove congressional sanctions calls. `[00:17:38]`

## The Jamba Jamboree `[00:24:34]`
- South Africa kept arming UNITA (deliveries tripled in 1983). Savimbi embraced the "**freedom fighter**" label — a term coined by right-wing adventurer **Jack Wheeler** ("the Indiana Jones of the right"), who profiled Savimbi for *Reason*. In **June 1985**, Wheeler and a 26-year-old **Jack Abramoff** (with funding from drugstore heir Lou Lehrman's "Citizens for America," White House-blessed) staged the "**Democratic International**" at Jamba — gathering Nicaraguan Contras, Laotian guerrillas, and Afghan mujahideen reps (Renamo and the Khmer Rouge couldn't attend) for a PR "happening." Reporters arrived on the "Vomit Comet" DC-3; the South African origin of everything was obvious (a translator called Savimbi "Dr. Zimbabwe"). Reagan sent a tepid letter ("their goals are our goals"); Crocker called it South Africa's "wishful thinking." Crocker's memoir hints the real organizer was **Bill Casey**. `[00:24:34]`

## Clark Amendment repealed & the aid fight `[00:34:52]`
- **July 1985** — the **Clark Amendment was repealed** (after 9 years); Savimbi was "ecstatic" (he called the 1976 amendment "a green light for the Russians"). Furious, Luanda ended talks with South Africa. A Pepper/Kemp push for an *overt* $27M aid package was scuttled by an unusual liberal-Democrat + Reagan-administration team-up (Crocker/Shultz wanted *covert* aid, since neighboring states wouldn't tolerate open South Africa-linked support). Four days later Reagan authorized a secret program — $18M of lethal aid. As under Kissinger in 1975, the aim was to **tie down** the Soviets, not topple the MPLA (Crocker: "there was no question of creating conditions for a UNITA victory"). Conservatives made backing Savimbi a 1988 GOP litmus test. `[00:34:52]`

## Operation Second Congress `[00:39:45]`
- The Angolans (with 60 Soviet advisors, the Cubans abstaining over strategy) launched **Operation Second Congress** (Aug 1985) to retake Mavinga. It nearly succeeded — forcing South Africa to deploy "the largest number of forces to Angola since 1975" to stop it, exposing its "open secret" support for Savimbi (a problem for UNITA at the UN). The MPLA was pushed back (as the Cubans had predicted). The episode hardened Castro's resolve for a strategy "that would force the South Africans out permanently": "South Africa has its hands in Angola. **It is time to cut them off**… we will deepen the crisis of apartheid." `[00:39:45]`

## The home front & Gorbachev's "no" `[00:43:30]`
- **Feb 1986** — Castro pitched air support to the new Soviet leader **Gorbachev** (their first meeting) — turned down, repeatedly. Militarily, fortune still favored Angola: a 1986 UNITA/South African attack on Cuito Cuanavale failed, met by a successful MPLA tank counterattack ("I have never seen people run so far" — Breytenbach), and stasis returned. But Angola's domestic situation was grim: the 1986 oil-price collapse cost $200M+; dos Santos himself decried "rampant corruption" and over-centralization; 46-47% of the budget went to the military; once a food exporter, Angola now imported most of its food (only 2% of arable land cultivated; 120,000 tons of emergency aid needed). **Landmines** maimed ~15,000 civilians ("this war that strikes from farm to farm"). At its Dec 1985 Second Congress the MPLA reaffirmed socialism, but by 1986 was moving toward the **IMF**. Cuba too was hurting (debt crisis, drought) — but didn't change its Africa policy. `[00:43:30]`

## The selling of Jonas Savimbi `[00:49:23]`
- From summer 1985, Savimbi retained **Black, Manafort & Stone** for $600,000/year — monthly Washington updates, coaching on answering critics and complimenting patrons, ties to Heritage/CPAC. **Jan-Feb 1986** — his biggest press coup: 60 Minutes, Nightline, McNeil-Lehrer, a Time spread ("Red Carpet for an African Rebel"), warnings to US firms (Chevron, David Rockefeller opposed aid: "when two elephants fight, the grass suffers"), an **Oval Office photo-op** with Reagan ("our Contra in Africa"), and a rapturous **CPAC** reception. John Judis noted the self-greasing wheel: Savimbi banks US money → pays for PR → lobby agitates for more aid. (Jesse Jackson toured Angola urging recognition.) **March 1986** — UNITA got comms gear, anti-tank weapons, and the crown jewel: **Stinger** missiles (like the mujahideen), routed via Mobutu's Zaire; CIA officers stationed at Jamba; a St. Lucia Airways C-130 airlift Robert Gates called "a masterpiece of logistical planning." Yet 1986 was not a battlefield success for UNITA. `[00:49:23]`

## Iran-Contra & Angola `[01:01:49]`
- **Oct 1986** — CIA pilot **Eugene Hasenfus** was shot down over Nicaragua, exposing the illegal Contra supply network; **Nov 3** — a Lebanese paper broke the Iran arms-sale (McFarlane, Oliver North; Israel's participation; profits diverted to the Contras around the congressional ban). Reagan admitted it March 1987 ("I did not trade arms for hostages… the facts tell me it is not [true]"). Angola was quietly entangled: a Casey scheme used **captured Soviet weapons** (via a China/Israel laundering chain) to arm "freedom fighters worldwide" without State or Congress; the New York Post reported Iran-sale profits bought Chinese arms for the Afghan and Angolan rebels; **Oliver North** claimed to be "a veteran of two wars, Vietnam and Angola." But Congress "never asked a single question" about Angola. One irony: Hasenfus's CIA front, **Southern Air Transport**, was simultaneously contracted by the *MPLA* government (65% of the airline's income) until Luanda discovered its history — and the same airline later passed through **Les Wexner** and **Jeffrey Epstein**'s hands in 1990s Ohio. `[01:01:49]`

## The reckoning & toward Cuito `[01:12:00]`
- Iran-Contra forced Reagan to stop "playing Cold Warrior so aggressively": Casey died (brain tumor, May 1987); Weinberger and Poindexter were gone. **Oct 1986** — Congress passed South Africa **sanctions over Reagan's veto** (IBM, GM exiting). Talks with Angola resumed April 1987; even the Cubans thought a settlement had "ripened" (a weaker apartheid regime). But Gleijeses: Castro was wrong — "the South Africans were not ready to negotiate seriously. They still dreamed of bringing Savimbi to power." The next phase would be decided not in conference rooms but at a small town in southeastern Angola: **Cuito Cuanavale**. `[01:12:00]`
