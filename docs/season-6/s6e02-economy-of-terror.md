## Old Angola `[00:04:27]`
- Modern Angola (twice the size of France) was home to distinct kingdoms — the centralized **Kongo** (whose Manikongo welcomed the Portuguese in the late 15th c., adopting Christianity) and the **Ndongo** (whose ruler's title *Ngola* gives Angola its name; Queen Nzinga's resistance). The Portuguese "co-opted the rulers" and reduced the kingdoms to slave-trading partners (the 1574 charter to Paulo Dias de Novais — conquest "beneficial to the faith"). For 4–5 centuries it was "first and foremost an outpost of the slave trade," with little inland penetration; resistance included the prophetess **Dona Beatriz/Kimpa Vita** (burned as a heretic, 1706). `[00:04:27]`

## The modern colony `[00:10:31]`
- After Brazil's independence (1822), Portugal built "a new African Empire" — fixing Angola's frontiers by the 1920s, extending control to acquire cheap labor (coffee, sugar, rubber; railroads as "the long fingers of the empire"). Antonio Tomás: ~40% of all enslaved Africans passed through Portuguese hands. Portugal abolished slavery ~30 years behind Britain each step, and modified it into a **forced-labor** system — "fear and pain were the incentives… the economy of terror" (a Portuguese journalist's phrase). British journalist Henry Nevinson exposed it ("modern slavery"), triggering a Cadbury cocoa boycott (1909). The barbarity was also *less productive* than free-labor rivals (tech sometimes flowing "from blacks to whites"). `[00:10:31]`

## Diamonds and white settlers `[00:18:11]`
- **Diamang** got "the rights of a state within a state" — wired prisons to stop workers escaping, white technicians living in South-Africa-style segregation, product flowing to De Beers (Nkrumah's *Neo-Colonialism* dissected the Portuguese/Belgian shareholding and the forced-labor wages). The early-20th-c. governor **Norton de Matos** pushed **white settlement** (replacing mixed-race Creole elites with semi-literate white immigrants) — hard to attract (poor soil, indigenous-occupied land, burned plantations). The mid-1920s **native law** created a two-tier system of "**citizens**" vs. "**natives**" (natives needing to prove they were monogamous, Portuguese-speaking, fork-using, European-dressed) — restricting movement, imposing a poll tax and forced labor: "a variant of apartheid." `[00:18:11]`

## A brief history of apartheid South Africa `[00:24:33]`
- The Dutch **Boers/Afrikaners** (trekboers) fanned out, set up Boer republics, clashed with the British (the Boer Wars), and formed the **Union of South Africa** (Afrikaners retaining power). They built "a kind of Jim Crow nation" — blacks as cheap labor, the **Native Lands Act** (blacks restricted to 7% of land), the pass system, violence against resisters like the **ANC**. (Germany's neighboring colony became **Namibia**.) `[00:24:33]`

## Salazar's Estado Novo `[00:28:36]`
- A 1920s coup brought finance minister **Salazar** to power (president by 1932) — "professorial, voraciously Catholic and anti-communist," backed by landowners, the military, bishops, and Lisbon/Porto finance (linked to Britain). He suppressed unions and opposition, legislated patriarchal Catholic values, and made **empire fundamental** (the 1930 Colonial Act centralizing control). Angola's economy was brought "into line with the belt-tightening at home"; segregation deepened (whiteness "materially beneficial however poor your skill"; white men fathering then abandoning mixed-race children). `[00:28:36]`
- **WWII and the Azores card** `[00:33:00]`: neutral Portugal traded tungsten to Germany for gold, and leased the **Azores** airbase to the Allies — which got Portugal into **NATO (1949)**, with a secret clause allowing US aid in Africa (a card it would play against the US's "anti-colonial complex"). Only Albania was poorer in Europe; 300,000 Angolans still lived "under conditions of near slavery," with no real education system. `[00:33:00]`

## Apartheid hardens; America's "reluctant uncle" `[00:36:30]`
- **1948** — South Africa's **National Party** won on the slogan **apartheid**, building the machinery (Population Registration Act, Mixed Marriages/Immorality Acts, the **Bantustans**/homelands as slums). Where did the US stand? Tom Borstelmann's "**apartheid's reluctant uncle**": Cold War anti-communism made Pretoria and the colonial powers "precious in the eyes of American policymakers" (the Korean War cementing the US–South Africa tie). A 1950 State memo: "We need more from South Africa than she does from us." `[00:36:30]`

## Coffee, oil, and the boom `[00:43:36]`
- Coffee (and cocoa) was "a godsend" — production rising to 200,000 tons/year, accelerating the transfer of indigenous plots to white planters and importing resented **Ovimbundu** labor from the south. The Portuguese oil company **SACOR** discovered oil in the 1950s (the **Cabinda** enclave — using Texas capital). To outward appearances the colonies were "tranquil backwaters" enjoying a boom; Luanda became starkly segregated (Africans pushed into the **musseques**, the informal edge neighborhoods). "The only problem then was that Angolans themselves were at that very moment mobilizing to liberate their country." `[00:43:36]`

## The three nationalisms `[00:46:40]`
- The leaders gathered abroad (chiefly Lisbon): Agostinho Neto, Lúcio Lara, Holden Roberto, Jonas Savimbi — and **Deolinda Rodrigues** (US- and Brazil-educated, who organized the women's section and died in Congolese prison). The three groups:
  - **The FNLA / Holden Roberto group** `[00:48:28]` — the strongest early on, a northern (Kongo) supergroup tied to the Congo's Léopoldville; Roberto an "all-in on armed struggle" *anti-communist* who took CIA money from 1955 ($6,000/month), pitched at the UN (1959). His pro-free-enterprise ethos fit northern coffee-farming Angolans.
  - **The MPLA** `[00:53:00]` — the **People's Movement for the Liberation of Angola**, rising from Luanda's Mbundu hinterland, incorporating leftists and the Angolan communists (with Portuguese Communist Party links). Driven into exile (Paris, then Conakry) after secret-police arrests. Led by **Agostinho Neto** (Methodist minister's son, doctor-poet, married to a white woman — fodder for Roberto's anti-race-mixing propaganda; jailed by Salazar, Amnesty International's first "prisoner of conscience"). By the late 50s it was "brittle… debilitated by internal power struggles."
  - **Jonas Savimbi** `[00:55:00]` — an Ovimbundu Christian, scholarship student in Portugal/Switzerland, mixing with the MPLA but courted by Roberto — "uncommitted… weighing his options, something he would do for the rest of his life."

## On the brink `[00:55:50]`
- By the late 1950s Luanda's "sand slums" felt at "undeclared war" with the European city; the colonial government answered protests with arrests, air raids, paratroopers ("a somewhat overdone reply to leaflets"). The **Belgian Congo's** chaotic independence (Lumumba seized by Mobutu) became "a boogeyman" for the colonists. Salazar (late 1960): "we have been in Africa for 400 years… not the same as to abandon human destiny to the so-called winds of history" — but "the winds of history would prove more powerful than anyone thought." `[00:55:50]`
