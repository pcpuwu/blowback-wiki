## Cold open: Cuba's missions `[00:00:00]`
- **Oct 1962** — amid the Missile Crisis, JFK toasted Algeria's **Ben Bella**, who then flew to Cuba; the visit birthed Cuba's **medical missions abroad** (doctors to Algeria, then military aid). Cuba backed African struggles (Zanzibar, Guinea-Bissau, the Congo where Che fought in 1965) out of "self-defense and revolutionary fervor" — never (per US intelligence) "at the behest of the Soviet Union." Ben Bella: "given the choice to bend or to stand firm, they, like us, have chosen to stand firm." `[00:00:00]`

## The Year of Africa `[00:07:14]`
- **1960** — 17 states gained independence. The examples of the **Congo** (Lumumba, killed after a CIA-backed coup) and **Algeria** terrified the colonial masters; the **Sharpeville** massacre kickstarted ANC underground resistance. All of it inspired Angola's black majority, "left out of any progress." `[00:07:14]`

## The 1961 uprisings `[00:11:50]`
- **Jan 1961** — wage-cut protests in the cotton/coffee north met with arrests and air bombing. **Feb 4** — a crowd attacked Luanda's prison to free political prisoners; white settlers, "armed by the government," massacred Africans (thousands killed). **March 15** — Holden Roberto's group organized a northern uprising: Bakongo killed several hundred whites (and resented Ovimbundu migrants and *assimilados*) with machetes; Portugal answered with a six-month terror campaign (~50,000 Africans killed, 300,000 refugees to Zaire). An army officer in the Daily Mirror: "I estimate that we've killed 30,000 of these animals." The MPLA "had neither the capacity nor the desire" to lead it — benefiting Roberto (Fanon: "Holden is ready to begin, and they are not"). `[00:11:50]`

## America picks Portugal `[00:21:26]`
- The UN condemned Portugal; JFK broke a Cold War pattern by joining the USSR's Security Council condemnation. But Salazar threatened to close the **Azores** NATO base (used heavily in the 1961 Berlin airlift) — so Kennedy supplied Salazar's air force with **napalm** to bomb Angolan villages (like America's own emerging Vietnam tactics). The USSR, meanwhile, sent its first MPLA aid (1961), favoring the MPLA ideologically but pushing for an MPLA-Roberto coalition. Portugal sent a "gigantic conscript army" (70,000 in Angola by 1974); India seized **Goa** (Dec 1961). `[00:21:26]`

## Cuba enters Africa `[00:37:01]`
- Cuba's 1960s internationalism (a third of Cubans of African descent): doctors to Algeria, Che to the Congo, the largest sub-Saharan station in **Dar es Salaam** ("the Mecca of African liberation movements"); the 1966 **Tricontinental Conference**. The Cuban-Angolan relationship started rocky — a Cuban-advised MPLA attack on **Cabinda** (Christmas 1965) failed; by 1967 Cuba pulled back from Africa (Che killed in Bolivia). Gleijeses: through 1967–74 Cuba kept "friendly contacts" and gave diplomatic aid, but "no one could have predicted that in a few years, Cuban combat troops would be deployed by the tens of thousands." `[00:37:01]`

## The grinding war `[00:42:42]`
- By the late 60s Portugal had 182,000+ troops (the highest world percentage under arms). British Baptist missionaries documented village bombings and massacres; a Portuguese defense minister told troops they fought "not human beings, but savages and wild beasts." The Portuguese forcibly resettled Angolans (Vietnam-style) under PIDE secret-police surveillance (informants at music clubs, soccer matches). But the **MPLA** found surprising success in the **east** (downing aircraft, ambushing patrols), and by 1971 the Portuguese judged it "the most effective nationalist party" (a South African journalist: MPLA men "ask villagers for food… rarely touch the women," unlike the "grab-all FNLA"). `[00:42:42]`

## Savimbi collaborates with Portugal `[00:49:34]`
- Facing the dual MPLA/FNLA threat, the Portuguese teamed up with the odd man out — **Jonas Savimbi** (who'd quit Roberto's group in 1964 accusing him of being a US creation, and founded **UNITA** in 1966). From ~1972 Savimbi was a Portuguese agent (revealed later by *Afrique-Asie*): the Portuguese left UNITA alone in return for it attacking the MPLA and FNLA ("we have done everything we could to weaken the forces of the common enemy"). "He enjoyed the best of two worlds — helping to destroy his rivals while safeguarding his own weak forces." `[00:49:34]`

## The FNLA rots; the MPLA's patrons `[00:52:10]`
- Roberto became "completely bourgeois-fied" (a Kinshasa businessman, half-brother to **Mobutu**, owning buildings, attacking MPLA detachments). The MPLA, near "practically dead" after 1961, was a predominantly *urban* movement (uneasy in the bush) revived by **Agostinho Neto** (arriving 1962, the "Neto Trail" of weapons from Tanzania) — but restarting its chronic internal conflicts. Rejected again by Roberto, the MPLA shifted to the Republic of the Congo, gaining a foothold near **Cabinda**. Its arms came first from **Czechoslovakia**, then cautiously from the USSR (which redirected toward building African militaries after the "coup contagion"), and from **China** (the Sino-Soviet contest — Beijing backing Roberto/Savimbi against Moscow's MPLA), plus Nordic humanitarian aid. `[00:52:10]`

## South Africa & Namibia `[00:57:01]`
- A Portuguese defeat would be "a severe blow to White South Africa's developing plan… to expand its apartheid system" — it could tolerate any Angola but "one governed by a genuinely independent national movement." **Namibia** (a former German colony — site of the 1900s genocide whose colonial uniforms became the Nazi brownshirts) was under brutal South African occupation; the **SWAPO** insurgency (from 1966) fought both South Africa and the Portuguese (crossing southeast Angola). The 1971 ICJ ruling (South African presence unlawful) and a major 1971–72 strike radicalized SWAPO — fating a "long and fruitful friendship" between the MPLA and SWAPO (South Africa and Portugal cooperating via the **Alcora** agreement to hit Namibian nationalists in Angolan territory). `[00:57:01]`

## Salazar's strange end & Portugal digs in `[00:58:39]`
- **1968** — Salazar's stroke; replaced by **Caetano** — but Salazar *woke up*, and the entire leadership kept up a two-year **charade** that he still ruled (denied newspapers, "nodding to his directives") until his death in 1970, "without knowing that he had been replaced." Nixon and Kissinger backed Portugal (cutting Roberto's aid as a gesture to Lisbon, while keeping Mobutu and favoring white minority rule). A secret 1969 Kissinger NSC report: "the rebels cannot oust the Portuguese, and the Portuguese can contain, but not eliminate the rebels." `[00:58:39]`

## The MPLA's crisis `[01:09:50]`
- The 1970 Portuguese offensives devastated the MPLA (the army dropping industrial chemicals on crops); in "one of the darkest chapters," MPLA troops enforced **witchcraft** accusations and treason executions in the east. The Soviets criticized the MPLA as too aloof from the black population, and the **Eastern Revolt** (Daniel Chipenda breaking away) became a major internal crisis — the Soviets *suspended assistance* in 1973 to pressure Neto, then warned him Chipenda was plotting to assassinate him ("possibly a fabrication… keeping their options open"). The MPLA was "once again in danger of collapsing," losing support, with Savimbi gaining and its Soviet patron frozen. "But four thousand miles away, there was a conspiracy brewing, one that would change everything." `[01:09:50]`
