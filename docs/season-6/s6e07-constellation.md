# Blowback S6E7 — "Constellation"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s6e07-constellation.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:02:30
> **Thesis:** The early Reagan years, **1981–84** — the new administration formalizes Carter's "bleeder" line as Chester Crocker's **"constructive engagement"/linkage** (Cuban exit ⇄ Namibian independence), which South Africa accepts as a sham cover for its doctrine of a regional **"constellation"** of friendly anti-communist states. Savimbi's UNITA, propped by Pretoria, the Safari Club, Israel and Saudi/Moroccan money, wins set-piece victories (Mavinga) while South Africa invades repeatedly (Protea, Askari) — and the episode ends on the depravity behind UNITA's myth: ivory smuggling, hostage ransoms, and Savimbi's **"Red September"** witch-burnings.

## Cold open & roadmap `[00:00:00]`
- UN ambassador **Jeane Kirkpatrick** dodges whether apartheid South Africa is "authoritarian" or "totalitarian" ("a real political science question"). Roadmap: a new Reagan policy to ease Cubans out of Angola and South Africa out of Namibia — an intensification of Carter's bleeder line, flowing support to anti-communist "freedom fighters" "no matter what horrors these cold warriors might have to their names." `[00:00:00]`

## Reagan arrives — and the October Surprise `[00:03:42]`
- Reagan took office (Jan 1981) amid recession (8.8% unemployment by year's end) — the economy his first-term obsession. His first foreign "victory" was the **October Surprise**: campaign manager **William Casey** had secretly negotiated to delay the Tehran hostage release until after the election (Iran getting weapons via Israel) — echoing Nixon's 1968 Vietnam sabotage, with some of the same advisors (Dick Allen). Once a "conspiracy theory," now acknowledged even by the NYT. Casey became CIA director; Allen, National Security Advisor. Central America was the priority; Southern Africa less so — but the Savimbi lobby had extracted a 1980 campaign commitment from Reagan to arm UNITA. `[00:03:42]`

## The linkage box `[00:06:58]`
- Secretary of State **Alexander Haig** signaled the intent at his confirmation ("in Angola, UNITA elements are still going strong"); Savimbi told *The New Republic* (via neocon Michael Ledeen) he'd nearly quit had Carter won. But the **Clark Amendment** still barred official aid to UNITA (Congress had just reaffirmed it). A March 1981 secret Haig memo described the "box": curtail Soviet/Cuban influence while not rupturing relations with allies and African states — solution: nominal support for Namibian independence *plus* strengthening Savimbi "through public support and indirect covert help… to harass the Cubans." `[00:06:58]`

## Constructive engagement `[00:11:22]`
- **Chester Crocker** (Assistant Secretary for African Affairs) brought "constructive engagement"/**linkage**: pressure South Africa "from inside the house" rather than isolating it, treating Pretoria as a "legitimate participant." Right-wingers distrusted him as too dovish, but Crocker framed it as cold-blooded dealer strategy that "spelled problems for the MPLA and the Cubans for years to come" — undermining "Moscow's heavily militarized African diplomacy." Notably, the Angolans had *already* offered the linkage logic (a free Namibia = a real buffer; NYT, three days after Reagan's inauguration: "Angolan aide links Cubans' exit to a free Namibia"). South Africa, sold on linkage by Crocker and **Elliott Abrams**, signaled it was a sham: a free Namibia would mean a SWAPO victory ("you cannot have a SWAPO president of Namibia without a red flag" — Pik Botha). Haig privately agreed it was a fig leaf ("no intention of allowing the hammer and sickle to fly over Windhoek"). No sanctions; renewed effort to repeal Clark; US protection of Pretoria at the UN. Gleijeses: "linkage was a boon for South Africa." `[00:11:22]`

## Angola's economic crisis `[00:18:15]`
- The war disrupted the Benguela railway and governance; the global downturn (coffee prices halved, oil and diamond revenues down) caused a foreign-exchange crisis. **Dos Santos** chose harsh **austerity** (shops "literally empty for almost three months") over an IMF loan, to protect domestic industry — above all **oil**. Ironically, the American oil firms (Gulf Oil in Cabinda) became "some of the strongest advocates for peace in Angola," and the US Export-Import Bank granted Angola **$85M** in 1981 to develop oil fields — even as Washington withheld diplomatic recognition and backed Angola's enemies. (The Economist noted US executives meeting the ANC's Oliver Tambo to hedge.) `[00:18:15]`

## Mavinga & Operation Protea `[00:23:50]`
- Late 1980 — UNITA took **Mavinga**; **May 1981** — it repulsed the MPLA's attempt to retake it ("UNITA's first successful defense of a town"). Western reporters (Bridgland, the Post's Dick Harwood) were invited to publicize the victory (a UNITA "morality play" caricatured Brezhnev, Castro, and Neto). Savimbi claimed "South Africa provided no weapons" — a lie; Pretoria gave "massive logistical support," already flying supplies through the Mavinga airstrip. **Aug 23–24, 1981** — South Africa launched **Operation Protea** (4,000–5,000 troops, "the biggest mechanized operation by the South African army since WWII"), nominally against SWAPO but venting fury on the MPLA. Western capitals condemned it — "but not Washington," which **vetoed** the UN condemnation. Emboldened, South Africa launched Operations **Daisy, Super, and Mibos** (Nov 1981–summer 1982), occupying much of Kunene province (killing livestock, poisoning wells, denying food to alienate locals from Luanda/SWAPO; arming UNITA). `[00:23:50]`

## Savimbi's December 1981 DC trip `[00:38:00]`
- After Mavinga "put the Angola War back on the international journalism map," Savimbi returned to DC (Dec 1981, via AEI/Freedom House/American Security Council) amid another Clark-repeal push. He played all sides (anti-communism to hawks, reconciliation to critics like Wolpe), but only the right-wing press took him seriously; the State Department still said it was considering recognizing the MPLA. The repeal failed (Black Caucus + House Democrats), but clandestine third-party aid was already flowing ("ways and means would be found to bypass the Clark Amendment"). `[00:38:00]`

## Cuba re-escalates `[00:35:00]`
- Pressured by South Africa/UNITA gains, Luanda asked Havana for a *closer* relationship and more battlefield help. Cuba agreed — for the first time committing 2,700+ troops to counter-guerrilla (anti-UNITA) operations; by early 1983 total Cuban presence reached **30,000**. A 1982 joint statement tepidly endorsed linkage (Cubans leaving per Resolution 435). Lúcio Lara had said the Cubans could leave once Namibia was free — "just describing reality," not endorsing linkage. But Crocker demanded *simultaneous* (not staged) withdrawal; dos Santos flatly rejected it. Angola-South Africa talks in Cape Verde were "a dialogue of the deaf." `[00:38:00]`

## The "constellation" `[00:40:00]`
- South Africa's generals wanted Angola as "the centerpiece of the constellation of southern African states" — anti-communist black governments tolerant of apartheid and hostile to the ANC and SWAPO. They didn't believe in linkage: a Cuban exit was fine (more room for Savimbi), but they would never tolerate a SWAPO-led Namibia. Crocker and Eagleburger, who'd "broken some diplomatic china" with South Africa in 1981, shifted to "carrots, not sticks" by late 1982 — while Pretoria, considering the Americans "fair-weather friends," hid the full scope of its war plans. `[00:40:00]`

## Kangamba & the Cuban surge `[00:41:37]`
- Early 1983 — UNITA took **Cangonga**, Bridgland documenting the systematic demolition (the Benguela railway, water tanks, "liberated" goats and chickens) and the forced resettlement of ~600 civilians (200 children) for "re-education." (Commander "Ben Ben" laughed at Pik Botha's "absurd duplicity" — secretly aiding three anti-Soviet movements: "we're not even having to ask them.") **July 1983** — at **Kangamba** (818 Angolans + 92 Cuban advisors), ~6,000 UNITA attacked; Cuban air support held them off for a week, but the town had no air defenses, so Castro ordered the Cubans to leave ("we won a great battle, but now we must leave"); UNITA returned with South African airstrikes and took it. Castro then *surged*: the Cuban contingent rose from 30,000 to **39,000** in the year after Kangamba, with secret tank/infantry shipments — and Castro told dos Santos Cuba would "not charge for our technical assistance." **Dec 1983** — South Africa's **Operation Askari** (Soviet-Cuban command friction causing MPLA losses) made real gains but met fierce resistance, prompting a South African withdrawal offer. `[00:41:37]`

## Lusaka & the new precondition `[00:47:38]`
- Jan–Feb 1984 — the US-brokered **Lusaka Agreement** (South African withdrawal ⇄ Angola barring SWAPO/Cuban forces from the vacated area), followed by the **Nkomati Accord** (Mozambique barring the ANC) — "a bad omen for the anti-apartheid struggle." Dos Santos flew to Havana (Castro distressed the Angolans signed without consulting Cuba, but affirmed support + free medical aid). A new precondition appeared in the March 19, 1984 communiqué: **the end of all US/South African aid to UNITA** as a precondition for any Cuban withdrawal. `[00:47:38]`

## The money: ivory, hostages, smuggling `[00:50:12]`
- Reagan's White House "activated the Safari Club": Saudi Arabia funded Morocco $50–70M/year to train UNITA; **Israel** supplied arms and trained UNITA in Namibia/Zaire — all alongside South Africa. But Savimbi needed cash (overseas offices, watches for cronies, Swiss bank accounts) — so UNITA smuggled, bartered, and looted (foreshadowing the Khmer Rouge insurgency): clothing/salt traded for villagers' ivory and leopard/python skins (seven garments per elephant tusk), while at Jamba Western reporters slept on ironed sheets and drank beer cans reading "keep South Africa tidy." Savimbi seized **66 Czechoslovak** factory workers (marched 800 miles, held a year, ransom demanded — released when Prague threatened to brand UNITA "a pack of bandits"). His most lucrative trade was **ivory** smuggling with South African military intelligence — Jan Breytenbach (SA Special Forces founder) recounted a captain finding "hundreds of boxes of ivory" in an ammo store and being told to "shut up." `[00:50:12]`

## Red September `[00:57:22]`
- **Sept 7, 1983 — "Red September":** Savimbi summoned all of Jamba to the parade ground, accused "witches" of UNITA's battlefield setbacks, had blindfolded men shot, then forced the crowd (children included) to build a bonfire and **burned women and children alive** ("a snake's offspring is also a snake"; he forced one escapee back into the fire with his ivory-handled pistol) — at least 13 killed, others shot, hacked, or made to wear the ashes of the burned. A survivor: "Jonas Savimbi led his followers into evil and antisocial psychopathic behavior… an atmosphere of fear and paranoia." Historian Justin Pearce: loyalists like senior officer **Samuel Chiwale** (who saw his own aunt burned as a witch and his wife forcibly remarried) "struggled to process this extreme cruelty" from the leader they admired. `[00:57:22]`
