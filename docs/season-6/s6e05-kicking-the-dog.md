## Cold open: the mail-order mercenary `[00:00:00]`
- **Feb 1976** — **Danny Gerhardt**, a 34-year-old Maryland cafeteria mechanic and Vietnam vet "with a paunch" and Walter-Mitty dreams, answered a *Soldier of Fortune* ad and flew to Angola to fight for the FNLA. Captured by Cuban troops within days; the MPLA put the mercenaries on **trial** in Luanda; convicted June 1976, **executed by firing squad** in July (NBC cameras caught his wife learning of the death sentence from the radio). The CIA denied recruiting mercenaries (illegal under the Neutrality Act) — but Stockwell wrote the agency's accounting was set up for plausible deniability, and Bay-of-Pigs veterans + Watergate burglar **Frank Sturgis** were openly recruiting Americans. Stockwell on the mercenary "Callan": "a raving psychopath." `[00:00:00]`

## Recap `[00:06:05]`
- The Carnation Revolution; the three factions; Nov 11, 1975 independence; the Cuban "ace in the hole" repelling South Africa's invasion. By early 1976 the MPLA was the de facto government; South Africa had retreated — "a stunning defeat of a white army by black African forces." But the war was far from over. `[00:06:05]`

## The Washington scandal `[00:09:44]`
- The defeat wasn't just military: the CIA's $30M had been "dwarfed by a Russian grant of $400 million." Worse, **Colby** had lied to Congress across **35 briefings** (Jan 1975–Jan 1976) — claiming "there are no Americans involved" — under rules that gagged the briefed members. Iowa Senator **Dick Clark** broke it open in Dec 1975 with a trick: grilling a junior CIA man (who admitted weapons went "directly" to Angola) before the senior one arrived to deny it. Combined with **Seymour Hersh**'s NYT scoops (Nat Davis's resignation; years of US involvement; coordination with apartheid South Africa), it became a full scandal. **Dec 20** — the Senate passed the **Tunney Amendment** (paired the next year with the stronger **Clark Amendment**) restricting US action in Angola. The **Church** and **Pike** Committees were investigating the spy agencies — but the House voted 2:1 to suppress the Pike report, and Ford's Jan 1976 State of the Union defended the agencies and shouted out Angola. `[00:09:44]`

## Angola as a campaign weapon `[00:20:04]`
- **Carter** ran as a moral "breath of fresh air"; advisor **Andrew Young** (ex-MLK aide) framed Southern Africa through a civil-rights/religious lens. But **Reagan** made Angola a GOP-primary cudgel against Ford and Kissinger — "the peace they have come to know is the peace of the grave" — blasting them for abandoning the white regimes of Rhodesia and South Africa and for Kissinger's "Athens to their Sparta" defeatism. Reagan's ads: "we gave just enough support to one side to encourage it to fight and die." Reagan overreached on committing US troops to Rhodesia ("Reagan might use U.S. troops in Africa") and narrowly lost the Aug 1976 nomination — but the revolution was "deferred, not defeated." And Carter, despite Young, kept Cold Warrior **Brzezinski** at his side — "a cold warrior from day one." `[00:20:04]`

## Cuba digs in; the Neto–Castro tension `[00:32:36]`
- **March 27, 1976** — the last South African troops left. But the MPLA was weak alone: 3,000 Cubans by January had become **36,000 by early April** (Gleijeses). The USSR, now confident, committed $25M, "officially launching the long-term Soviet-Cuban cooperation." Castro agreed to a phased withdrawal (all troops out by end of 1978) — but a tension emerged: Moscow wanted an Angolan army to repel *foreign* invaders; Cuba wanted it to fight the *internal* insurgency (Savimbi's UNITA) while Cubans handled South Africa. Castro to Neto (March 1977): "what you need to do now is focus on **liquidating the bandits**" (UNITA) — "almost an obsession." `[00:32:36]`

## Carter's shallow consensus `[00:36:36]`
- Carter took office "determined to reestablish the prestige of the United States in Africa." Capitalist logic favored recognizing the MPLA (Gulf Oil pumped in Cabinda, Boeing sold planes — "Communist Cuban troops stationed to protect Gulf oil"). Vance (the "dealers") and Young pushed toward recognizing Angola and normalizing with Cuba and Vietnam. But Brzezinski (the "bleeders") wanted little wars to bleed Moscow. Direct US-Angola talks were planned for April 1977 — then came **Shaba**. `[00:36:36]`

## Soweto & South Africa's undeclared war `[00:39:42]`
- 1976 was bruising for Pretoria: defeat in Angola, plus the **Soweto uprising** (June — schoolchildren protesting mandatory Afrikaans instruction, "put down with great harshness," hundreds killed, worldwide news). Kissinger met Vorster about ending white Rhodesia (an LA Times cartoon: Vorster's bloody hand, "Dr. Kissinger, I presume"). The SADF, though its regulars had withdrawn, waged an "undeclared war" on Angola — building up black soldiers and a kidnapped force for UNITA. Historian Justin Pearce: UNITA conscripted teachers and pastors who "weren't going to be able to go home," and children — "when I was 10 years old, I became separated from my family, and I ended up with UNITA." Savimbi led UNITA on a Mao-style **Long March** (1976) — "really just a question of marketing," one participant said. His Dec 1976 NYT op-ed begging for US support fell on deaf ears. `[00:39:42]`

## Shaba I `[00:48:43]`
- **March 8, 1977** — 1,500 Katangan exiles (anti-Mobutu, living in Angola) invaded Zaire's **Shaba** province. Neto had restrained them, but "the raids from Zaire did not stop," so he "unleashed the Katangans" in retaliation for Mobutu's backing of anti-MPLA insurgents. Mobutu got $2M from the US in a week (then $13M more); France airlifted Moroccan troops. **There was no evidence Cuba was involved** (Cuba was surprised and critical) — Carter publicly admitted as much — but Shaba "poisoned the well," ending normalization hopes. Cuba, though it disagreed, agreed to slow its withdrawal: "we saw that a new threat had arisen." `[00:48:43]`

## The Nito Alves coup `[00:52:46]`
- **May 1977** — a coup attempt against Neto by MPLA rival **Nito Alves** (a former Maoist turned "diehard partisan of the Soviet Union" after a 1976 Moscow trip). The **Cubans again saved Neto**, retaking government buildings. The crackdown that followed was "one of the most violent and disgraceful moments of Angola's revolution" — thousands imprisoned and killed as real or imagined Alves collaborators. Neto was convinced Moscow had sponsored it; Gleijeses calls the Soviet role "murky" (some officials "probably knew of the plot and welcomed it," distrusting Neto "to the point of paranoia") — relations were never the same. `[00:52:46]`

## Ethiopia & the Ogaden `[00:55:31]`
- Summer 1977 — **Somalia** invaded Ethiopia's **Ogaden** (a US-nodded attack). Ethiopia's **Derg** (Mengistu) requested Cuban troops; impressed by Angola, Castro — this time with Moscow — sent ~12,000 to repel the invasion. Carter, months from seeking Cuba normalization, now faced "two cases of militant third-world internationalism." Brzezinski: the arms treaty "lies buried in the sands of the Ogaden." But the Cubans were acting within international law (helping repel an invasion, refusing to fight Ethiopia's own Marxist insurgencies). Gleijeses: what died in the Ogaden "was the delusion of a one-sided detente" — enemies of the US having no right to send troops anywhere, friends always doing so. Even VP Mondale conceded "the Cubans and Soviets are fighting an aggressor." `[00:55:31]`

## Shaba II & Brzezinski's trap `[01:00:09]`
- After Shaba I, Mobutu's army "unleashed a wave of terror" (looting, rape, massacres driving tens of thousands into Angola) — but the West stayed quiet because Mobutu was opening Zaire to foreign capital (a $200M bank loan, 1978). **May 13, 1978** — **Shaba II**: better-organized Katangans took the mining city **Kolwezi**; Mobutu's army collapsed; French/Belgian forces (on Carter-supplied C-141s) retook it within weeks — exactly as a Cuban memo (via Jorge Risquet) had warned Neto. Neto had assured the Cubans he opposed it, then **deceived them**. Cuba told the US it had no involvement; Vance accepted Cuba's version — then, hours later, the White House had the State Department announce Cuba had "trained and equipped" the Katangans. Carter called the Cubans liars; Brzezinski said they were "indispensable" (though many officials privately disagreed). Shaba II solidified **Brzezinski's trap**: "suppose we start helping Savimbi… why not make them increase their involvement in Angola? Let them be pinched by it." Donald McHenry later admitted the US got around the Clark Amendment by funneling aid via the Saudis/Moroccans — "Angola was the pilot program" for the Afghan strategy. `[01:00:09]`

## The Kassinga massacre `[01:11:23]`
- Shaba II blotted out another atrocity a week earlier and 800 miles south: **May 4, 1978** — South Africa bombed a refugee camp at **Kassinga** in southern Angola (1,200 anti-personnel bombs, 20,000 lbs of high explosive, strafing, then paratroopers), claiming it was a secret SWAPO base. Cuban fighters pushed through the bombing (16+ killed, ~100 casualties) and reinforcements drove the South Africans into retreat. Over **600 Namibians killed**; the UNHCR and WHO later verified it was indeed a refugee camp (the London Times: a mass grave with "the brightly colored dresses of a large number of women among the dead"). Carter: "we hope it's just a transient strike… and we hope it's all over." Cuba's Risquet: South Africa was never made to pay for Kassinga — "this means they are free to repeat this again and again." `[01:11:23]`
