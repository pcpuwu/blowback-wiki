> **Thesis:** The long, brutal aftermath, **1991–present** — the Cold War leaves but the war doesn't. Savimbi loses the 1992 election (certified free and fair) and restarts the bloodiest phase yet, a war of cities funded by diamonds, mercenaries (Executive Outcomes), and post-Soviet arms-for-hire. The US recognizes the MPLA (1993), Mandela's South Africa frees Namibia and abandons Savimbi, and after losing his Zaire base (Mobutu falls 1997) Savimbi is finally hunted down — with Israeli surveillance help — and **killed Feb 22, 2002**, ending Africa's longest civil war (~500,000–1 million dead). Angola today: dos Santos's crony-capitalist dynasty, a US-China rivalry over the Lobito Corridor, and a peace still waiting on a social agenda.

## Cold open & roadmap `[00:00:00]`
- A lame-duck **Biden** in Angola: "the future runs through Africa." Recap: the New York Accords freed Namibia and sent the Cubans home, but the *internal* war wasn't over — Savimbi refused to concede even after South Africa deserted him, still getting covert US aid, while being pressured into multiparty elections. This finale: the false dawn of 1992, the renewed war, Savimbi's death, and Angola today. `[00:00:00]`

## A ruined country & a fake peace `[00:05:00]`
- Birmingham: by 1991 a top Angolan export was **scrap metal** (from half a million tons of blown-up vehicles) — "the graveyard of military vehicles matched by the graveyard of human victims." UNITA moved to Luanda; refugees headed home. But the **Bicesse Accords** were "half-heartedly implemented." The US ran a "Janus-faced" policy — funding the electoral commission via NED/USAID while still refusing to recognize the MPLA and still aiding Savimbi. Savimbi (now in a collarless suit and gold rings, "putting the country up for auction" to De Beers and others) violated the ceasefire (killing tourists and voter registrars, hiding 20,000 guerrillas and stockpiling US Stingers), while government troops demobilized into food-less assembly points. `[00:05:00]`

## The 1992 election `[00:10:30]`
- Held end of **September 1992** with UN/US/EU/Portuguese observers; ~90% turnout. The MPLA won parliament ~2:1; **dos Santos** led the presidential race ~10% but fell just short of 50% (necessitating a runoff vs. Savimbi's 40%). All monitors, including the US State Department, **certified the elections free and fair, no widespread fraud**. But Savimbi rejected the results on his Radio Vorgan as "fraud," his generals quit the integrated army, and he secretly flew to **Huambo** — "if he's gone to Huambo, it is a declaration of war." The NYT: "Savimbi, Trailing, Hints at New War." `[00:10:30]`

## The war of the cities `[00:13:13]`
- **Oct 30, 1992** — UNITA attacked the airport and police stations near Luanda. Birmingham: this war was *different* — fought over "whole cities," because UNITA had lost its bid in the urban heartland and "set out to destroy disloyal cities." In Luanda, house-to-house slaughter; MPLA-armed youths took revenge on suspected UNITA/Zairean supporters; UNITA took hostages in the embassy district. UNITA captured the Cuango diamond mines and, by 1993-94, up to **70% of the country**. The 55-day **siege of Huambo** "raised it to the ground" (~12,000 killed); by early 1993 ~10,000 dead and ~3 million displaced since the election. `[00:13:13]`

## The "forgotten war" `[00:19:53]`
- A humanitarian disaster "worse than any of the previous two decades" — UNITA fired on UN aid planes; a 1994 UN deal to feed besieged Cuito gave half the food to UNITA's army (prolonging the siege). Up to **100,000 died in a single year** (more from war and hunger "than any place else in the world"), yet it drew less attention than Somalia. Birmingham: Savimbi "craved power pathologically since his student days" and saw his only hope as "the barrel of a gun." `[00:19:53]`

## Washington shifts `[00:22:21]`
- The lame-duck Bush administration had done little to constrain Savimbi pre-election (Hank Cohen even faulted the MPLA's "winner-take-all attitude"). But Savimbi had gone too far — reneging on a US-backed peace deal, rejecting US-backed elections, attacking US-owned oil facilities, refusing all concessions. **May 1993** — the **Clinton** administration officially **recognized the Luanda government** after 15 years, as Angola became "the forgotten war" (GQ). `[00:22:21]`

## How Savimbi kept fighting `[00:24:57]`
- Cut off from the US, a post-apartheid South Africa, and the UN, Savimbi ran the new war on **diamonds, mercenaries, and post-Soviet arms-for-hire** (Justin Pearce): the MPLA hired the South African mercenary firm **Executive Outcomes** (ex-SADF men who knew UNITA from the apartheid wars); both sides used privatized Soviet Antonovs and pilots. UNITA funded itself via the **Cuango** diamond fields, smuggling stones out through Mobutu's Zaire to Belgium and Israel. But diamonds couldn't match the MPLA's oil money. The **Lusaka Protocol** (Nov 1994) "generated none of the euphoria" of 1991 (Savimbi skipped the signing); "neither war nor peace" lasted four years, his war chest reaching ~$2 billion (45 tons of Bulgarian weapons). `[00:24:57]`

## Mandela's South Africa abandons Savimbi `[00:29:00]`
- Botha resigned (stroke, 1989); **De Klerk** led real reform — unbanning the ANC, freeing **Mandela** (1990), Namibia's independence (March 1990). **1994** — South Africa's first free elections; **Mandela** president, now trying to broker Angolan peace — Savimbi "wanted none of it." `[00:29:00]`

## Mobutu's fall & the endgame `[00:30:54]`
- Savimbi's other prop was **Zaire**: the CIA's Kamina airfield (1986-91) had let Mobutu extract huge US aid, and he kept Savimbi's arms and smuggling routes open afterward. But **Mobutu fell in 1997** (dying of cancer in Morocco) — "a dramatic setback from which Savimbi could never recover," losing his rear base and diamond conduit. Savimbi rejected another peace deal (1998); the war re-intensified, UNITA downing two UN aid planes (the UN ended its $1.5B operation). Famine and **landmines** ravaged the country (an estimated 13 million mines maiming/killing 150 Angolans a week; one Angolan de-miner had trained in Cambodia; Princess Diana's 1997 visit had spotlighted it). Having lost "all but its most loyal supporters," Savimbi — "in a very similar situation to Pol Pot in the 1990s" — relied on thousands of **child soldiers**, hunted by ex-UNITA-commanders-turned-MPLA-generals. In his last days he requested a CD of Jimmy Cliff's "Many Rivers to Cross." `[00:30:54]`

## Savimbi's death `[00:38:20]`
- 1999 — driven from his headquarters; 2000 — UN sanctions choked UNITA's finances. An **Israeli diamond businessman** with IDF/Mossad contacts (whom Savimbi was cutting into) arranged for ex-IDF/Mossad officers to help the MPLA find him (Pearce: Israeli surveillance experts operated in Luena "right up until the day Savimbi died"). **Feb 22, 2002** — the Angolan army ambushed Savimbi in Moxico province; he was killed reaching for his pistol, "shot a total of 15 times." His body was put on display. **Two months later** UNITA signed a peace deal — and the civil war "really did come to an end." `[00:38:20]`

## The reckoning `[00:41:50]`
- Savimbi was widely recognized as "a butcher" (Kristof: "more than 500,000 deaths since 1975," possibly approaching **1 million**; ~4 million displaced, ~70,000 lost limbs, two-thirds without drinking water; sustained famine, 3 million on aid). His Washington fans (Jeane Kirkpatrick, Brad Phillips) still eulogized him as "extraordinarily educated… a guerrilla leader on our side." The **MPLA** was no liberation beacon either: dos Santos lived in luxury in a Cuban-built compound, its "commitment to socialism entirely bogus" — a front for "a cabal of wealthy interrelated families" enriching themselves via privatization, even running an ethno-nationalist campaign ("Cancer One"). Antonio Tomás still credits dos Santos as a pragmatic survivor who held the country together. `[00:41:50]`

## Angola today `[00:46:00]`
- The civil war ended 2002; **dos Santos** retired in 2018 after 38 years (an oil-price fall pushing him out), dying in 2022 in "comparatively modest circumstances" — but his daughter **Isabel dos Santos** (Africa's richest woman, ~$2B, now in Dubai under an Interpol red notice) is the dynasty's face. Successor **João Lourenço** has Angola at peace, courting both the US and China ("the world is a rainbow… not made of two colors"). **Biden's** final presidential trip was to Angola — honoring the slave-trade history and the **Lobito Corridor** (the Benguela Railway carrying green-transition minerals from Congo to the Atlantic), now "another front in the contest between a declining USA and a rising China." The show pushes back on the US "debt-trap" framing of Chinese investment (no wave of asset seizures; loans are negotiable; China rebuilt Angola's infrastructure "where the West had hung back"). Tomás's closing note: a better future depends on Angolans bringing a **social agenda** to the table — "otherwise it's going to be just extraction and exploitation." `[00:46:00]`
