# Blowback S6E6 — "Total Onslaught"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s6e06-total-onslaught.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:03:30
> **Thesis:** **1978–80** — Carter's Africa policy collapses as **Brzezinski** (the "bleeders") routs Vance and Young (the "dealers"); South Africa rejects UN Resolution 435 and embraces P.W. Botha's doctrine of **"Total Onslaught/Total Strategy,"** building Savimbi's pseudo-state at **Jamba**; the MPLA's revolution rots under oil corruption and war; founding father **Agostinho Neto dies** (Sept 1979, succeeded by dos Santos); and a string of phantom and real crises (the "Soviet brigade" in Cuba, the Vela flash, Mariel, the hostage rescue fiasco) sinks Carter and ushers in **Reagan** — to the delight of Savimbi, the Contras, and the mujahideen.

## Recap & roadmap `[00:00:23]`
- Recap: MPLA consolidation; the FNLA destroyed, UNITA near-destroyed; the end of the Watergate/Vietnam reckoning; Carter's peacemaker hopes turned cold-warrior by Shaba and the Ogaden; the white settler states resisting; Kassinga. This episode: Brzezinski's ascendance, Neto's death, South Africa's recommitment to regional war, and the returns of Reagan and Savimbi. `[00:00:23]`

## Carter's policy falls apart `[00:03:42]`
- Carter's first NSC meeting on Africa had aimed to pursue Rhodesia, Namibian independence, and ending apartheid simultaneously — "battered by reality" by mid-1978. A June delegation to Luanda found cross-purposes: the Angolans wanted diplomatic relations and protection from South Africa; the US wanted the Cubans gone but "offered no substitute." `[00:03:42]`

## Brzezinski wins `[00:06:02]`
- Hawk columnists (Evans & Novak) complained Brzezinski had been "ripped on Africa policy." On NBC's *Meet the Press* (late May 1978, returning from China), he declared the Soviets/Cubans shouldn't operate in Africa "cost-free" — and the Washington Post editorial board endorsed him. At the Oct 1978 NSC meeting (after a SAM downed Air Rhodesia Flight 825), Brzezinski "pounced": "we are not able to succeed unless we face up to the Soviet and Cuban problem," pushing **linkage** (arms-control concessions tied to African ones). He was already funneling aid to Savimbi via US allies — "not unlike what he did for the Mujahideen in Afghanistan." Dick Clark warned Carter "has made the decision to re-involve the United States in the Angolan Civil War" — but the 1978 midterms cost doves like Clark their seats; McGovern's Dec 1978 call to recognize Angola "no longer carried much water." `[00:06:02]`

## Namibia & Resolution 435 `[00:11:54]`
- US pressure on Neto was indirect pressure on SWAPO. Neto pressured SWAPO to accept Western plans for Namibian independence (it agreed in July 1978) — but **South Africa rejected UN Security Council Resolution 435** (Sept 1978) and held sham elections for a rump Namibian government (SWAPO boycotted). Neto told Castro the South Africans "are not willing to leave Namibia"; asked what SWAPO should do: "**Fight. I see no alternative to war.**" He was right (per SA archives): Pretoria saw Namibia as a path to dominate Angola. The new hardline PM **P.W. Botha** embodied **"Total Onslaught/Total Strategy"** (Jamie Miller) — an extremist us-vs-them doctrine entrenched until the Cold War's end: apartheid's "way or the highway." `[00:11:54]`

## The revolution rots `[00:17:23]`
- The civil war smothered the MPLA's egalitarian goals. The abrupt Portuguese exit left "few functioning institutions" (no civil service, currency, or integrated army, unlike most decolonized states). **Oil** "kept the several Angolan wars running for 27 years" — funding the war and food imports but corrupting leaders (Western firms greasing them; no national auditing). The MPLA became a privileged nomenklatura (Scotch, Belgian chocolate, meat rations; barter currency of eggs and lager). War disfigured the country: conscription/kidnapping of young men, **landmines** maiming women and children (Birmingham compares it to Cambodia's killing fields). In the north, returning exiles ("Zairotas") became an indispensable but resented private-trading class. `[00:17:23]`

## Savimbi & Jamba `[00:23:59]`
- US press knew little of Savimbi (Novak & Evans even called him "Lucas"). But Pretoria armed UNITA (Mercedes trucks, fuel, food across the border, Sept 1978), and by **March 1979** built **Jamba** — a "Potemkin village" in the desolate southeast corner (no water, no farming), chosen to be far from Luanda and near South African bases in Namibia, "Savimbi's headquarters for 13 years." His 1979 US publicity tour (snubbed by Carter, but meeting Kissinger, Scoop Jackson, Moynihan, Schlesinger, Haig via Freedom House/Social Democrats USA) won him the right-wing lobby ties (Commentary, WSJ, American Spectator) that mattered in the 1980s. He played the Cold War hits, citing the Dec 1979 Soviet invasion of Afghanistan: "we are one of the peoples who can [combat Russia] by unconventional means." Meanwhile UNITA was kidnapping "tens of thousands of youths" and "thousands of young girls" — atrocities the weak, isolated MPLA couldn't publicize, handing the CIA and Pretoria "a blank sheet." Savimbi's sanitized self-image (5:30am cabinet meetings, elephant hunts, reading Montgomery's autobiography) impressed Westerners; diplomat **Chas Freeman**'s theory: "he had cerebral malaria, which made him murderous" (Freeman bringing him books "as an insurance policy against being strangled"). `[00:23:59]`

## Cuban drawdown & Neto's death `[00:34:55]`
- Early 1979 — Neto in Havana; the Cuba-Angola relationship had stabilized ("the Cubans bent over backwards… even when provoked, they did not threaten to withdraw — this is rare in international relations"). They agreed to a *drawdown* (not withdrawal): aid workers fell from ~7,000 (early 1979) to <4,000 (late 1980), Western/Portuguese experts replacing them; from January 1979 Cuban troops stopped counterinsurgency against UNITA. But by autumn 1979 Neto had pancreatic cancer (adamant peace required South Africa out of Namibia and continued support for SWAPO). He **died Sept 1979** in Moscow after surgery. Lúcio Lara (best prepared but a "light-skinned mulatto" — distrusted) withdrew; the MPLA chose **José Eduardo dos Santos** (37, black, a compromise). Neto became a mythical hero (Harding: like Nkrumah, Cabral, Lumumba, Mandela). Gleijeses: the one principle the corrupting MPLA kept was Neto's — Angola as the **base for southern Africa's liberation movements** (SWAPO, ZAPU, the ANC) in an unspoken tripartite deal: Angola the land, Cuba the instructors, the USSR the weapons. `[00:34:55]`

## The phantom "Soviet brigade" `[00:39:38]`
- Carter's reverses (the Horn, the Shah's fall, Grenada, Nicaragua, the Cuban presence) all seemed to bear "the hand of Castro." The month Neto died, the CIA "discovered" a Soviet combat brigade in Cuba — a media bombshell echoing 1962. Even Frank Church demanded the brigade's removal. But **Vance was right**: it was an old, minimal force the Cubans had asked for after the missile crisis — the Ford administration had known; the records were lost. Carter begged Brezhnev to dismantle it as a face-saving favor; Brezhnev refused ("artificially created"). Too embarrassed to admit the blunder, Carter went on TV promising a Cuba crackdown — and the manufactured crisis helped doom the real **SALT II** treaty (the Afghanistan invasion finishing it, and "the Carter era itself"). `[00:39:38]`

## Vela, Mariel & Carter's collapse `[00:46:49]`
- **Sept 1979** — the **Vela incident** (a nuclear flash in the southern Indian Ocean): officials concluded **Israel and South Africa** had conducted a joint test — an embarrassment to the non-proliferation president. Then provocative SR-71 overflights of Cuba ("broke windows all over Cuba"). After 1979 Cuban-American visits flaunted US prosperity to a slumping island, a wave of hijackings and embassy occupations led Castro to open the port of **Mariel** (April 21, 1980) — **125,000+** left for the US (including people with criminal records). It "humiliated the Carter administration… it seemed Jimmy Carter could not even control America's border." Dos Santos, visiting Havana on Mariel's eve, told Castro: "please don't even think of withdrawing troops in the coming years" — as South Africa's "Total Onslaught" struck deep into Angola. Historian **Gerald Bender** testified to Congress (fall 1980) on the SADF's daily bombing (a hospital, schools, a church in Mungo), warning that if Reagan won, "we could be at war in that country again." `[00:46:49]`

## Reagan wins `[00:55:31]`
- Inflation (post-Iran oil spike), the malaise speech, the Iran hostage crisis, and the disastrous **Operation Eagle Claw** rescue (April 1980, 8 servicemen dead) finished Carter. Eagle Claw was "the last straw" for **Vance**, who resigned (he'd opposed it) — the second dealer's exit after **Andrew Young** was forced out (Aug 1979) over an unauthorized PLO meeting that the Mossad surfaced. With both gone by mid-1980, "Carter's dealer era was done — most notably in Afghanistan and Angola." **Reagan** won ~50% (lowest turnout since 1948; 43% of his voters "just voting against Carter"). What sounded "unhinged" in the 1976 primary now thrilled the base — and abroad, the winners were "the Contra death squads in Nicaragua… the Islamist drug kingpins in Afghanistan, and Angola's own battle-tested, unkillable anti-communist commander, Dr. Jonas Savimbi." `[00:55:31]`
