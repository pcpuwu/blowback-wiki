> **Thesis:** Nixon & Kissinger turn Cambodia into a battlefield (1969–70). The "madman theory," the all-powerful Kissinger NSC, the secret bombing (Operation Menu) and its dual-reporting cover-up, the opening to China against the USSR, "Vietnamization," the US-blessed **Lon Nol coup** against Sihanouk (March 1970), and the **invasion of Cambodia** (April 1970) — which destroyed Cambodia's neutrality, ignited its civil war, drove peasants to the Khmer Rouge, and detonated the protests (Kent State) at home. Saloth Sâr becomes Pol Pot.

## Cold open `[00:00:01]`
- Nixon on tape: "we cannot lose this war… we've got to use the maximum power of this country against this shit-ass little country to win the war." `[00:00:01]`

## Nixon the Orthogonian `[00:02:14]`
- Nixon's mandate was to end the war — but on his terms, by seizing power from the CIA "Ivy League liberals," the State Department "nerds," and the rest of the elite he despised. Perlstein's frame: at college Nixon split the world into the well-born "**Franklins**" and the striving "**Orthogonians**" — now the "Orthogonian-in-chief," with his "pet Franklin" **Kissinger** as the blunt instrument. `[00:02:14]`
- **The all-powerful NSC** `[00:05:20]`: Kissinger, made National Security Advisor, routed all decisions through committees he chaired (incl. the covert-action "**40 Committee**" that later destroyed Allende) — "no national security advisor possessed the far-reaching supervisory powers Kissinger claimed." Beloved by the Franklins (the press, academia) as "a civilizing influence on Nixon" — telling visiting Quakers, "give us six months." `[00:05:20]`

## Madman theory `[00:08:50]`
- Nixon to Haldeman: "I call it the madman theory… I want the North Vietnamese to believe I've reached the point where I might do anything." But the US no longer had the nuclear monopoly of the Korea era — so the threat became *expanding* the war into **Cambodia** (Short: perhaps the real aim was "to spread the war to Cambodia in order to divert attention from the withdrawal" — a "carcass thrown to distract the wolves"). Nixon asked about "quarantining" Cambodia on day one. `[00:08:50]`

## Détente and the opening to China `[00:11:21]`
- Toward the USSR: **détente** (Kissinger dangling SALT for Soviet pressure on Hanoi — Dobrynin refusing: the USSR "could not, under any circumstances, cut off aid"). The radical idea: induce **China** to press Hanoi — also isolating the USSR. Absurd on its face (1969–70 Chinese papers in "full-scale vitriol" against US "atrocities"), but Beijing was now *more hostile to its communist neighbor than to the US*: the **Sino-Soviet split** had flared into a March 1969 **border war at the Ussuri River**. By late 1969 secret US–China talks began (via Pakistan's Yahya Khan), hidden even from State — Nixon wanting "a surprise for the maximum political boost." `[00:11:21]`

## Haig and the secret bombing `[00:16:24]`
- Kissinger's deputy **Alexander Haig** ("Haig was the guy who pushed the goddamn thing") became indispensable, getting the **Cambodia bombing** before Nixon (over the objections of Defense Secretary Laird and SoS Rogers). The cover-up: **dual reporting** — B-52 crews briefed on South Vietnam "cover targets," then redirected by ground radar to the *real* Cambodian targets, with debriefs logged as South Vietnam (a dedicated furnace in Saigon burned the records). One radar man's question — hide the raids "from who?" "I guess the Foreign Relations Committee." `[00:16:24]`
- **Operation Breakfast/Menu** `[00:21:59]`: **March 1969** — Nixon approved bombing the "**Fish Hook**" (Operation Breakfast); the crews reported "73 secondary explosions" (the mythical Viet Cong HQ "must have been hit") — but it didn't work (the communists just moved deeper into Cambodia). A **Daniel Boone** special-forces team sent to grab "stunned" survivors was instead slaughtered (a replacement squad refused the order: "fuck you"; the deaths went unmentioned by Nixon/Kissinger). Breakfast became **Operation Menu** (Lunch, Snack, Dinner, Dessert) — Kissinger personally selected the targets. `[00:21:59]`
- **The leak → Watergate's roots** `[00:27:05]`: a May 9, 1969 NYT story exposed the raids; Kissinger ("we will destroy whoever did this") had the FBI **wiretap** officials and journalists — the springtime 1969 taps that "would lead bit by bit all the way to a break-in at the Watergate Hotel." `[00:27:05]`

## Vietnamization `[00:28:00]`
- The bombing's counterpart: **Vietnamization** — bringing US troops home in increments (540,000 → 335,000) while expanding the South Vietnamese army, plus a draft lottery to defuse discontent. But the antiwar movement surged: the **Oct 15, 1969 Moratorium** (the largest protest yet — 30,000 led by Coretta Scott King in DC, 50,000 in NYC). Nixon's **Nov 3 "Silent Majority"** address ("I would be untrue to my oath… [to allow] policy… dictated by the minority") polled 70–80% support. `[00:28:00]`
- 100,000+ tons of bombs fell during Menu (thousands to tens of thousands of Cambodians dead) — "Cambodia was being sucked into a conflict it had done everything to avoid." `[00:31:35]`

## The Lon Nol coup `[00:32:17]`
- The key that unlocked Cambodia was the **coup against Sihanouk**. Sihanouk had handed his government to the right (Lon Nol sabotaging his social programs; the economy stalling, alienating the Phnom Penh middle class) — even amid an odd arms-selling collaboration between Lon Nol's military and the Viet Cong (via Sihanoukville). Sihanouk, having lost his nerve, reopened to the US. His rival **Prince Sirik Matak** was "a known friend of the Americans" with "ready access" to the CIA. The coup "had all the hallmarks of American approval, if not direct support" (Frank Snepp; CIA links to Lon Nol via the DIA in Saigon and the Indonesian embassy; US commanders warned days ahead). "It was the worst possible time for Sihanouk to take a vacation." `[00:32:17]`
- **The coup unfolds** `[00:38:06]`: **March 1970** — with Sihanouk abroad, Lon Nol staged anti-Vietnamese riots; Sihanouk, hesitating, went to Moscow anyway (a fortune-teller and a black-stained sacred sword foretelling his fall; the CIA telling his mother to deny the coup). **March 18** — Matak strong-armed Lon Nol into joining him; they ousted Sihanouk (Parliament voting to end his reign). Sihanouk heard it on the radio en route to Moscow's airport. (A bizarre side-note: mutineers on the USS Columbia Eagle, carrying napalm, sought asylum in "neutral" Cambodia — and were jailed by the new regime.) "One of the world's oldest continuous monarchies was abolished." Cambodia's High Court condemned Sihanouk to death in absentia (for the very Vietnamese deals Lon Nol had also made). `[00:38:06]`
- **FUNK** `[00:43:48]`: in Beijing, Sihanouk learned of his overthrow from the Chinese, who urged him to ally with the communists. Swallowing his distaste, he became the figurehead of **FUNK** (the National United Front of Kampuchea) — the guerrilla army to "liberate the motherland," then mostly Viet Cong. The coup "was the beginning not only of the American invasion of Cambodia, but the Khmer's own civil war." `[00:43:48]`

## The invasion `[00:46:42]`
- Haig applied the Nixon Doctrine in Cambodia (a Phnom Penh military mission; air power to clear the way for Lon Nol), with funds diverted (largely from South Korea) to avoid a hostile Congress. Westmoreland warned the sanctuaries couldn't be cleaned up (monsoon coming); Nixon: "Let's go blow the hell out of him." But Cambodia was really a tool for Vietnamization (officials told the NYT it was "a laboratory"); Kissinger to a colonel: "Don't think of victory. Just keep it alive." `[00:46:42]`
- **Jack McCain & the green light** `[00:49:13]`: the US pumped Lon Nol with aid (his "grandiose plan for an army of 400,000" alarming even the Americans). In Hawaii, Admiral **Jack McCain** (father of John; the "Big Red Arrow Man" whose briefings of "Chai Com" claws so exhausted him he'd fall asleep) convinced Nixon the North was about to take all of Cambodia. Nixon, wanting to go "for all the marbles" ("the liberals are waiting to see Nixon let Cambodia go down the drain"), overrode Laird and Rogers. `[00:49:13]`
- **April 30, 1970** `[00:53:08]`: Nixon announced "not an invasion of Cambodia" — a 30,000-troop air and ground invasion through the Fish Hook. They found no legendary HQ (Nixon demanding the army find "something, anything"). UPI from **Snuol**: a leveled town, looting GIs ("Grab them shades"), a maimed dead girl (the AP censored the looting). Captured US reporters saw the truth: large numbers of Cambodian civilians fleeing the bombing; "no Viet Cong headquarters was destroyed" — the insurgents (and refugees) moved deeper into Cambodia. The mission, "as sold by Nixon on television, was a failure" — but the US now had a new subcontractor in Lon Nol. `[00:53:08]`

## The home front explodes `[00:59:35]`
- The invasion (and the discovery of the secret Laos war) shattered American denial — millions took to the streets ("over 4 million college students"). **May 4, 1970** — National Guard killed four students at **Kent State**; days later bayonets at New Mexico; then two killed by police at **Jackson State** (a historically Black college). Nixon lashed back (approving the **Huston Plan** for mail-opening, burglaries, surveillance — scrapped only after Hoover's turf objection; intelligence agencies had been doing it for years anyway, building on LBJ's CHAOS and COINTELPRO). Nixon, "on the edge of a nervous breakdown" (per Kissinger), showed up at the Lincoln Memorial at 5 AM to talk to students. `[00:59:35]`
- **The resignations** `[01:04:11]`: 250 foreign-service officers signed a protest; staffers resigned (some staying to "protect" Kissinger as "our last best hope"); William Watts, told his views "represent the cowardice of the eastern establishment," took a swing at Kissinger ("I missed him"), and refused Haig's "you've had an order from your commander in chief" — "I just have." `[01:04:11]`

## Congress & the free-fire zone `[01:06:48]`
- Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (June) and passed a watered-down **Cooper-Church Amendment** (limiting ground troops, already gone). The White House, "unclenched and almost bragging," declared new strikes — Nixon wanting "limitless freedom" for the bombers; by summer's end "much of Cambodia was a free-fire zone." `[01:06:48]`

## Lon Nol's pogroms & the Khmer Rouge windfall `[01:08:43]`
- Eight weeks after the coup, Lon Nol declared a religious war on the Vietnamese ("enemies of Buddha"), with **pogroms** (1,000 rope-bound corpses in the Mekong). South Vietnamese troops "plunged into Cambodia, raping, looting, burning" as "a conquering hostile nation" — in stark contrast to the disciplined Viet Cong ("exemplary guests… leaving payment for anything they took"). "The result was a recruiting opportunity made in heaven for the Khmer Rouge" — tens of thousands of villagers joining the resistance; Phnom Penh swelling to 2–3 million refugees. Watching from a secret base, **Saloth Sâr** saw half of Cambodia fall to the resistance (mostly the Vietnamese, "not yet his own Khmer Rouge"). And in 1970 he changed his name to the one history would remember: **Pol Pot**. `[01:08:43]`
