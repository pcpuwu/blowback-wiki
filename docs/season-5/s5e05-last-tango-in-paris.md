# Blowback S5E5 — "Last Tango in Paris"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s5e05-last-tango-in-paris.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:05:30
> **Thesis:** 1970–73 — the Khmer Rouge come into their own (Pol Pot's rise, the FUNK alliance with exiled Sihanouk, early signs of fanaticism), Lon Nol's republic rots into a corrupt garrison state committing anti-Vietnamese pogroms, Nixon completes the **opening to China** (at Vietnam's expense), the Paris Accords finally end America's Vietnam War (Jan 1973) — and the saturation bombing of Cambodia escalates toward its apocalyptic peak.

## Cold open `[00:00:01]`
- **May 5, 1972** — Nixon to Kissinger, who worried civilian death tolls might hurt re-election: "You're so goddamn concerned about the civilians, and I don't give a damn. I don't care." `[00:00:01]`

## The Khmer Rouge ascendant `[00:03:43]`
- Pol Pot's army was still small, nested inside the Viet Cong who did most of the fighting against Lon Nol. After the coup, Beijing organized **FUNK** (the National United Front) under figurehead **Sihanouk** — ruling from a Beijing mansion ("a heated pool and nine personal chefs… I am a gourmet"), forcing the austere **Ieng Sary** to watch pornographic films ("he's going to have a hell of a self-criticism tomorrow"). The Sihanouk association gave the Khmer Rouge prestige (China shifting support from Sihanouk *to* them — "another unforced error of America's Cambodia policy"). `[00:03:43]`
- **The real leaders** `[00:06:48]`: the party's first official history featured the photogenic "**three ghosts**" (Khieu Samphan, Hou Yuon, Hu Nim — presumed dead) as Renaissance men in the jungle, masking the *actual* leaders (Pol Pot, Ieng Sary, Nuon Chea) — "weary, glum… Saloth Sâr has the least memorable face… even in front of the camera, he hides." `[00:06:48]`

## "A light touch" — then the extremism `[00:09:09]`
- Early on the Khmer Rouge resembled the Vietnamese/Lao communists — recruiting from Lon Nol's army, treating peasants with respect (CIA reports admitting "the communists help the people with the harvesting… treat the women with respect"; abolishing liquor, gambling, "feudal terms of address"). It was Lon Nol's army that was then infamous for atrocities. But signs of extremism: the Maoist idea of *winning over prisoners* "did not come naturally to the Cambodians"; "to the Khmer Rouge, all foreigners were enemies" (26 Western journalists captured by the KR were killed, unlike those freed by the Viet Cong). Pol Pot's wife **Khieu Ponnary** embodied a clinical anti-Vietnamese paranoia (later diagnosed as paranoid schizophrenia) — "a paranoia present among all the Khmer Rouge leaders." `[00:09:09]`

## The China gambit pays off `[00:13:16]`
- Reeling from Kent State and the failed invasion before the 1970 midterms, Nixon pushed the secret **China** opening (to pressure Vietnam *and* isolate the USSR). After silence, the breakthrough came via Pakistan's **Yahya Khan** ("It is essential that we open negotiations with China"); **Zhou Enlai** replied that a Nixon visit would be "most welcome" if Taiwan was on the table — the cost being a US troop withdrawal from Taiwan, the upside Chinese support for the Paris talks (which Beijing had been telling Hanoi to reject). `[00:13:16]`

## Lon Nol's republic `[00:17:33]`
- **Oct 1970** — the **Khmer Republic** declared (the inaugural 101-gun salute's 15th cannon exploding, killing a gunner — "such was the official start"). Lon Nol ran "a garrison state" losing territory yearly (peasants still thinking Sihanouk ruled), riddled with mafia-style corruption: no-show jobs, ghost regiments, officers selling equipment (even to the Khmer Rouge), "too much *bonjour* [corruption] everywhere." Rice was hoarded for the black market; the US withheld humanitarian aid for two years; Lon Nol eradicated civil liberties (1973). At the top: a superstitious dictator sprinkling "holy sand" around the city, a "dangerous racist" waging holy war for Buddha — and a bad general (losing half the country to the North Vietnamese by August). `[00:17:33]`

## The anti-Vietnamese pogroms `[00:23:17]`
- Lon Nol turned anti-Viet-Cong rhetoric into a **mass pogrom of all Vietnamese** in Cambodia (~several hundred thousand, mostly anti-communist themselves but "inconveniently of the same race"). Internment camps → massacres (troops killing hundreds of civilians at a time; 1,000 corpses in the Mekong; churches bombed). Per Kiernan, of 400,000 Vietnamese, half were expelled in 1970, several thousand killed — souring relations with South Vietnam's Thieu. Lon Nol also targeted ethnic Chinese and tried to make Cambodia an official ethno-state (vetoed by elites, since most Khmer have mixed heritage) — making the elite "think wistfully of good old Prince Sihanouk." `[00:23:17]`

## Paris fails again `[00:27:36]`
- **Aug 1970** — Kissinger excitedly went to Paris (thinking the Cambodia invasion had worked), offering a "standstill ceasefire" preserving Saigon — but Vietnamization had *strengthened* Hanoi's position, so they refused. Nixon's Oct 7 TV "five-point" peace deal (known to be dead on arrival) drew rave US reviews but Hanoi's dismissal ("only a gift certificate for the votes of the American electorate"). The GOP did okay in the midterms. `[00:27:36]`

## Ping-pong diplomacy `[00:30:24]`
- A second gambit — an invasion of **Laos** (Operation Lam Son 719, *South Vietnamese only*, to prove Vietnamization). It failed badly (ARVN rangers routed). Meanwhile Kissinger fought to keep his pole position (he'd sicced the FBI on his own staff; the Joint Chiefs were now spying on *him* via a Navy stenographer — the **Moorer-Radford** leaks). But the China breakthrough bailed Nixon out: **ping-pong diplomacy** (April 1971), then Kissinger's secret July Beijing trip (Mao on the US troop concession: "it would take some time for a monkey to evolve into a human"), then the public announcement of a 1972 Nixon visit — a two-year approval boost (until Watergate). The strategy stayed détente (the USSR "the main enemy"), but "someone would have to pay the price": the US betraying Taiwan, China beginning "to turn their backs on… the North Vietnamese." `[00:30:24]`

## Cambodia's brutal war `[00:37:18]`
- "Neither side took prisoners" (a grinning Lon Nol soldier with two severed heads; Khmer Rouge captives disemboweled and hung in trees). Nixon: "the dollars we send to Cambodia save American lives… this is no civil war." Lon Nol's two big offensives — **Chenla 1 and Chenla 2** (1970–71) toward Kampong Thom, Pol Pot's base — were guerrilla-victory disasters (Chenla 1's commander, Col. **Um Savuth**, "nearly always drunk," had half his head blown off ordering a soldier to shoot a cat off it). The Khmer Rouge were no rock either (mostly raw village volunteers — some joining "because the girls teased us if we stayed behind," or for Sihanouk). Lon Nol rigged a 1971 election; the US embassy spent the year begging the *actual winners* to join his government. `[00:37:18]`

## Bomb, bomb, bomb `[00:41:42]`
- After the Laos defeat convinced Nixon/Kissinger that Vietnamization (the "Mug's game" of client states) wasn't working, it was "time to bomb." Nixon: no limits "except to exclude nuclear weapons." US pilots over Cambodia had even *more* freedom than in Vietnam (falsified reports "accepted as normal," the press shut out). By now the Khmer Rouge numbered 35,000 + 100,000 Vietnamese guerrillas, with a third of Cambodia (2M+ people) under Khmer Rouge control. `[00:41:42]`

## The Paris Accords `[00:43:42]`
- The China thaw paradoxically *strengthened* Hanoi's resolve (fearing Beijing's betrayal). Kissinger kept the back channel alive; Hanoi dropped the "guilt clause" on reparations but wouldn't budge on removing Thieu. A 1972 timeline: Nixon's China trip (Feb); Hanoi's **Spring/Easter Offensive** (March, met with "kick the shit out of them" bombing); the mining of **Haiphong Harbor** (May, Operation Pocket Money); the **Watergate break-in** (June 18); Nixon's renomination; Kissinger's "**peace is at hand**" (Oct 26, 12 days pre-election). Nixon won a landslide (Nov 7, 60.7%, McGovern taking only Massachusetts + DC). But **Thieu refused** the deal ("a cunning, crafty trick"); the breakdown drove Nixon to madman-theory **Christmas bombing** of Hanoi. `[00:43:42]`
- **Peace** `[00:51:00]`: the deal signed **Jan 27, 1973** "differed only cosmetically from the accord… of October 1968" that Nixon/Kissinger had sabotaged (Le Duc Tho: "you, and no one else, stained the honor of the United States"). 2–3 million Vietnamese dead; in just the four extra Nixon years, 3 million civilians killed/injured/displaced. America stepped back from Vietnam — but "an entirely new war was foisted on neutral Cambodia, and that war would continue." `[00:51:00]`

## No ceasefire for Cambodia `[00:54:00]`
- No real attempt at a Cambodian ceasefire. The NSC's psychiatric assessment found Lon Nol deteriorating (stroke risk in "6 to 18 months"), but the US kept "Black Papa" anyway. His republic was "a weak U.S. colony, corrupt from the top down," its army facing "unkillable communists," Phnom Penh overrun by refugees, the health ministry collapsed (of ~$250M in US aid, ~$1M went to refugees — "not the Americans' area"). `[00:54:00]`
- **Christian charity as CIA cover** `[00:56:38]`: humanitarian relief came largely from Catholic missionaries and the evangelical NGO **World Vision** — which (like Dr. Tom Dooley and USAID's John Hannah before) fed the CIA intelligence ("we give much more service to the U.S. government than we get from it… the giving of information. We often go to places where government officials cannot go"). Rev. Nakajima: the churches were "serving as willing pawns of U.S. foreign policy." `[00:56:38]`

## The Khmer Rouge come into their own (1973) `[01:00:18]`
- As the Vietnamese turned to taking Saigon, they drifted from the Cambodian communists, who now numbered ~50,000 and could act independently. Sihanouk was sidelined in Beijing (Ieng Sary the real Cambodian leader there) — "the joke was on him." Pol Pot's early **collectivization** (1972) was relatively mild (the poorest peasants "did well"), but darker policies emerged: the cultural leveling of the **Cham** Muslim minority (banning their dress, jewelry, hairstyles for plain black pajamas); new battlefield commandments dropping Mao's "don't ill-treat captives" for "**have burning rage towards the enemy**, and not to depend on foreigners"; deserters now killed, not indulged. `[01:00:18]`
- **The purge of the returnees** `[01:04:22]`: after Paris, the Hanoi-trained Cambodian old guard (exiled since Geneva, ~20 years) returned home — and Pol Pot ordered them rounded up as "suspected Vietnamese agents" and mostly executed. "The Cambodian communists who had founded the party… were but for a few wiped out." Pol Pot and Ieng Sary, trusting no one, "turned their gaze to the dirty, corrupt city of Phnom Penh, with a burning vision of a glorious future." `[01:04:22]`
