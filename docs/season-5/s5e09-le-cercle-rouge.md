# Blowback S5E9 — "Le Cercle Rouge"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s5e09-le-cercle-rouge.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:07:00
> **Thesis:** The Faustian pact — after Vietnam topples the Khmer Rouge, the US (Brzezinski), China (Deng), and Thailand back the genocidal Khmer Rouge as a "nationalist insurgency" to "bleed" Vietnam. Cambodia (the new People's Republic) is met with isolation, blocked aid, and Carter's "genocide" charge — aimed at the *Vietnamese*, not Pol Pot. China invades Vietnam; the Khmer Rouge keep their UN seat; and a Vietnamese-run genocide tribunal convicts Pol Pot in absentia to no Western interest.

## Cold open: Sihanouk's elevator note `[00:00:00]`
- Freed from house arrest (at China's urging) but still "handcuffed to the Khmer Rouge cause," **Sihanouk** was sent to the UN to denounce Vietnam's invasion ("I do not approve of [Pol Pot's] internal policy… but his external policy is good"). At the Waldorf (China paying $1,200/day, three Khmer Rouge minders sharing his suite), he slipped a Secret Service agent a note begging to defect ("free me from the control of the Khmer Rouge"). Holbrooke ran "a little cloak-and-dagger operation"; the US message: "Defect if you want to, but… once a person defects… he loses his usefulness as a political leader." `[00:00:00]`

## Sihanouk's loss and Deng's pitch `[00:09:00]`
- Sihanouk had grasped the magnitude of the genocide — his own missing children and relatives "liquidated by the Khmer Rouge." Vance offered him guest status but not asylum ("why risk upsetting America's more important friends?"). Both Washington and Beijing wanted to keep him as an asset (even Hanoi offered him the head of its new government). **Deng Xiaoping**, in a secret DC meeting, admitted "we Chinese must confess… [Pol Pot] is too tough" but persuaded Sihanouk to return to Beijing (promising to search for his missing relatives). The defection crisis ended — but "a dramatic new plan to punish Vietnam… was only just beginning." `[00:09:00]`

## Vietnam in Cambodia `[00:11:36]`
- Vietnam's troops were "welcomed and celebrated" — the expected anti-Vietnamese xenophobia "failed to materialize" (some Cambodians took revenge on fleeing Khmer Rouge: "they hated us… they just wanted us gone"). Vietnam declared the **People's Republic of Kampuchea** (PRK) under **Heng Samrin** (an Eastern Zone defector), dismantling the Khmer Rouge systems. Shawcross (an anti-communist by the 80s): "for the overwhelming majority… the invasion meant freedom" — the roads filled with people searching for their families. But it was a military occupation (no contested elections until well past 1981); the country was wrecked (one ambulance, no drinking water, no money), with **famine looming**. `[00:11:36]`

## The campaign against Vietnam `[00:13:46]`
- Washington's reaction was to **condemn Vietnam and mobilize a global campaign** — cutting aid, restricting IMF/UN loans. Japan and Europe cut aid; the US fought any aid to Cambodia as "breaking the siege." `[00:13:46]`

## The famine and blocked aid `[00:17:00]`
- The harvest was missed amid the upheaval (Cambodians breaking into Khmer Rouge granaries for feasts); by July the PRK warned of famine. US officials called it "alarmist," then opposed Western aid — to "punish Vietnam." Becker: "after the Khmer Rouge had isolated Cambodia, now the Westerners isolate Cambodia." Eventually relief flowed, but the US won the tug-of-war: aid would reach the **Khmer Rouge areas** too. **Carter** announced $70M to prevent "a tragedy of almost genocidal proportions," then in November accused the *PRK* of "genocide"; the Western press called the *Vietnamese* the heirs of Nazi camps. But ABC's Hong Kong bureau and even the US ambassador to Thailand found **no evidence** Vietnam/PRK was blocking aid. The lopsided spending ($1,124/head in Thailand, $439 at the border, $48 inside Cambodia) "served to draw people… into the orbit of the Khmer Rouge." The Soviet bloc and Cuba sent aid directly to Phnom Penh. `[00:17:00]`

## China invades Vietnam `[00:25:00]`
- **Late January 1979** — during his US normalization trip, **Deng Xiaoping** told Carter (in a secret White House meeting) of a planned **punitive invasion of Vietnam** ("exactly a month"). Vu Minh Hoang: "the ironic thing is that Deng was doing it for the United States… this is what would get the Americans woken up to the threats of the Vietnamese and the Soviet Union." Carter's nine-point argument against it didn't change Deng's mind. **Feb 17, 1979** — China sent 250,000 troops ~15 miles into Vietnam; by the month's end Vietnam had lost 10,000 dead, its border infrastructure destroyed. The USSR was "discredited" (failing to come to Vietnam's aid); Deng used the army's poor showing (20,000 Chinese casualties) to purge leftist officers. But the stated goal — forcing Vietnam out of Cambodia — failed: Vietnam stayed and even launched a new offensive on Pol Pot's base. `[00:25:00]`

## Brzezinski's Khmer Rouge plan `[00:33:00]`
- Brzezinski (the "bleeder") saw the Cambodia-Vietnam war as "a proxy war between China and the Soviet Union" — the US must back the anti-Vietnamese side. Just as he'd do a year later with the Afghan mujahideen, he made "common cause with a band of local fanatics": the **Khmer Rouge**. On the record: "I encouraged the Chinese to support Pol Pot. I encouraged the Thai to help Democratic Kampuchea." The Thai border became the Khmer Rouge base (as Afghanistan-Pakistan would be for the mujahideen) — "a Faustian pact between the U.S., China, and the Khmer Rouge" that outlasted Carter (Reagan's Secretary of State **Haig**: "we took the difficult decision to continue to support the Khmer Rouge… this charnel figure"). Kissinger's 1975 line: "they are murderous thugs. But we won't let that stand in our way." `[00:33:00]`

## Rebuilding the Khmer Rouge `[00:38:00]`
- The new Khmer Rouge army was built from "walking skeletons" trapped in the western zones (a UNICEF team found ~10,000 starving, malaria-ridden people). Through the 1980s the Khmer Rouge operated as a guerrilla army (attacks, massacres of Vietnamese civilians, landmines). **Pol Pot** changed his name again (to Phem), abandoned the socialist iconography ("we have to turn to the West and follow their way"), put his troops in Chinese jungle green, and took to custom **pastel safari suits** from Bangkok. Diagnosed with Hodgkin's, he remarried (a young woman, Mea, after his wife Ponnary fully succumbed to schizophrenia) and had a daughter. (His old enemy **Lon Nol** died in California, 1985, still calling himself "president of the Khmer Republic.") `[00:38:00]`

## US aid reaches the Khmer Rouge `[00:44:49]`
- The US "could not be seen" to directly supply the Khmer Rouge, but funded a "nationalist umbrella coalition" of which the Khmer Rouge was the largest member — and aid reached them directly (Joel Brinkley: of $215M, a Congressional probe "found some leakage," played down — "isn't the larger objective here defeating the Vietnamese puppets?"). A 1990 Peter Jennings/ABC report (denied by the Bush admin) showed US aid going through UN camps controlled by the Khmer Rouge; Assistant Secretary Solomon stonewalled on why the "Cambodia working group" was covert. `[00:44:49]`

## The UN seat & the refugee atrocities `[00:47:43]`
- At the UN, the **Khmer Rouge kept Cambodia's official seat** (the US throwing its weight to preserve their credentials; after one vote, US delegate Rosenstock found Ieng Sary "shaking his hand, grinning broadly"). China called Vietnam "the Cubans of Asia, but rather more dangerous"; delegates feared expelling the Khmer Rouge would set a precedent (e.g. for Israel). The revitalized insurgency created a new refugee crisis — and **Thailand pushed tens of thousands back** to death: ~2,000 handed to the Khmer Serei (200 dead within a week); 43,000–45,000 driven down mined cliffs at Preah Vihear (Thai soldiers taking refugees' valuables, then opening fire). The border camps were ruled by warlords and black marketeers, shelled by all sides. `[00:47:43]`

## The genocide tribunal `[00:53:54]`
- To establish its legitimacy, the PRK held a **genocide tribunal** (Aug 1979) — "a quintessentially political piece of theater" (East German prosecutors flown in secretly; the Vietnamese role hidden), trying the "**Pol Pot–Ieng Sary clique**" (both in absentia). Witnesses testified to mass execution, torture, feeding flesh to crocodiles, and the targeting of ethnic minorities and Buddhist monks (some mass graves in old US bomb craters). US law professor **John Quigley** testified that genocide (per the 1948 Convention's "intent to destroy… a national, ethnical, racial, or religious group") *had* been committed. **Aug 19** — Pol Pot and Ieng Sary found guilty, sentenced to death in absentia. But the convicts "remained at large… armed and dangerous"; "much of the international community was totally unimpressed." Soon after, the **UN voted to seat the Khmer Rouge** over the PRK. `[00:53:54]`

## "Sovereignty over human rights" `[01:03:45]`
- The UN dropped its investigation into Khmer Rouge abuses — "sovereignty was more important than human rights" — the prevailing logic being that the Khmer Rouge army was "needed to force the Vietnamese to retreat" (any trial would have to wait until after a peace accord that *enticed* the Khmer Rouge). The Reagan/Bush administrations "deliberately did not call the Khmer Rouge responsible for genocide" — wanting "the Vietnamese occupation to be the only catastrophe under discussion," hiding behind "irrelevant gestures of disdain" (forbidding diplomats to shake Khmer Rouge hands). Congressman Chester Atkins summed it up: "It's a policy of hatred… We're still fighting the Vietnam War, and this is the last battle of that war, and if we have to use the Khmer Rouge as a pawn in that, we'll use them." `[01:03:45]`
