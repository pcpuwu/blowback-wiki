## Cold open: Mao counsels Pol Pot `[00:00:08]`
- **June 1975** — the dying Mao met Pol Pot in Beijing. Mao, in "a swirl of dialectics," urged him to "do rectification… the road is torturous" — Short's reading: a hint to abandon extremism. Pol Pot "remained all smiles" and "said nothing of substance" — then went home and pursued "the most extreme policies imaginable." Mao was "entranced by Pol's boldness in emptying the cities," asking Le Duan if Vietnam could do the same (Le Duan shook his head; "no, we couldn't do it either"). `[00:00:08]`

## Year Zero `[00:03:30]`
- "Year Zero" (coined by the missionary François Ponchaud, not the Khmer Rouge) — "Pol Pot and his comrades would pound their society into dust… a return to ancient Khmer glory as much as the dawn of a socialist utopia. It was somehow **less than zero**." On **April 17, 1975** Lon Nol's stranded PM Long Boret was beheaded; the cabinet executed. The chaos coincided with the Cambodian New Year (a 7-year-old who lost her sisters for years). `[00:03:30]`

## Emptying the cities `[00:07:50]`
- No initial bloodbath ("white flags everywhere"), but by the afternoon of day one came the order to **evacuate Phnom Penh** ("just for a few days," to avoid a supposed new US bombing). All 2 million driven out, including hospital patients ("people in surgery… moved off into the streets to die"). "Auntie": "we were terrified since we were labeled as **new people**… the comrades were cruel every step of the way." Banker **Mey Komphot** marched 10 days in "a thick sea of people." Kiernan: ~20,000 died on the marches (the old/bedridden killed, pregnant women dying) — "the responsibility of their decision to empty the cities without sparing even the hospital patients." `[00:07:50]`
- **The agrarian transformation** `[00:14:22]`: a national pivot to self-sufficient agriculture — "new people" embedded as farmers in cooperatives. Short notes the *idea* wasn't irrational (a 1976 Western plan for Thailand resembled it) — "the problem lay in the way it was to be implemented… just cruel and inhuman." Pol Pot and Ieng Sary slipped quietly into the dead city — no celebration, no announcement. `[00:14:22]`

## Vietnam after victory `[00:15:55]`
- Unified Vietnam inherited devastation (4M tons of US bombs, 0.5M tons of napalm; 2–3M dead). Bitterness ran beneath reunification: the North imposed its rule on the South (formal Socialist Republic, **July 2, 1976**), executing tens of thousands and sending ~200,000 to re-education camps; ~1 million **boat people** fled (damaging Vietnam's image). But also progress: near-total literacy within a decade, a health system built for the South. Vietnam normalized relations with 97 nations — but, strapped for resources, "deeply desired a normalization with the U.S." `[00:15:55]`
- **The aid that never came** `[00:21:00]`: the US kept its embargo and refused reparations — though Nixon had *secretly promised* Hanoi >$3.25 billion (a year's GDP). Washington dismissed it and made the **POW/MIA** issue the price of normalization. `[00:21:00]`
- **The MIA myth** `[00:23:00]`: Perlstein — "MIA" was a Nixonian invention (renaming "killed in action, body unrecovered" as "missing in action/POW") to frame Vietnam as uniquely cruel, though fewer Americans were captured than in any previous war. Operation Homecoming returned all 587 POWs, but Nixon's "1,600" figure left ~1,000 phantom captives, fueling decades of POW/MIA flags and lobbying (used by the right to torpedo normalization). The US left blossomed in the antiwar years but "faded quickly after 1975." `[00:23:00]`

## Life under Angkar `[00:27:10]`
- The first refugee stories (eyeglasses-wearers killed, all toys banned, no dogs) were "fables based on a truth too awful to explain." Democratic Kampuchea — "a slave society": everyone working ~12+ hours/day, one day off for political education, no wages (money abolished). The country closed off entirely (mined borders, no mail/phone, death for crossing). Society split into **new people** (city-dwellers, near-starvation rations) and **old people** (peasants, slightly better). Komphot's ration: 30 lbs of rice/month for a full worker, "no fish, vegetables, oil, or meat" — "I decided to give it two years. If nothing changed, I would commit suicide." The phrase: "If you keep this man, there is no profit, and if he goes, there is no loss." `[00:27:10]`
- **Death by neglect** `[00:34:00]`: new people died of diarrhea, dysentery, malaria, typhoid (so widespread the Khmer Rouge broke their self-sufficiency rule to ask an American charity for DDT). Location-dependent (a village with plenty of rice; 30 miles south, cannibalism and a third dead). Most victims were of humble origin. Angkar destroyed all existing education ("the revolution rejected all engineers and technicians of the old society"), replacing it with barefoot children "memorizing how to thread wires." The cadres on the ground: "teenagers with murderous weapons… convinced theirs was a just and historic mission" (Kang Vansak: "giving illiterate kids guns after such indoctrination, can anyone be astonished at the results?"). Becker: "they learned to trust no one. Everything was secret." `[00:34:00]`

## The Mayaguez & the border `[00:41:00]`
- The first confrontation was with the Vietnamese — over French-drawn borders (the **Brévié Line** islands). Securing them, the Khmer Rouge captured a US merchant ship, the **SS Mayaguez**. Kissinger (madman theory) launched bombing + a Marine assault — but the Khmer Rouge had already released the sailors; "a completely meaningless fight, in which no Americans were saved because they were not there," killing **41 US personnel** (the last to die in America's Indochina war). Becker: the real victor was *Vietnam* (the US having gutted the Khmer Air Force/Navy). Cambodia apologized and signed an economic deal with Vietnam. `[00:41:00]`

## Sihanouk's golden cage `[00:44:53]`
- **Sept 1975** — Sihanouk returned to a flower-strewn welcome (Pol Pot watching hidden, never revealed to him). He chose to stay permanently — legitimizing the Khmer Rouge while being placed under **house arrest** ("My people had been transformed into cattle… a golden cage" while his family members vanished into the countryside). `[00:44:53]`

## China, Vietnam, and the Hoa `[00:47:30]`
- Beijing became Democratic Kampuchea's only real channel — despite Mao/Zhou/Deng's reservations, China gave "all-out support," because of the shared enemy: **Vietnam**. The Sino-Soviet split now caught Vietnam (Spratly/Paracel disputes with China) and the **Hoa** (ethnic Chinese in Vietnam) — Vietnam tightening control and nationalizing ~30,000 (mostly Chinese-run) businesses; China accusing persecution then refusing repatriation. "It was realpolitik, opposition to Vietnam, that brought China and Cambodia together." `[00:47:30]`

## "Instant communism" `[00:51:00]`
- The Khmer Rouge hadn't even declared themselves Marxist. **Sept 1976** — Mao died; a week later Pol Pot announced Angkar was a Marxist-Leninist organization (still not naming Pol Pot as Saloth Sâr), claiming they'd already achieved the first stage of socialism and were ready for a "super great leap forward." Sihanouk rolled his eyes at "instant communism" (Khieu Samphan saying *Kim Il-sung* was "on the wrong track" for raising living standards too much). Zhou Enlai had warned them: "Don't follow the bad example of our Great Leap Forward. Take things slowly." The Khmer Rouge "just smiled." `[00:51:00]`

## The faces of Angkar `[00:53:53]`
- The official government (1976): head of state **Khieu Samphan** (a "ghost," largely ceremonial — "passive but loyal, incorruptible but small-minded"); foreign minister **Ieng Sary** ("the Kissinger to Pol Pot's Nixon" — same receding hairline and ruthlessness, a fanatical diaspora Khmer, a nepotist); **Nuon Chea** (Brother Number Two); and **Pol Pot** as premier ("serene, like a monk… he would smile his unruffled smile, and then they would be taken away and executed"). His wife **Khieu Ponnary** sank into paranoid schizophrenia (violent anti-Vietnamese outbursts). And the head executioner, **Duch**, running the Tuol Sleng torture center. `[00:53:53]`

## Nixon's letter & Carter's failed reset `[00:59:02]`
- **1977** — Hanoi published **Nixon's secret letter** promising "$3.25 billion in non-refundable aid" (~$17B today, a year's GDP). **Carter** attempted a reset: a goodwill mission (Leonard Woodcock) found no remaining POWs/MIAs, but the Vietnamese wouldn't drop the aid demand ("you are saying you will sell us the remains of our MIAs in return for economic aid… no president could approve that"). **Richard Holbrooke** (35, the future Balkans/Afghanistan diplomat) offered UN admission + ending the embargo, but with no aid; the Vietnamese (his old colleague Phan) refused ("Without aid, it is impossible"). The US admitted Vietnam to the UN but the talks collapsed; Vietnam realized too late it had overplayed its hand ("you just whisper in my ear the amount" — no dice). Carter, echoing Nixon, turned it over to **Brzezinski**, who "had a very different idea… one that would find a place for the Khmer Rouge." `[00:59:02]`

## The road to war `[01:04:59]`
- The first Khmer Rouge years were "relatively stable" in the region, but their radicalism, incompetence, and failure turned their "burning eyes" toward Vietnam. **April 1977** — they reignited the feud, burning villages and shelling **Chau Doc** (9 civilians killed); ethnic Vietnamese inside Cambodia increasingly targeted. Cambodia, Vietnam, and China were "hurtling towards a new war… among communist neighbors who not that long ago had been allies." Defense minister **Son Sen**: "Before, Vietnam was our friend, but a friend with a conflict. Now, it has become our real enemy." `[01:04:59]`
