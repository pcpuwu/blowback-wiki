# Blowback S5E8 — "Third World War"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s5e08-third-world-war.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:13:00
> **Thesis:** The genocide machine and the war that ended it — the Tuol Sleng (S-21) torture system; the Khmer Rouge's scapegoating of Vietnam (border massacres, the extermination of Vietnamese, Chinese, Thai, and Cham minorities); the doomed internal revolts and self-cannibalizing purges; and the **Third Indochina War** (Vietnam's invasion, Christmas 1978 → Phnom Penh falls Jan 7, 1979). Meanwhile Brzezinski engineers a US–China–Khmer Rouge axis against Soviet-backed Vietnam.

## Cold open: Tuol Sleng discovered `[00:00:04]`
- **Jan 8, 1979** — two Vietnamese photojournalists, following the smell of rotting flesh through a silent Phnom Penh, found a fenced-off old school: corpses chained to beds with cut throats, torture devices, cells. This was **S-21** (Santebal — security police), known by the school's name **Tuol Sleng**. Its chief was a former schoolteacher; children were tortured and killed too. **Norng Chan Phal**, arrested at nine ("they lied to us, saying they were going to take us to see our father"), survived because "we were the last victims." `[00:00:04]`

## Picking a fight with Vietnam `[00:06:48]`
- Relations were solid through 1975–76, but Pol Pot was "simply playing for time." After a turbulent 1976 (only a quarter of Cambodians behind the government, the rice harvest failing), "the Pol Pot regime needed an active enemy" — and Vietnam "could strike a chord in the desperate, jumbled minds of all Cambodians." Becker: when the rice failed, "they start killing people… they have to be agents of the CIA, the Soviet Union, or the Vietnam… pretty soon there are very few people left to blame, so you blame Vietnam." `[00:06:48]`
- **The border raids** `[00:08:33]`: Cambodian raids began January 1977 (organized by defense minister **Son Sen**); the ferocious **April 30, 1977** attack (the anniversary of South Vietnam's liberation) killed ~100 civilians at Tinh Bien. Vietnam, "keeping up a facade of normalcy" (still hoping for US aid, wary of China), bombed Khmer Rouge positions and declared a 200-mile economic zone — but the "darker to come" was building. `[00:08:33]`

## China leans in `[00:13:00]`
- China was a "basket case" until the **Gang of Four** fell (late 1976) and moderates like **Deng Xiaoping** returned. Despite skepticism of the Khmer Rouge's extremism, China and Cambodia shared "a historic enemy in common: Vietnam" (Deng calling the Vietnamese "the hooligans of the East"). China made noise about the Spratlys; a senior Chinese general met Ieng Sary. Vietnam's Pham Van Dong got handed "a list of Chinese grievances" — so Vietnam, narrowing its options, "swallowed its pride and turned again to Moscow" (June 1977). `[00:13:00]`

## Tuol Sleng `[00:17:00]`
- S-21 was the "flagship" of a nationwide machinery of death (with informal killing fields everywhere) — "strictly speaking, an interrogation and torture facility rather than a prison… no one was ever released." Everyone who entered had to produce "a piece of the grand puzzle of conspiracy against Angkar." Chandler's catalog of tortures (beatings, electric shock, fingernails pulled, water torture, forced to eat excrement). Days began at 5 AM; up to three interrogation sessions. The chief, **Duch** (Kang Kek Iew, a schoolteacher arrested under Sihanouk), was "fastidious" — his memo "The Last Plan" wove the confessions into a grand conspiracy theory implicating the US, USSR, Taiwan, and Vietnam ("like James Jesus Angleton… mesmerized by the idea of moles"). His staff: mostly young, unmarried, rural poor-peasant men. `[00:17:00]`
- **The escalating purges** `[00:25:00]`: 1975 victims were Lon Nol associates; 1976 saw the feeling the party itself was infiltrated (the Northwest zone purged); 1977 a turning point — party officials and *workers* killed (142 workers, "to murder the proletariat in whose name the revolution was waged"); 1978 an inter-party purge. From mid-1977 the guiding principle was the threat from Vietnam — "one great hunt for Vietnamese minds in Khmer bodies." `[00:25:00]`

## The Tinh Bien massacre & Pol Pot revealed `[00:28:14]`
- **Sept 24, 1977** — a Khmer Rouge attack on Tinh Bien (a Hungarian journalist, Sándor Gyori, shown bloated beheaded corpses; "more people than any single raid… involving the Americans or the French"). On the day of Gyori's visit, **Pol Pot gave a five-hour radio speech** finally revealing himself as leader of the Communist Party of Kampuchea — "the mystery was over" — a rallying cry against Vietnam and "Vietnamese agents within the party." He, Ieng Sary, and others then went to Beijing/Pyongyang (Ieng Sary on to the UN). `[00:28:14]`

## Escalation to war `[00:33:30]`
- Vietnam recruited Cambodian refugees for a front to oust Pol Pot. Hanoi's first real punchback (October 1977 — a feigned-retreat "mousetrap" catching hundreds of Khmer Rouge). China cut flights to Hanoi and told Le Duan (November) it wouldn't stop backing the Khmer Rouge. Vietnam pushed tanks toward Phnom Penh (late December) — but Cambodia **publicly cut relations on New Year's Eve 1977** (expelling Vietnam's embassy), making Beijing uncomfortable; Vietnam withdrew, Pol Pot declared victory. China sent Madame Deng Yingchao (Zhou's widow) to counsel "slow down, negotiate" — Pol Pot brushed her off and launched new raids. `[00:33:30]`
- **The springtime 1978 massacres** `[00:36:38]`: the **Budap** raid (March 16, 1978 — ~250 killed; Ms. Trúc: "kill all, destroy all, burn all"); the worst at **Ba Chuc** (a month later — **3,500+** Vietnamese civilians hunted with guns, dogs, grenades, knives; a woman waking covered in blood, everyone dead). `[00:36:38]`

## Brzezinski's plan `[00:39:49]`
- Mid-1978 — Brzezinski went to Beijing. Carter's State Department had condemned the Khmer Rouge's abuses, but Brzezinski "took a different view." The Carter version of the **bleeders vs. dealers** split (over Afghanistan *and* Vietnam): Brzezinski (the bleeder) declared the Cambodia-Vietnam war "a proxy war between China (Cambodia) and the USSR (Vietnam)," and the US must intervene against Moscow — turning Vance's China-normalization policy into "an anti-Vietnam policy." "Whereas Kissinger mined the Soviet-Chinese-American triangle… Brzezinski was willing to alter the triangle into a Chinese-American entente against the Soviet Union." At the Great Wall he "shouted against the polar bear." China then condemned Vietnam over the ethnic Chinese and closed the border; Hanoi mobilized 100,000+ troops. (The David Truong spy case froze US-Vietnam normalization; **Nov 3, 1978** — Vietnam signed a friendship treaty with the USSR.) `[00:39:49]`

## The extermination of minorities `[00:47:00]`
- The Vietnamese in Cambodia (after Lon Nol's expulsions, ~20,000 remained) were targeted "for destruction as such" from April 1977 — by the time the Khmer Rouge were finished, "only a few dozen survived" (the UN tribunal: killings "systematically organized and directed against the Vietnamese… based on their membership of the group"). The **Chinese** ("its most respected ally") — roughly half the urban Chinese killed; the **Thai** (a third of the Koh Kong community); and the Muslim **Cham** — forced to cut hair, shave beards, eat pork at gunpoint; their number falling from ~250,000 (1975) to ~173,000 (1979) — "more than one third of the Chams, about 90,000 people, perished" (Kiernan). `[00:47:00]`

## Revolt and the self-cannibalizing purges `[00:56:54]`
- There *was* resistance (a 1973 Koh Kong insurrection, a 1975 Cham revolt, troop mutinies) — all met by lining everyone up and killing the suspected. The **Eastern Zone** revolt (May 1978): zone boss **So Phim**, an old-guard cadre soured on the revolution, sped to "bring things under control"; the clash touched off the most vicious purge in DK history (Duch's instructions: "no need to interrogate them, just smash them"; hundreds of thousands killed or deported). Burchett's theory on why no resistance succeeded: "there was no leading organization to guide and coordinate." So Phim shot himself ("You must rise up and struggle. The leadership are traitors… We are alone"). `[00:56:54]`

## The last witnesses `[01:01:32]`
- Sensing war, Pol Pot tried to polish DK's image, inviting journalists (Becker and Richard Dudman, plus the pro-Khmer-Rouge Scottish academic **Malcolm Caldwell**). Becker: "it was all propaganda all the time… scary, haunting, the emptiness." On the last day they interviewed Pol Pot (the only such interview in power) — he lectured for two hours that Vietnam would invade with Warsaw Pact support, and (he wanted them to write) the US and NATO would come support him. That night, an intruder broke in; Becker hid in the bathtub ("a porcelain fortress"); **Caldwell was murdered**. Becker's read: "someone wanted to send a message: don't send any more witnesses." They flew to Beijing with Caldwell's corpse (Dec 23). `[01:01:32]`

## The Third Indochina War `[01:07:00]`
- **Christmas Day, 1978** — Vietnam launched its invasion (a 14-division push, the same general who took the South in 1975) to eradicate the Khmer Rouge once and for all (still publicly denying it was invading). They debuted the **Salvation Front** (purged/escaped Khmer Rouge). Sihanouk was released and flown to China a day before the capital fell; Pol Pot and the leadership fled to the jungle (leaving "40,000 workers and soldiers to fend for themselves"). **Jan 7, 1979** — Vietnamese forces entered Phnom Penh; "the terrible three-and-a-half-year Khmer Rouge experiment in power was no more." Norng Chan Phal: at S-21, the guards fled, his mother was taken ("I never saw her again"); the Vietnamese freed the prisoners and gave them food. `[01:07:00]`

## The aftermath begins `[01:12:20]`
- The two-week war augured "a new stage of conflict and crisis" — Deng Xiaoping's China would make the next play (to punish Vietnam), and Brzezinski "saw all of this as one door closing while another opened." His next step: **help rebuild the Khmer Rouge** — on the record: "We couldn't support the Khmer Rouge directly, but we can support a coalition with Southeast Asia… Europe supporting them." So a country that "barely survived famine, deprivation, the death of a quarter of their people" was about to be turned on by the West and its neighbors. `[01:12:20]`
