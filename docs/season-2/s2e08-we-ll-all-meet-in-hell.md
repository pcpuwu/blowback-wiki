## Cold open: Che at Hiroshima `[00:00:00]`
- In 1959, on a foreign tour, Che **insisted on visiting Hiroshima** (against his hosts' wishes), refusing to honor Japan's WWII dead ("an imperialist army") and instead mourning the ~140,000 the US killed with the bomb — "the concentrated desperation of those who have seen so many human beings perish." The nuclear theme that frames the episode. `[00:00:00]`

## The quarantine `[00:07:11]`
- **Oct 22** — JFK reveals the missiles. The key rhetorical move: calling Cuba's missiles "**offensive**" while the US Jupiters in Turkey were "defensive" — even though the Soviet/Cuban deployment was *legal* and the US **blockade was illegal**. Cuba mobilized 350,000 and was **excluded from the negotiations** (the US spoke only to Moscow). `[00:07:11]`
- Khrushchev was actually relieved (he read the speech as JFK buying time); he raced the nuclear-laden **Alexandrovsk** to dock (it beat the blockade by hours), turned back the trailing ships, and slept on his office couch. "If the US insists on war, we'll all meet in hell." `[00:12:35]`

## The knot of war `[00:18:30]`
- **Oct 25** — SAC went to nuclear alert; Pentagon radio chatter suggested an invasion. **Oct 26** — Khrushchev's long, emotional letter (the "**knot of war**"): an offer to remove the missiles in exchange for a US **non-invasion pledge**. Castro, now convinced an attack was imminent (20:1 odds), prepared to fire on US overflights. `[00:18:30]`
- **Oct 27 ("Black Saturday")** `[00:27:00]` — escalation: Castro's letter urging Khrushchev to launch a first strike *if* the US invaded; Khrushchev's **second, public letter** (over Radio Moscow) raising the price to a **Jupiter trade** (Turkey for Cuba) — giving ExComm heartburn. A **U-2 was shot down over Cuba** (Maj. Rudolf Anderson killed) by a Soviet officer on the ground. JFK, nearly alone, saw the Jupiter trade as fair ("any rational man… it will look like a very fair trade") and sent **RFK to Dobrynin** to accept it — on condition it stay **secret**. `[00:27:00]`
- **The submarine** `[00:38:30]`: surrounded by the US Navy and pounded by signaling depth charges, the cut-off, sweltering **B-59** (armed with a nuclear torpedo) nearly fired — Captain Savitsky and the commissar were ready, but second captain **Vasily Arkhipov refused to sign off**, almost certainly preventing the first nuclear exchange in history (a deed kept secret for decades). `[00:38:30]`

## The deal `[00:41:33]`
- **Oct 28** — Khrushchev accepted (a public radio letter + a private one confirming the secret Jupiter removal); he told Castro to stop shooting at planes. The Joint Chiefs were **furious** ("we've been had"; LeMay demanded to see JFK). `[00:41:33]`
- **Castro, sidelined** `[00:43:33]`: he learned of the deal *over the radio* and was stung at not being consulted; the Jupiter half was kept secret even from him, making it look like Moscow caved. He issued **Cuba's own terms** (Oct 29): end the blockade, the embargo, Mongoose, the overflights, and **Guantánamo** — and absolutely **no on-site inspections** (rightly fearing a spy bonanza, as the US would later run in 1990s Iraq). `[00:43:33]`

## The cleanup `[00:49:48]`
- **Mikoyan** was sent to Havana to sell Cuba on the withdrawal (his wife died mid-mission; he stayed). The US added a last demand — remove the **IL-28 bombers** too. In the end: inspections dropped, the IL-28s left (on the USSR's schedule), the **blockade lifted** — but the **embargo and Guantánamo remained** (to this day). The last **tactical nukes** left on **Christmas Day 1962**. Khrushchev's letters to Castro framed it as a victory (the US pledged not to invade); Che to Mikoyan: "you offended our feelings… you as if recognized the right of the USA to violate international law." `[00:49:48]`

## Historiography & aftermath `[01:00:00]`
- The canonical account — Graham Allison's 1971 *Essence of Decision* — framed the USSR as spoiling for a fight. Cold War historian **Jim Hershberg**'s 1990s critique (drawing on declassified docs) showed the **US invasion planning was real and serious** before the missiles were even found — vindicating the "defense of Cuba" motive Khrushchev always claimed. `[01:00:00]`
- Exile attacks continued; the Kennedys began **reining in the hardline groups** (DRE, Alpha 66 — handled by the CIA's "Maurice Bishop"). And in **1963**, something unthinkable would begin: a back channel toward **normalizing relations** with Castro. `[01:02:00]`
