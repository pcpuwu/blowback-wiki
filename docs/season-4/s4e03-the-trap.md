## The storming of Amin's palace `[00:03:02]`
- **Christmas Eve, 1979** — as a Soviet interpreter fried potatoes, the guns lit up. Amin had retreated to a heavily defended hideout; the troops were told he'd "betrayed the April Revolution… was in contact with the CIA" and had to be eliminated. In a darkly comic turn, Amin collapsed at lunch (a botched KGB poisoning) and was *saved by Soviet doctors* who didn't know the KGB had tried to kill him. The Muslim Battalion (Soviet Central Asians) led a chaotic assault (a driver fled, friendly fire avoided only by Russian swearing identifying comrades; grenades hurled at "Amin's people" in an elevator shaft turned out to be the palace cat). Amin was found dead, rolled in a carpet, buried in a secret grave. The Kremlin had tried "to the very last minute" to quietly remove him instead. `[00:03:02]`
- **The deployment** `[00:07:01]`: ~80,000 troops in via the ring road; officers grumbled it was nowhere near enough (experts later said 30–35 divisions were needed). Soldiers were told they were protecting the Afghan people "before the Americans did" — but the US considered itself already there. The mujahideen were already getting cash/training/guns from the US, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, and China; recruiting ran through charities like the **Al-Kifah Refugee Center in Brooklyn** (training for assassinations, bombings, terror worldwide). 1979–82 the CIA program was modest (hundreds of thousands, not yet billions) — the mission was to "bleed them dry," and Howard Hart was "the first CIA officer ever given the mandate to kill… the troops of the Red Army." `[00:07:01]`

## The morning after `[00:09:09]`
- Afghans woke to a new government and celebrated Amin's overthrow (no civilian casualties in the palace assault); **Babrak Karmal** (the gradualist, Soviet-friendly alternative) led a "day of mourning," opened the prisons (thousands poured out, greeting soldiers as "liberators"). But the Politburo was already scheming to *get out* — "secure the towns and roads… withdraw as soon as the Afghan government… could take over." A local told a soldier: "we are glad to see you, but you will be very well advised to leave again as soon as you can." `[00:09:09]`

## The American reaction `[00:13:05]`
- The US condemned the invasion within hours — but the logic was odd: it had earlier called the 1978 revolution a "Soviet coup" and Amin "hopelessly pro-Soviet," yet now Carter mourned Amin's regime as "struggling to retain a modicum of independence." Garthoff: both the Soviet line (Amin invited them to overthrow him) and Carter's "posthumous promotion of Amin as an independent leader" were "equal departures from reality." The US UN resolution made no mention of the CIA-funded army inside Afghanistan/Pakistan. The reprisals: a **grain embargo** (compensating US farmers), the **Moscow Olympics boycott** (only China, Japan, West Germany, Canada fully joined), Brzezinski's trip to Zia (all aid to flow *through Pakistan* — eventually $4.2B), and the **Carter Doctrine** (any attempt to control the Persian Gulf "will be repelled by any means necessary, including military force"). `[00:13:05]`

## The propaganda war `[00:17:01]`
- Like Waugh's *Scoop*, US journalists manufactured the story. Reporter **Philip Bonosky** arrived in Kabul in 1980 expecting streets "choked with Russian tanks" — and saw essentially *one* (Soviet troops had withdrawn to barracks). Western reporters were "herded" — debriefed at the embassy, then filing on cue. The papers ran **fabricated obituaries**: VP Sultan Ali Keshtmand "died" (he hadn't), Mohammad Rafi repeatedly "assassinated," Education Minister **Anahita Ratebzad** "shot to death" (alive). The WaPo front-paged airport "fighting" that "never took place"; the London Telegraph noted the US embassy "consistently putting out exaggerated reports of rebel victories." Honest CBS reporters **Fitzgerald and Gould** found their nuanced footage spiked (CBS aired only "Soviet troops we did not see," cutting the Afghan president's withdrawal conditions); ABC's *Nightline* relegated them to a post-midnight panel against a Hekmatyar official Koppel called "an anti-communist resistance group." `[00:17:01]`
- **Schools as targets** `[00:23:34]`: Ratebzad's education programs (the first high-ranking woman in government) made teachers, schools, and pupils favorite mujahideen targets — school buses attacked, children killed, ~1,500 schools burned since Dec 1979. Even the NYT quoted a mujahideen camp head: "we killed the teacher, who was a communist, and fled." Meanwhile Brzezinski pushed **nuclear-war-fighting** directives (PD-59: win a months-long nuclear war). `[00:23:34]`

## The Soviet quagmire `[00:27:00]`
- The Soviets dug in (a withdrawal would destroy credibility and expose retreating troops). Their generals had "no recent experience" of counterinsurgency in mountains — "a slow, confusing, exhausting slog" of villages taken, retaken, lost again, with heavy civilian death. A 1985 Red Army booklet enjoined respect for Afghan customs, women, and a ban on narcotics — "but in a guerrilla war… the manual was just a manual" (2,500+ Soviet soldiers eventually imprisoned, 200+ for premeditated murder). Atrocities ran both ways: a mujahideen leader boasted of half-skinning Russian prisoners and leaving them booby-trapped; a Soviet soldier described killing an old woman and children with grenades. A rebel: "I prefer to sell my enemy for cash." `[00:27:00]`

## The CIA's "dirty dozen" `[00:31:02]`
- Inside CIA HQ: a doctored photo of a Soviet tank before Parliament — "This year Afghanistan, next year London." By 1982 the Near East Division belonged to **Gus Avrakotos** ("Why not come upstairs? We are killing Russians"), who ran a small office, the "dirty dozen": **Dwayne** the polio-stricken analyst; **Larry** the lawyer-consigliere (who'd reframe "terrorist devices… assassination techniques" as legal); **"Hilly-billy"** who moved black-market money (AK-47s driven from $299 → under $100 via Egyptian then Chinese production); **Art Alper** of the Office of Technical Services ("ever more lethal ways of killing Russians"). "I took people no one wanted," Avrakotos said. `[00:31:02]`

## "Gunga Dan" and "Russia's Vietnam" `[00:35:25]`
- **April 1980** — CBS's **Dan Rather** smuggled himself into Afghanistan dressed as a mujahid ("Gunga Dan"); critic Tom Shales: "punchy, crunchy… essentially uninformative," its theme that "ill-equipped resistance fighters desperately need American arms." Darker: Rather stood by as a mujahid executed a captured Afghan soldier for the camera (he was later tried in absentia in Afghanistan and found guilty of complicity in murder), and told viewers "no country is providing arms" to the fighters. The "**Russia's own Vietnam**" framing was conceived inside US intelligence (USIA's Alvin Snyder: "the American government's made-for-TV movie") and propagated via Radio Free Kabul and NGOs (the IRC, Freedom House, the Committee for a Free Afghanistan) with boards featuring Brzezinski and Bill Casey. `[00:35:25]`

## Charlie Wilson & Joanne Herring `[00:39:25]`
- **Charlie Wilson** saw Rather's report, thought of the Alamo, called the defense subcommittee ("How much are we giving the Afghans?" "Five million." "Double it"). His path ran through Texas socialite **Joanne Herring** — "Scarlett O'Hara, Zsa Zsa Gabor, and Dolly Parton," a card-carrying reactionary (the "Minute Women") whose toga parties featured "Nubian slaves" and the whole Safari Club; she fundraised for Angola's **Jonas Savimbi** and fell for **Zia** (even as he reimposed fundamentalist restrictions on women). `[00:39:25]`
- **The October Surprise & visa pipeline** `[00:42:44]`: before the 1980 election, the Reagan campaign and Israel colluded with Iran to delay the hostages' return (BCCI processing Israel→Iran arms). Michael Springmann (US visa bureau, Jeddah) recalled being ordered to issue visas to unqualified applicants — "an effort to bring recruits, rounded up by Osama bin Laden, to the United States for terrorist training by the CIA." `[00:42:44]`

## Zia's Pakistan `[00:44:00]`
- **Zia ul-Haq** (1977 coup, hanged predecessor Bhutto): military rule, death to socialism, Islamic law, a nuclear bomb, closer US ties. Carter's $400M offer was "peanuts" to Zia; Reagan upped it 10× to **$4.2B**. Madrasas exploded from 900 (1971) to ~8,000 official + ~25,000 unregistered (1988), funded by Gulf patrons. **1982** — Herring's Houston gala for Zia (she defended his hanging of Bhutto to wincing guests); Wilson floated to Zia: would Pakistan deal with the Israelis? (It did — a deadly secret; a 1985 Pakistani journalist who spotted Israeli advisors in Peshawar was captured by the ISI-alerted mujahideen and killed.) `[00:44:00]`

## Wilson, Avrakotos, and Operation Cyclone `[00:49:14]`
- Wilson wasn't central (Casey backed the op more each year) but crucial — "as a canny politician and effectively a CIA asset," he legitimized the mujahideen in Congress ("a cause only slightly below Christianity… told the conservatives it was time we fucked the Russians, told the liberals it would prove they were against communism"). Blue-blood Howard Hart found him "repugnant," but Avrakotos took a shine to him — "Wilson the pitchman, Avrakotos the enforcer," both wanting it bigger. 1983's $15M (hidden in an Air Force appropriation) + matching Saudi funds → $30M, ballooning by the late 80s into **Operation Cyclone**, "the most vast and expensive CIA operation in the institution's history." `[00:49:14]`

## The WMD smear `[00:51:38]`
- "**Yellow rain**" — chemical-weapons allegations (first via Rather, from mujahideen/Pakistani sources). Sept 1981 Haig claimed Soviet chemical use in Laos, Cambodia, Afghanistan; the State Department's "smoking gun" had "no evidence." The evidence never arrived (whereas US Agent Orange in Vietnam is thoroughly documented). Meanwhile the US was spending $1B+ to *build new nerve-gas facilities*, citing "reported use… by Soviet troops." A CIA official: "I don't see anything wrong with letting that rumor run." `[00:51:38]`

## Life inside Afghanistan `[00:54:13]`
- In the countryside: death by Soviet Mi-24 gunship, a CIA bicycle bomb, or a warlord's campaign against secular/Islamic education. In the cities, especially Kabul, life felt "eerily normal" (Braithwaite, Jonathan Steele): women students without headscarves, jeans and short skirts, professors and doctors, wedding parties, thriving markets — Soviet advisors dug ditches, ran mines, built clinics ("tangible benefits… poor compensation… for the carnage"). Ahmed Rashid: 3–4 million refugees fled — "the first major refugee crisis of the Cold War." `[00:54:13]`

## The Soviets wanted out — the US said no `[00:58:35]`
- **1983** — a Soviet official told US negotiator Roger Fisher: "We're not stupid… We want to go home" (calling the invasion a mistake), proposing a face-saving phased withdrawal if the US held back mujahideen support. Brezhnev had ordered partial withdrawals as early as May 1980; Andropov (KGB chief, soon General Secretary) pushed UN peace talks. But the US gave Pakistan "zero encouragement to talk to the Soviets about peace" — "the Safari Club was just getting started." `[00:58:35]`
