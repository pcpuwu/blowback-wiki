## Cold open: Hekmatyar returns `[00:00:01]`
- **May 2017** — after 20 years on the run, the "**Butcher of Kabul**," **Gulbuddin Hekmatyar** (the cruelest mujahid, CIA/ISI favorite who'd rocketed his own capital and deployed suicide bombers against Kabul), strode back as an "elder statesman." He'd negotiated amnesty; the UN cleared him from sanctions; the US praised his "rehabilitation." A bereaved Kabul resident: "He is the killer of the people of Afghanistan." A cucumber salesman captured the mood: "all the warlords are corrupt and have blood on their hands… but we welcome [him] because we are tired of war." **Peace at any price.** `[00:00:01]`

## The Taliban's open letter `[00:04:56]`
- **Feb 2018** — the Taliban sent an open letter to the American people offering to negotiate, calling the war "aimless slaughter and tons and tons of heroin" (a Western official: "they have started to hit where it hurts, simply by telling the truth"). **Trump** thought the war "a loser" and took a personal interest in getting out via a deal with the Taliban — the case Holbrooke and Clinton had made to Obama, who wasn't convinced. `[00:04:56]`

## Mullah Omar is dead `[00:07:15]`
- **Mullah Omar** had actually died of TB in a Pakistani hospital in **2013** — the Taliban hid it, "puppeting Omar's corpse" with statements for years (even saying in April 2015 he was alive and backed peace talks), before confessing in late 2015. The succession: **Mullah Baradar** (the military head, secretly talking to Ahmed Wali Karzai — the CIA had the ISI capture him in 2010; the ISI then refused to let the CIA interrogate him and jailed him); **Mullah Mansour** (administrator, "famed perfume shopping trips to Dubai," killed by a US drone in 2016 with quiet Pakistani cooperation); and finally **Mullah Hibatullah** (a Kandahar disciplinarian who'd pushed Omar to adopt suicide bombing) — under whom the Taliban formed a team to negotiate in Doha. `[00:07:15]`

## Trump escalates, then deals `[00:12:48]`
- Trump ("I could win that war in a week… I just don't want to kill 10 million people") first "adopted an aggressive posture," and casualties skyrocketed. **Spring 2017** — the **"Mother of All Bombs"** (MOAB, $16M, the largest non-nuclear weapon) dropped on **ISK** (~700 fighters) near the Pakistani border — condemned by both Karzai ("a testing ground for new and dangerous weapons") and the Taliban. Civilian deaths from US airstrikes rose **~330%** from 2016 to 2019 (Brown University), mostly from drones. But the stalemate held, and everyone moved toward the table; the Taliban earned credit for an Eid 2018 ceasefire (Trump shouting "Where is my deal?"). `[00:12:48]`

## The Doha deal `[00:18:06]`
- The man: **Zalmay Khalilzad**; the place: **Doha**, Qatar. The Afghan **government was excluded** — both sides agreed the US and Taliban had to make peace first (the Americans being "the only thing stopping" the Taliban from overwhelming Kabul). Pakistan was happy to help — "Islamabad's final victory in a project that went all the way back to the handshake between Brzezinski and General Zia" (ousting India). A surreal March 2019 meeting: ex-Guantanamo inmates across from the US/NATO commander, who said "we could keep fighting… or together we could kill ISIS." The **US–Taliban deal was signed Feb 29, 2020** (Pompeo and Khalilzad shaking hands with Baradar) — full troop withdrawal in 14 months + a secret prisoner swap (5,000 Taliban/al-Qaeda for Afghan personnel). Critics say Trump prioritized the deadline over leverage, but Abbas: "the general fact of American defeat after 20 years… was all but guaranteed." `[00:18:06]`

## Ghani's denial and Biden's withdrawal `[00:23:34]`
- **Ashraf Ghani** "stalled things immediately… wanted peace on his terms only," so out of touch he reportedly didn't believe the US would actually leave. **April 14, 2021** — **Biden** announced full withdrawal by September ("time to end the forever war"). Ahmed Rashid: the withdrawal was "necessary policy… carried out in a disastrous manner… a total disaster." Tariq Ali: Biden was "simply ratifying the peace process initiated by Trump." `[00:23:34]`
- **The fall of Kabul** `[00:27:54]`: mid-August — Ghani, imagining a Taliban–Karzai conspiracy, sent feelers to the Haqqani network; the word back was "give up and get out." His protection force melted away (guards *paid to step aside*); he fled by helicopter to Uzbekistan, then the UAE ("I did not want to give the Taliban… the pleasure of yet again humiliating an Afghan president" — the memory of Najibullah fresh). The 300,000-man army crumbled in 11 days (many defecting to the Taliban). Unlike the orderly Soviet withdrawal that left a functioning government, the US exit was "pure chaos"; the evacuation (cramped into weeks because the White House feared signaling "a lack of faith") left ~200,000 US-allied Afghans behind; 13 US service members (and ~170 Afghans) died in a suicide bombing. Biden: "I bear responsibility… but the former president made a deal with the Taliban." `[00:27:54]`

## The cast's fates `[00:33:20]`
- **Zawahiri** (al-Qaeda's leader after bin Laden) — killed by a CIA drone in 2022 in a "Taliban-supported safehouse in downtown Kabul." `[00:33:20]`
- **Hekmatyar** — still playing kingmaker (his office attacked by suicide bombers in 2020); Hekmatyar fondly recalled Charlie Wilson as "a good friend." **Dostum** — fled to Uzbekistan/Turkey, his palace raided by the Taliban (a young fighter asleep beneath giant fish tanks). `[00:33:20]`
- **Brzezinski** (the jihad's prime architect) — died 2017, his "geostrategic blueprint" alive in the Ukraine policy (his son ambassador to Poland, daughter Mika an MSNBC anchor). **Avrakotos** — wrote a newsletter for Murdoch, died 2005. **Vickers** — a counterterror advisor under Obama, now on the board of arms dealer BAE (with Gina Haspel). **Bill Casey** — died in 1987 the day after being named in the Iran-Contra hearings; verbally lashed by a bishop at his own funeral. **Musharraf** — chased out of Pakistan, convicted of high treason in absentia (death sentence later revoked), died Feb 2023. **Jalaluddin Haqqani** — died secretly (2015–18), leaving the business to his Taliban-leader sons. **Charlie Wilson** — became a *lobbyist for Pakistan*; died 2010 (a UT-Austin chair in Pakistan studies bears his name; a plaque in his home quotes a 19th-c. emir: "never trust the Russians"). `[00:36:00]`

## The reckoning `[00:42:26]`
- Since 2021 the US has **frozen billions in Afghan central bank assets** — 43% of Afghans living on <1 meal/day, 97% projected below the poverty line (reports of selling daughters and kidneys). Malalai Joya's old line: Afghans have "three enemies — the Taliban, this group of warlords disguised as government that the US supports, and the US occupation… if you could get the US occupation out, we'd only have two." Tariq Ali on the legacy: billions spent air-conditioning barracks while a huge slum grew in Kabul; land contaminated with unexploded ordnance; no reliable figures on the sex-work industry or rape; one in ten young Afghans now opium addicts. `[00:42:26]`
- **The body counts** `[00:46:50]`: hard to state — much violence was disguised local disputes; the US never issued an official tally. Brown University's Costs of War: **~243,000 killed** in the Afghanistan-Pakistan war zone since 2001. `[00:46:50]`
- **The new great game** `[00:48:09]`: a July 2021 Taliban delegation visited China (pledging Afghanistan wouldn't be a launchpad); "Beijing will replace Washington as the capital of importance for Afghanistan." Critics say the US is punishing Afghans to undermine the Taliban — but Joya alleges worse: the US "funding the Taliban to the tune of $14 million a week… in the name of so-called humanitarian support" (NBC, April 2023: the US watchdog "cannot assure… we are not currently funding the Taliban"). The Haqqani paradox: Sirajuddin Haqqani, with "a huge amount of American blood on his hands," is now someone Western envoys "think they can work with." `[00:48:09]`

## The snake eats its tail `[00:53:34]`
- Andrew Cockburn: "in a sane world… 9/11 might have permanently ended Washington's… taste for mixing Islam with politics. But old habits die hard." The Iraq War drove "the biggest global recruitment drive of jihadism since Operation Cyclone"; in **Syria**, the US backed rebel coalitions led by al-Qaeda's branch (Jabhat al-Nusra) and Ahrar al-Sham (inspired by Afghan-Arab Abdullah Azzam) — a CIA-backed fighter explaining Nusra "allowed militias vetted by the United States to appear independent" to keep getting supplies; a White House official: "a turnover of weapons is probably unavoidable… I'm fatalistic" — echoing Milt Bearden's 1980s line, "once the stuff's delivered, we lose all control over it." Joya: "terrorism itself was a strategic tool in the hand of the US and NATO… Afghanistan, Iraq, Syria, Yemen, Libya — and now Ukraine." `[00:53:34]`
- **The Ukraine ouroboros** `[00:56:13]`: the US bought Russian **Mi-17** helicopters for the Afghan government in the 2010s — and after the 2021 collapse and Russia's 2022 Ukraine invasion, transferred those same helicopters to **Kyiv**. "Forty years after the US sent billions to Islamic warriors fighting the Russians in Afghanistan, it now sends Russian-made helicopters — purchased to kill Islamic warriors fighting Americans in Afghanistan — to kill Russians in a proxy war inside Ukraine." `[00:56:13]`
