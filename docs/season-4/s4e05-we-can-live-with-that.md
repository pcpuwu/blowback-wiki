# Blowback S4E5 — "We Can Live With That"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s4e05-we-can-live-with-that.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:03:30
> **Thesis:** The interregnum (1989–96): Najibullah's communist government outlasts everyone's predictions; the USSR dies; the victorious warlords turn on each other and shred Kabul (the "small Holocaust"); and out of that chaos rise the **Taliban** — whom the US, chasing a Central Asian gas pipeline, decides it "can live with." Meanwhile the Afghan-Arab network blows back onto US soil (the Kahane murder, the 1993 WTC bombing) and bin Laden declares war.

## Cold open: tea for the Taliban `[00:00:04]`
- **February 1997** — the State Department hosted Taliban envoys to discuss a **Unocal** gas pipeline, despite women's-rights protests over the Clinton administration's cordial relations with a regime that had erased women's rights. A senior US diplomat's verdict: "The Taliban will probably develop like the Saudis did. There will be pipelines, an emir, no parliament, and lots of Sharia law. **We can live with that.**" `[00:00:04]`
- Roadmap: Najibullah's surprising survival, the warlord takeover, the civil war that birthed the Taliban. A US special envoy's prophetic cable: a Hekmatyar seizure of Kabul would have Arab extremists "stoking Islamic radicalism… including the Soviet Central Asian republics." `[00:02:01]`

## Najibullah hangs on `[00:03:37]`
- **March 1989** — President **Najibullah** ("a competent and pragmatic communist") faced a nation ringed by foreign-financed warlords, but proved "the Afghan leader with nine lives." The Soviets left billions in weapons and 250,000 tons/year of wheat; his priority was keeping the mujahideen out of Kabul. Bin Laden returned to Saudi Arabia a "pious warrior," his anti-Western sermons passed around on cassette. `[00:03:37]`
- **Jalalabad** `[00:06:43]`: weeks after withdrawal, the warlords and Pakistan launched an assault on **Jalalabad** (the CIA buying hundreds of Toyota trucks for it) — a *disaster for the warlords*, repelled by Najibullah's forces. "The regime was going to survive longer than others anticipated." By winter, Afghanistan lurched toward civil war. `[00:06:43]`

## The USSR collapses `[00:07:33]`
- *Rolling Stone* (July 1990): "Anarchy in the USSR." The Soviet bloc was in freefall (Gorbachev's reforms "cannibalized government revenue"). H.W. Bush declined a $250B "grand bargain" aid package — pennies on the dollar instead — while Gorbachev naively expected American support. "What Gorbachev failed to understand was that the Cold War was still on." `[00:07:33]`
- **The interim government** `[00:09:39]`: Feb 1989 — seven mujahideen groups formed a Saudi-funded "shotgun marriage" interim government dominated by **Hekmatyar** (excluding Shia and Massoud), swollen with thousands of foreign fighters. `[00:09:39]`

## 1991–92: the lifeline cut `[00:13:23]`
- **1991** turning point — Massoud won chunks of the north; Soviet aid deliveries fell short as the USSR broke down; **James Baker** negotiated an end to Soviet assistance. US spoils from the Gulf War against Saddam were "captured and shipped to the Mujahideen" (the first "unholy bond" between the Iraq and Afghanistan wars). Bin Laden, having denounced King Fahd for inviting US troops onto holy land, was clamped down on and slipped to **Sudan** (1991–92), officially renounced — though Saudi-funded banks (some BCCI-linked) kept transferring him money. **1992** — the USSR ceased to exist. `[00:13:23]`
- **Najibullah falls** `[00:17:01]`: diplomat Peter Tomsen's cables (and ex-warlord Abdul Haq's warning) foresaw "50 or more separate kingdoms… a training ground and munitions dump for foreign terrorists… the world's largest poppy field." **March 18, 1992** — Najibullah resigned (officially April 15), arrested by his own ex-general, the warlord **Rashid Dostum**. His own prophecy: "If fundamentalism comes to Afghanistan… [it] will turn into a center of world smuggling for narcotic drugs… a center for terrorism." Cole: "Najibullah could see the future, but there was no one to listen" — the US had announced "hands off." `[00:17:01]`

## The battle for Kabul `[00:19:43]`
- The "Seven" alliance was worthless. Two rivals closed on Kabul: **Hekmatyar** (from the south, with ISI/CIA/Saudi backing and Arab jihadists, including bin Laden urging him to share power — he refused) and **Massoud** (from the north). The night before Hekmatyar's "imagined triumph," **Massoud** seized the airport (bribing ex-communists, flying in Dostum's Uzbek militia) and out-generaled him, entering Kabul "on a tank strewn with flowers." Enraged, Hekmatyar "began to lob rockets blindly at Kabul." A new mujahideen government formed under President **Burhanuddin Rabbani** — "for the first time in 300 years, the Pashtuns had lost control of the capital." `[00:19:43]`

## The warlord years (1992–96) `[00:24:20]`
- Afghanistan fractured into private kingdoms, armies, drug operations, and shakedown rackets. Ahmed Rashid: "the most destructive… Kabul itself was almost destroyed," birthing al-Qaeda and other extremist groups under the warlords' umbrella (Saudis backing Pashtuns to "knock out the Iranians," Iran backing the Shia Hazaras to "knock out the Saudis"). Malalai Joya: "they raped even young girls and grandmothers… more than 65,000 people they killed in Kabul… even 'the small Holocaust' is not enough to explain." `[00:24:20]`
- **The Stinger blowback** `[00:27:46]`: the US packed up (except a **$65M campaign to buy back Stingers**), but the missiles had already dispersed worldwide (Iran, Chechnya, Algeria, even a Medellín cartel attempt). By 1992 there were "more personal weapons in Afghanistan than in India and Pakistan combined." Civilians starved (500,000 in Kabul on food coupons); Sayyaf's 1993 **Afshar** campaign ("repeated human butchery") slaughtered Hazaras; warlords carved out fiefdoms (Dostum north, Ismail Khan in Herat, the drug lords in Helmand). `[00:27:46]`

## Blowback on US soil `[00:33:34]`
- The US covered the tracks of its old clients at the **Al-Kifah Center in Brooklyn**. Investigators of the 1990 murder of right-winger **Meir Kahane** found Fort Bragg Special Warfare manuals, classified Joint Chiefs documents, and maps of NYC landmarks (the WTC, Statue of Liberty) — plus the Blind Sheikh **Omar Abdel-Rahman**'s sermons. Prosecutors narrowed it to the gunman **El Sayyid Nosair**, sparing scrutiny of his trainer **Ali Mohamed** (US Special Forces + FBI informant). `[00:33:34]`
- **The 1993 WTC bombing** `[00:35:36]`: the FBI's own informant **Emad Salem** had recorded discussions of substituting harmless powder for the explosives — they didn't, and six died. Bomber **Ramzi Yousef** (caught Feb 1995 in Islamabad, in a bin Laden-funded guesthouse) was cagey; a business card linked him to bin Laden's relative **Mohammed Jamal Khalifa**. Yousef's circle had discussed flying an explosives-filled plane into CIA HQ and attacking a nuclear facility — funded by a man who "knew a guy who knew a guy": **Khalid Sheikh Mohammed**. `[00:35:36]`

## The Taliban emerge `[00:38:36]`
- A new generation: boys raised in refugee camps and madrasas who'd "never seen their country at peace," with no memory of tribe or elders — "what the war in the 1980s had thrown up." Their leaders were actual anti-Soviet veterans, "probably the most disfigured and disabled set of commanders in the entire world." Their creed came from the reformist **Deobandi** strain, supercharged by Zia's madrasa funding (a far more extreme version than the original Deobandis would recognize). To some secular Afghans (like one **Hamid Karzai**) they looked like a cleansing force against the warlords. `[00:38:36]`
- **Mullah Omar** `[00:43:43]`: the one-eyed, shy, dry-witted leader (orphaned, raised by a village mullah). The origin story: spring 1994 — Omar's 30 Taliban (16 rifles) freed two girls a commander had abducted and raped, hanging him from a tank barrel; a later dispute over sodomizing a boy added to his "Robin Hood" prestige (he "asked for no reward"). Many in the region believed the **ISI** organized the Taliban ("all Taliban are ISI Taliban"; Omar "so stupid, it was easy for the ISI to use him"). `[00:43:43]`
- **The conquest** `[00:46:41]`: Oct 1994 — the Taliban raided an ISI convoy, then took **Kandahar** (shooting the commander, hanging his body from a tank barrel); 12,000 students joined by year's end. Backed by Pakistan, they imposed "the strictest interpretation of Sharia ever seen in the Muslim world," neutralized Hekmatyar, and reinvested their drug/toll spoils. `[00:46:41]`

## The fall of Kabul `[00:48:54]`
- **1996** — Rabbani toured Asia for support; even Hekmatyar joined the government, accelerating the Taliban assault. Saudi and Pakistani money bribed rival warlords (up to $10M) to let the Taliban through. **Sept 26, 1996** — the Taliban stormed Kabul. Massoud fled north; **Najibullah** (who refused Massoud's evacuation offer out of Pashtun pride) was castrated, dragged behind a jeep, shot, and hanged with his brother from a traffic post. Mullah Omar's men — in their CIA-bought Toyota pickups — controlled Kabul as the new **Islamic Emirate of Afghanistan**. `[00:48:54]`

## The business of the Emirate `[00:51:09]`
- **Drugs** `[00:51:50]`: despite the pious message and Pakistan's implausible show-raids, the Taliban kept poppy production alive — Afghanistan would double output to **75% of the world's heroin** (McCoy). `[00:51:50]`
- **PR & pipelines** `[00:53:22]`: the Taliban had powerful Washington advocates — chiefly **Lely Helms** (niece of ex-CIA director Richard Helms), who arranged broadcasts and meetings for them through Sept 2001. A State Department bureaucrat: "you find that they really have a great sense of humor." The prize: Central Asian gas (Turkmenistan, landlocked) — the US and **Unocal** courting the Taliban for a pipeline to Pakistan/India (against Russian and Iranian interests). **Zalmay Khalilzad** (RAND, later the top US agent in Afghanistan): "the Taliban does not practice the anti-US style of fundamentalism practiced by Iran. It is closer to the Saudi model." A bidding war (Unocal vs. Bridas) opened. Malalai Joya described teaching girls underground, hiding books under her burqa. `[00:53:22]`

## Bin Laden declares war `[00:59:23]`
- **1992–96** — bin Laden in Sudan became part of the elite ("a hero in those days" — Turabi), building "the beginnings of a multinational private army" (CIA: the "Ford Foundation of Sunni Islamic Terrorism"). The CIA created "**Alec Station**" (the Get-Bin-Laden team) in early 1996. Sudan, under pressure, may or may not have offered to hand him over (disputed); **summer 1996** — bin Laden flew back to Jalalabad. About a week later, in Nangarhar, the 40-year-old declared the **June 1996 killing of 19 Americans in Saudi Arabia** "the beginning of war between Muslims and the United States." `[00:59:23]`
