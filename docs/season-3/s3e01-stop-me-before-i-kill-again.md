## Cold open `[00:00:23]`
- Rumsfeld on North Korea — the "stop me before I kill again" framing that blames the regime's existence on its own inexplicable evil, with no US history attached. `[00:00:23]`

## "Forgotten" vs "unknown" `[00:07:31]`
- The Korean War is branded the "**forgotten war**" — a label that, the hosts argue, lets Americans avert their eyes from what actually happened (even MASH, the most famous Korea media, was really about Vietnam). Historian **Bruce Cumings** calls it the "**unknown war**." North Korea is presented as both ultimate evil and a ridiculous basket case, with no explanation of *why* there's a North and South Korea or of America's role. `[00:07:31]`
- **Deconstructing the demonization** `[00:10:00]`: the famous "lights at night" satellite image (North dark, South bright) that Rumsfeld used is a *constructed* composite, not a naked-eye view (scholar **Suzy Kim**) — and it smuggles in the premise that energy-consumption = good and its absence = "tragedy." `[00:10:00]`

## The two histories `[00:13:09]`
- **The conventional story**: in summer 1950 the UN/US defended newborn South Korea after communist hordes (Stalin's proxies) crossed the 38th parallel — aggressive communist expansion, contained. `[00:13:09]`
- **The revisionist story** the show tells: the **38th parallel was an arbitrary line American officials drew** (meant to be temporary); *neither* Korean leadership nor the people saw it as legitimate, and **both** Kim Il-sung and Syngman Rhee wanted to reunify by force. Before June 1950, the US-backed South was already **massacring tens of thousands**, was run partly by **former Japanese collaborators**, while the North's communist leadership were respected **anti-Japanese guerrillas** carrying out a real **social revolution** (land, women's rights, labor, a modern state). The US arrival turned a Korean conflict that would have resolved internally into a three-year war that killed millions. `[00:13:09]`
- **The brutality question** `[00:19:15]`: Truman's "police action" cover; the show's recurring question of whether the US air war was **genocidal** (civilian-targeting). And the war's effect on *America*: it built the permanent **national security state**, an economy and politics organized around a Cold War that (per scholar Masuda Hajimu) was less a situation than a constructed **mindset** — fueling a **Red Scare** that targeted not just communists but civil-rights, labor, and women's-rights activists. `[00:19:15]`

## The cast `[00:25:04]`
- **Kim Il-sung** — anti-Japanese guerrilla in Manchuria (the Japanese ran a "get Kim squad"); *not* a Stalin/Mao puppet but "his own man," bent on reunifying a country artificially divided by the US in 1945 (US/South propaganda branded him an "imposter"). `[00:25:04]`
- **Syngman Rhee** — the US-educated exile (studied under Woodrow Wilson) who returned to build a corrupt anti-communist police state ("somewhere between Chiang Kai-shek and Mussolini"); a pain for the US but the only figure who could hold the South against a left-wing takeover. `[00:26:45]`
- **Dean Acheson** — the genteel architect ("prestige is the shadow cast by power"); his "great crescent" strategy linking Northeast Asia to the Middle East — yet still smeared as a "blue-blooded red" by hardliners. `[00:28:09]`
- **The China Lobby / Chiang Kai-shek** — the network backing the deposed Nationalist leader (fled to Taiwan after Mao's 1949 win) and pushing Truman toward aggression; some argue Chiang helped *instigate* the Korean War to save himself. `[00:29:43]`
- **Douglas MacArthur** — the right-wing darling (raised by "Pinky"), WWII Pacific commander, who would fight to expand the war into a final showdown with **China and the USSR** against Truman's wishes. (Recurring faces: Truman, LeMay, Dulles, Nixon, Khrushchev, JFK.) `[00:33:00]`

## The season ahead `[00:34:28]`
- A deep dive on Korean history → the **38th parallel** drawn at WWII's close → war breaks out summer 1950 → the **Truman vs. MacArthur** conflict (localize the war vs. take it to China) → new warfare: **firebombing** (Korea absorbed more US bombs in 3 years than all of WWII; **LeMay**) and the rehabilitation of Japan (war criminal **Shiro Ishii** of **Unit 731** protected, not prosecuted) → the stalemate and the MacArthur showdown. Guests: Suzy Kim, Bruce Cumings, Tim Shorrock, and others. `[00:34:28]`
