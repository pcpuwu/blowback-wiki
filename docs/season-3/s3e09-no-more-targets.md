## Cold open: how we take a position `[00:00:02]`
- A US war-film narration: air burns it in with napalm, artillery softens the survivors, then small arms — "this is Korea." `[00:00:02]`
- Roadmap: after the spring 1951 reversal and MacArthur's firing, the war enters its final phase — which "drags on for another two years, longer than the entirety of the war so far": a static line, talks dropping in and out, "an unrelenting air campaign [pummeling] what is left of North Korea out of existence." `[00:01:08]`

## The softball peace offer rejected `[00:03:34]`
- By late May the UN drove the communists back across the parallel into the "Iron Triangle" (Chorwon–Kumhwa–Pyongyang). **June 23, 1951** — Soviet UN ambassador **Jacob Malik** proposed peace: a "pure and simple military armistice in the field" — *omitting* the communist side's cherished terms (immediate troop withdrawal, Red China at the UN, return of Formosa). Stone: these were essentially Truman's own terms. Yet **Dean Rusk** (who'd drawn the parallel, now a China Lobby darling) and the US dismissed it. Congressional rejection was bipartisan-Republican — Thomas Dewey: "Every time Stalin smiles, beware." `[00:03:34]`
- **Peace itself was the threat** `[00:07:11]`: Marshall warned Congress against a "letdown" of military buildup if peace came; Truman (July 4) said "we face a long period of world tension"; the WaPo editorial "Peril of Communist Treachery" warned an armistice would let rearmament "subside" and even floated war with China. Cumings: Truman "had sacrificed MacArthur's command to further pursue the general's nuclear policy" — his absence revealed how much the elite had agreed with him. `[00:07:11]`

## Talks at Kaesong — sabotaged `[00:08:53]`
- Talks opened **July 10** at Kaesong, collapsed in two days (US pretext: barred reporters — which baffled even a Chicago Tribune correspondent: "a hell of a note to stop a peace conference over such petulant trivialities"). Resumed; the communists dropped the immediate-withdrawal demand. The fight was over the ceasefire line — the US *publicly* said the parallel was acceptable but privately never entertained it (MacArthur's lingering spirit — the parallel sacrosanct only when communists crossed it). By mid-August the communists offered to "accept the present battlefront as a ceasefire line" — a real concession since the US was now north of the parallel. "Reds seen yielding." `[00:08:53]`
- **The torpedo** `[00:11:24]`: just as peace seemed in sight, UN troops launched "one of the most devastating artillery bombardments of the Korean War," and armed men invaded the Kaesong neutral zone, ambushing a communist platoon and killing its commander; US planes shot up NK negotiators' jeeps. The US blamed its South Korean allies and mocked the communists' (accurate) complaints ("Ridgway warns of red trickery") — only in September did reporters discover, past censorship, that the Americans had indeed launched the attack. `[00:11:24]`

## The Japan treaty `[00:12:42]`
- The talks were torpedoed just before the **San Francisco** peace treaty with Japan (signed Sept 1951). Stone's theory: the US *needed* the Korean talks to fail to get what it wanted from the Japan treaty — "only the war made the treaty possible." The Dulles-crafted treaty re-armed Japan, denied victims reparations, kept US bases, disregarded the Yalta settlement, and forced Japan to recognize Chiang (and not even establish economic ties with the PRC). "The sudden burst of incidents in Kaesong at the end of August served to prevent incidents at San Francisco in September." A US/ROK plane then strafed the Kaesong neutral zone in October, killing a 12-year-old boy — the NYT called such killings "trumped up by communist forces." Talks moved to **Panmunjom**. `[00:12:42]`

## The press turns `[00:16:57]`
- Western reporters discovered the communists had told the truth about a US attack the UN called "inconclusive"; officers told Herald Tribune reporters their questions were "none of your business" and "Don't forget which side you're on." Correspondents were barred from officials and base camps without military handlers. Stone praised the two Western reporters on the *other* side (London's Daily Worker, Paris's Ce Soir) who "can more easily afford to tell the truth." Even the NYT noted "embellished adjectives had replaced facts." `[00:16:57]`
- **The Kaesong roadblock** `[00:19:27]`: talks resumed Oct 25; the communists offered the current battle line — so the US suddenly demanded Kaesong (communist-held, of "little military value") go to the South. GIs on the ground were now asking "why don't we have a ceasefire now?", some convinced "their own commanders… are throwing up blocks against an agreement." `[00:19:27]`
- **"There come atrocities to the rescue"** `[00:21:45]`: the US announced the communists had massacred 5,500 (then 6,000+) American captives ("Reds butchered more Americans than fell in 1776"); Ridgway called it God's way of revealing the enemy's moral character. But the story "faded out of the headlines within two weeks" — to be debunked later. `[00:21:45]`

## The POW front `[00:23:02]`
- The war was bloodiest after China entered; North Korea's entire civilian population dug into caves and an "underground great wall" of tunnels against Ridgway's bombing. The **POWs became a weapon of resistance** challenging the UN's legitimacy. Koje Island (~170,000) grew violent in 1952 after the "**voluntary repatriation**" policy forced Koreans to "choose north or south." `[00:23:02]`
- **The Dodd kidnapping** `[00:24:42]`: **May 7, 1952** — POWs of Compound 76 lured camp commander Brig. Gen. **Francis Dodd** to the gate and seized him ("We have captured Dodd. He will not be harmed if PW problems are resolved"). Patton and Sherman tanks rolled up ("20 tanks scare reds into freeing Dodd"); Truman decried the demand against forced repatriation. The prisoners released Dodd peacefully ("we were sorry that we had captured him against his will"). **June 10** — US paratroopers raided Compound 76, killing 31 and wounding 80+. Of 34 charged with mutiny, 10 were born in *South* Korea (Kim: they "did not fit the later narrative of fanatic communist North Koreans"); all born under Japanese colonial rule, likely veterans of anti-colonial resistance. `[00:24:42]`
- **The repatriation outcome** `[00:28:18]`: the effort failed — at the 1953 ceasefire only 7,800 of 76,000 NK POWs refused repatriation (the higher Chinese refusal owed to the recent civil war, not Korea). Repatriation had been the very issue that prolonged the war 18 months. `[00:28:18]`

## MacArthur's political collapse `[00:29:07]`
- His firing prompted "one of the most savage attacks of public opinion ever directed against the president"; his "retirement address" drew ovations (Hoover called him "a reincarnation of St. Paul"; Rep. Dewey Short: "we heard God speak here today"). Parades of millions; he checked into the Waldorf "like a medieval lord [receiving] vassals." `[00:29:07]`
- **The hearings** `[00:31:53]`: a congressional inquiry into his dismissal; LBJ helped shape the Democrats' approach — "give him enough rope to hang himself," letting MacArthur talk at length (a member marveled at his bladder: "he must have the bladder of a college boy"). He then toured the country (financed by Texas oilmen H.L. Hunt and Clint Murchison) on right-wing invective ahead of the 1952 GOP nomination. `[00:31:53]`

## Truman's steel-seizure crisis `[00:33:34]`
- Truman's approval sat at/below 30%. Amid 1950–51 inflation and the era's wave of strikes, the **steel** dispute deadlocked (companies wouldn't raise wages without a price hike; unions would strike). With no Attorney General (Jan–May 1952, corruption scandals), Truman **seized the steel mills** (April 8, 1952) under the Defense Production Act — setting off a constitutional crisis. The **Supreme Court ruled it unconstitutional (June 2)**; the steelworkers struck and won basically the wage increases they'd offered — "perhaps the biggest victory in American labor since Taft-Hartley." But Truman and the Democrats shared none of the rewards. `[00:33:34]`

## The atrocity story debunked `[00:37:47]`
- Stone dismantled Ridgway's massacre claims: 5,000 (Nov 14) → 6,000 (Nov 15) → "proof of only 365" (Nov 20) → an 8,000 figure (incl. other UN forces, which *reduced* the known American total). Truman called it "the most uncivilized thing… this century" — but added "if true." Stone: "sheer statistical slapstick… understandable if the purpose was merely to stir up hate and upset peace talks." `[00:37:47]`

## Dean and Burchett `[00:39:52]`
- Captured Gen. **William Dean** befriended **Wilfred Burchett** (the communist Ce Soir correspondent), who in early 1952 frankly briefed Dean on the war's whole course (Dean later verified it "quite accurate, the only bias being the method of telling, not the facts") — including that US aircraft had violated the Kaesong truce area. Dean: "I like Burchett, and I'm grateful to him… I'm also very sorry that he is where he is." `[00:39:52]`

## Eisenhower defeats MacArthur `[00:42:50]`
- **Eisenhower** (who privately considered MacArthur "a dangerous opportunist") entered the 1952 GOP race. MacArthur keynoted the convention in July — but the "coronation speech" was "a dud" that "tanked his campaign": shrill anti-Truman, anti-"socialistic" invective ("captive to the schemers and planners who have infiltrated its ranks") that made him "sound like a bitter crank." Voters "liked Ike" — Eisenhower steamrolled Adlai Stevenson (442–89). Stone after the Jan 1953 inauguration: Eisenhower "no fire-eater… a rather simple man who enjoys his bridge and his golf"; Congress "wants something done about Korea… All it wants is a miracle." `[00:42:50]`
- **Stone's warning to the left** `[00:48:52]`: don't be "as giddy as a punch-drunk fighter" — "a powerful wing of the Republican Party is against a settlement… the American military bureaucracy has been and continues to be opposed to a settlement"; Eisenhower contends with more than "a few wild men." `[00:48:52]`

## The air war / genocide `[00:50:08]`
- The one constant of the war was **American total air supremacy**. From the NK and US-general view this was no mere "stalemate" — the US "bombed North Korea into submission." Ridgway pushed for bigger napalm to "wipe out all life in tactical locality." A 1951 **RAND Corporation** essay ("Air Force Psychological Warfare in Korea") claimed the psychological effect was "unplanned and unanticipated" — not quite defensible, since LeMay had used massed bombardment to break Japanese morale (a city is "easier to burn down than to blow up"). Of 200 NK POWs, 43% said desertions occurred during air attacks. `[00:50:08]`
- **Tim Shorrock** `[00:54:13]`: MacArthur's own psy-war chief (Bonner Fellers) called the firebombing of Japan among "the most ruthless and barbaric killings of noncombatants in all history" — 66 Japanese cities, 40% of urban areas to ash. "In Korea, every city was destroyed… 100% reduced to ashes. There was no targets left." And the US bombed *South* Korea too (the 1990s Truth and Reconciliation Commission found napalm sprayed on islands near Inchon, killing hundreds of civilians — "that's how the US liberated Korea, with napalm"). `[00:54:13]`
- **Napalm** `[00:55:47]`: the Air Force's "wonder weapon," used in Korea "quietly for several years, with much more devastating effect" than Vietnam. A NYT correspondent described a napalmed village frozen in death ("a man about to get on his bicycle, 50 boys and girls playing in an orphanage, a housewife… holding a page torn from a Sears catalog"). Pilots "came back to their ships stinking of vomit." A friendly-fire victim's skin peeled "like fried potato chips." BBC's Rene Cutforth's napalm pieces went unaired ("almost nobody… had heard of Napalm"); his description of a napalmed man — "no longer covered with a skin, but with a crust-like crackling" — became a line across a book of poems. The USAF logged it differently: "you can kiss that group of villages goodbye." `[00:55:47]`
- **The numbers & the genocide question** `[01:00:36]`: the US dropped **635,000 tons of bombs + 32,557 tons of napalm** on Korea — more than the entire Pacific theater of WWII. 18 of 22 major NK cities at least 50% obliterated; Pyongyang 75% destroyed; eventually "it's hard to find good targets, for we have burned out almost everything." LeMay: "we… burned down every town in North Korea anyway… killed off what, 20% of the population? This seemed to be acceptable to everybody." Cumings: the UN Genocide Convention entered force in 1951 "just as the US Air Force was inflicting genocide… on the citizens of North Korea" — "genocidal in that it targeted a civilian population… time and time again," starting at the top (Lovett: "we're tearing up their society. We should just go right ahead and do it"). `[01:00:36]`

## Armistice `[01:05:07]`
- **1953** — Gen. Dean was released, shocked at the destruction he'd "celebrated within his own mind" (Huichon's two-story buildings "wasn't there anymore"); "I'm just a dog-faced soldier." Thompson's prescient line: "Soldiers have become the street cleaners of the new war." A final UN attack destroyed a NK dam providing water for 75% of the North's food. The **armistice was signed July 27, 1953** — "not an end to the war… but to the immediate military engagement." Eisenhower: "we have won an armistice on the single battleground, not peace in the world." Monica Kim: the armistice "puts into place a constant… deferral of addressing reunification" and the binary that Koreans must "choose between north or south." `[01:05:07]`
- **The toll** `[01:10:30]`: **2–4 million dead and missing**, most in the North — yet an Iowa court ruled that with no congressional declaration, "there had been no state of war against Korea at all." `[01:10:30]`
- **The Rosenbergs** `[01:11:14]`: **June 19, 1953** — Julius and Ethel Rosenberg executed at Sing Sing, the sentencing judge blaming them for Korean War dead ("casualties of that communist aggression were exceeding 50,000"). A Havana memorial reads: "For peace, bread, and roses, we will face the executioner." `[01:11:14]`
- **Coda** `[01:12:16]`: a US war-film narration — Marines who "can't put it into words" what they're fighting for, ending on "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness shall not perish from this earth." `[01:12:16]`
