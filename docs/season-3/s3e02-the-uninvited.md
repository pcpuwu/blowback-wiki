# Blowback S3E2 — "The Uninvited"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s3e02-the-uninvited.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:10:07
> **Thesis:** The deep history behind the Korean War — Korea's kingdoms, its colonization by Imperial Japan, the parallel rise of the American empire (Hawaii, the Philippines), the anti-Japanese resistance that produced Kim Il-sung, and the arbitrary US division of Korea at the **38th parallel** in 1945.
>
> _Narration order; `[hh:mm:ss]` = audio jump point; real dates in **bold**. Drawn heavily from Han Woo-keun's *History of Korea* and Bruce Cumings's *Korea's Place in the Sun*._

## Old Korea `[00:03:04]`
- The Koryŏ (10th c.) and Chosŏn (from 1400s, capital Seoul) dynasties unified the peninsula; a 15th-c. peak (movable type, an alphabet, civil-service exams). A rigid order: the **Yangban** aristocracy atop a Confucian/Buddhist scholar class, then commoners, outcasts (chŏnmin), and hereditary **slaves** (~30% of society). Decline followed 16th–17th-c. Japanese and Manchu invasions; greedy tax collectors drove peasant revolts. `[00:03:04]`

## The West and Japan pry Korea open `[00:08:00]`
- After the Opium Wars cracked open East Asia, Korea (long isolationist) resisted: the **General Sherman** incident (1866, an armed US schooner destroyed), the 1871 "**Little War with the Heathen**" (~650 Koreans killed). Meanwhile **Meiji Japan** modernized and grew impatient; unequal treaties followed (Japan 1876; the US, Germany, UK 1882). The **Tonghak** peasant movement (fair taxes, halt rice exports to Japan, **land redistribution**) triggered the **First Sino-Japanese War (1894–95)** — Japan crushed the uprisings and took Taiwan. `[00:08:00]`

## America's parallel empire `[00:22:00]`
- **Hawaii** — the 1893 planter coup (deposing Queen Liliʻuokalani), engineered with **John W. Foster** and the **Dole** family; annexed during the 1898 war. (Foster was the role model and grandfather of **John Foster Dulles**.) `[00:22:00]`
- **The Philippines** `[00:26:00]`: McKinley's "**benevolent assimilation**" (his "Christianize them" prayer); the **Philippine-American War (1899–1902)** killed over **1 million** (per Ken De Bevoise). The military governor: **Arthur MacArthur** — Douglas MacArthur's father (whose own aide called him the most egotistical man he'd met "until I met his son"). `[00:26:00]`
- **Lenin's 1917 prediction** that the US entered WWI partly to prepare for a future war with Japan over the Pacific. `[00:31:24]`

## Korea becomes a colony `[00:19:00]`
- The **Russo-Japanese War (1904–05)** — Japan, a non-white power, stunned the world by beating Russia; **Teddy Roosevelt** brokered the peace (and a Nobel) and blessed Japan's grip on Korea in a trade for US rights in the Philippines. **Aug 1910** — Korea formally becomes a **Japanese colony** (always seen as illegitimate). `[00:19:00]`

## Colonial Korea `[00:36:00]`
- A police-state colony: legalized racial discrimination, a national police force (up to **half Korean** collaborators), the Yangban landlords kept on to discipline peasants, and the seeds of the **chaebol** (Samsung, LG). The **March 1, 1919** independence movement (thousands killed) forced a softer "cultural policy," opening space in which two camps formed: the **communists** (the real social base — disciplined anti-Japanese fighters) and the Western-looking **nationalists** (Syngman Rhee's "gentlemen" lobbying in Washington). `[00:36:00]`
- **Kim Il-sung's rise** `[00:43:00]`: Japan's 1931 invasion of **Manchuria** sparked a huge guerrilla resistance (~200,000, ~90% Korean among CCP membership there). Kim became a famous, hunted guerrilla leader (a Japanese "get-Kim squad"). Amid Japanese-stoked purges of Korean communists as "spies," Kim narrowly escaped death and consolidated leadership (burning the purge files) — learning how expendable small nations' revolutions were to big powers like the USSR and China (a lesson that would matter in 1950). `[00:43:00]`
- **Overdevelopment**: Japan industrialized northern Korea (steel, chemicals, hydro) for the empire's benefit, uprooting peasants and creating a working class paid a third of Japanese workers — pushing them toward communism, which the colony treated as a national-security threat. `[00:48:00]`

## War and the bombs `[00:52:00]`
- **1937** — Japan invades China; the path to **Pearl Harbor** (1941). Patrick Cockburn's echo for our times: the 1941 US oil embargo on Japan was "spectacularly counterproductive." `[00:52:00]`
- WWII's end: Korean culture squashed; conscript labor (**a third of Japan's industrial workforce**); up to **200,000 Korean "comfort women"** enslaved. FDR dies (April 1945) → **Truman**; **LeMay's firebombing** of Japan (~100,000 dead in one Tokyo night). Soviets enter Manchuria; **Hiroshima** (Aug 6, ~140,000 dead, incl. ~10,000 Koreans), USSR declares war (Aug 8), **Nagasaki** (Aug 9, ~70,000) — Japan surrenders **to the US**. `[00:55:00]`

## The line `[01:08:00]`
- Korea remained agrarian (4 in 5 were tenants) — desperate for **land reform**. And on the night of **Aug 14, 1945**, two American colonels — **Dean Rusk** (later JFK's Secretary of State) and Charles Bonesteel — under pressure, with a National Geographic map and no Korea expertise, picked a "convenient dividing line" for the US/Soviet occupation zones: the **38th parallel**. `[01:08:00]`
