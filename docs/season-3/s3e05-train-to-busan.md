# Blowback S3E5 — "Train to Busan"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s3e05-train-to-busan.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:18:38
> **Thesis:** The week the war "broke out" (**June 25, 1950**) — and the host's central argument that it didn't simply *break out*. A year of South-instigated border clashes, secret Kim–Stalin–Mao correspondence, conspicuous US/Dulles/MacArthur meetings, and an oddly silent American intelligence apparatus precede Kim Il-sung's invasion. Then the rout: Seoul falls in three days, the ROK collapses, Truman commits ground troops as a "police action," and Rhee's retreating regime massacres tens of thousands of its own (Daejeon, No Gun Ri, Jeju).
>

## Cold open: Kenneth Shadrick `[00:01:08]`
- A 1950 American Communist Press pamphlet (*What's Happening in Korea*) eulogizes **Kenneth Shadrick**, a 19-year-old who left the West Virginia coal mines "to see the world," enlisted, and died near Sojung — knowing nothing of Korea, its demand for independence, or that the South he fought for was jailing and torturing union men. `[00:01:08]`
- Episode roadmap: clashes on the 38th parallel, the Stalin–Mao–Kim correspondence, conspicuous US meetings in Asia, and the fateful week of **June 25**. The hosts pose the through-line question: *who was ready for war, who wanted it, and was everything as the textbooks say? Can we even know?* `[00:02:35]`

## The Red Scare at home `[00:04:48]`
- HUAC's Hollywood hearings (Adolphe Menjou, Robert Taylor, Ronald Reagan testifying). Businessman **Alfred Kohlberg** — China Lobby figure, John Birch co-founder, Chiang devotee — backed magazines *Plain Talk* and *Counterattack*; in **June 1950** Counterattack published **Red Channels**, 151 supposed entertainment-industry communists — the first McCarthy-era blacklist (Orson Welles, Dorothy Parker, Lillian Hellman; first victim: actress **Jean Muir**, fired from NBC's *The Aldrich Family*). The home-front fervor was being whipped up *because of* the Far East situation. `[00:04:48]`

## A year of border clashes — and who started them `[00:07:35]`
- **Summer 1949** — clashes erupt along the 38th parallel during the dying gasps of the Jeju revolt. **Early May**, at Kaesong, Southern troops fired on the North (~400 NK troops + 100 civilians dead; two ROK companies *defected* north). Per internal American accounts (and US commander **Gen. Roberts**), **the South instigated most of the fighting**; the South then outnumbered the North 100,000 men, and its commanders publicly called for "recovering our lost land" by breaking the parallel. US Ambassador **Philip Jessup**: the parallel "is a real front line… constant fighting." `[00:07:35]`
- **Bruce Cumings** (interview): the forays ran May–Dec 1949, the South starting the majority; it peaked in early August 1949 when the South seized a promontory near the **Ongjin Peninsula** to get a view toward Pyongyang — the North counter-attacked and "beat the hell out of them." Rhee told Ambassador **Muccio** he wanted to take Chuncheon north of the line; it "nearly led to civil war right then." North Korea was *not* ready — tens of thousands of its troops were still fighting in the **Chinese Civil War**; it did not respond to major provocations. The last Southern assault was **December 1949**. `[00:09:42]`
- **The depth of US control** `[00:11:21]`: British minister Vivian Holt and Cumings document that America ran the South's government, army, economy, railroads, mines, electricity — even fueled every motor vehicle and supplied the films, Voice of America, scholarships, and baseball. South Korea got **>$100M/year** (mostly outright grants) against a total 1951 national budget of $120M. `[00:11:21]`

## Kim, Stalin, Mao — the secret correspondence `[00:12:50]`
- **Spring 1949** — Stalin meets Kim Il-sung ("Everything will be all right… only the southerners are making trouble"). North Korea had begun training guerrillas to infiltrate the South, but by late 1949 **Rhee's anti-communist police state had effectively subdued them**. `[00:12:50]`
- **Cumings** (interview): Soviet archives show *both* Rhee and Kim wanting "to settle the hash of the other side," each restrained by their respective ambassador. The North's calculation: respond to the *first big Southern provocation of summer 1950* — figuring it'd be a repeat of 1949 — using the battle-hardened divisions returning from China. Meanwhile Rhee was told by OSS deputy **Preston Goodfellow** that the US would defend the South *only if it didn't provoke* — so the South tried not to provoke, which was itself "provocative to Washington." "The North Koreans didn't get that memo." `[00:13:55]`
- **The UN angle** `[00:15:52]`: the UN Commission on Korea grew out of "worries about aggression emanating from the *South* more than the North"; the North called the observers a US ploy to "legalize interference." The **Korean aid bill (Feb 1950)** made aid contingent on excluding any coalition with communists — *American law had effectively made Korean unification illegal*. A US military-government official, asked about a Northern attack: "at this point, we rather invite it. It will give us target practice." `[00:15:52]`
- **The intelligence question** `[00:17:52]`: **I.F. Stone** and the NYT note the US almost certainly saw the buildup — evacuation ships were pre-positioned; a Navy admiral said the CIA knew an invasion could come "this week or next." Cumings's standing hypothesis: the US saw it coming via **signals intelligence** (14 Army Security Agency listening posts + coastal surveillance flights) "until I'm contradicted… until the materials come out." `[00:17:52]`
- **Kim's lobbying** `[00:19:46]`: from winter 1949 Kim pressed Stalin, who voiced "general sympathy" but refused all-out attack, fearing US confrontation. Mao (in Moscow Jan 1950, having just signed the Sino-Soviet treaty) stayed hesitant. By now the Soviets had supplied hardware and the Manchuria veterans were home (14,000 sent back per Marshal **Nie Rongzhen**). `[00:19:46]`
- **The secret Moscow & Beijing visits** `[00:21:57]`: Kim secretly visited Moscow **March 30–April 25, 1950**, pitching the South's ripeness, surprise, and unlikely US response. Stalin consented *to the idea of an attack* but insisted Kim clear it with Mao — "If you should get kicked in the teeth, I shall not lift a finger." Kim stopped in **Beijing**; Marshal **Peng Dehuai** recalled Mao disagreed "but had no way of really opposing or stopping it." `[00:21:57]`
- **Why Mao backed it** `[00:23:30]`: two theories — revolutionary solidarity (China was still finishing its own civil war), and *realpolitik over **Formosa/Taiwan***. Mao was preparing the PLA invasion of Taiwan and didn't want to jeopardize Soviet support for it; he couldn't voice his fear of US intervention in Korea without admitting the same risk for Formosa. (Acheson, March 15, denounced the Sino-Soviet treaty as "an evil omen of imperialistic domination.") Mao may have been kept partly in the dark; on the war's eve, China–North Korea relations in Pyongyang were so tense the Soviet embassy had to mediate. Kim got Stalin's tacit approval and supplies — **but no Soviet troops or air support**. `[00:23:30]`

## June 1950 — the conspicuous run-up `[00:26:14]`
- **Late May** — South Korea's parliamentary elections were "a disaster for Rhee," returning moderates and leftists, "most hoping for unification." Rhee looked ready to lose power — and sent for backup. `[00:26:14]`
- **The Dulles visit** `[00:27:01]`: **June 19**, **John Foster Dulles** (GOP foreign-policy leader, still Truman's advisor) addressed the new Assembly and met Rhee privately. Per Dulles's reporter William Matthews, Rhee "advocated an attack on the North," thought he could take it "in a few days," and would do it "even if it brought on a general war." The famous photo of Dulles in a top hat peering over the 38th parallel — "Pyongyang has never tired of waving that photo around." Dulles welcomed Korea into "the free world… any despotism which wages aggressive war dooms itself to unutterable disaster." `[00:27:01]`
- **The Japan dimension** `[00:30:14]`: journalist **Tim Shorrock** explains Dulles synergized Chiang's lobby, the South Koreans, and a *rebuilt Japan*. After Mao's 1949 victory, US policy shifted from reforming Japan to building it up as an economic Cold War power. When the war came, "Japan's economy was reborn overnight" — the State Department's own site still says "Korea came along and saved us" (Japan became the US war workshop: jeeps, ammunition). The catch: Japan's **June 4 elections** went badly for the US-friendly party (Socialists surged), so MacArthur agitated to outlaw the Japanese Communist Party — purging its 24-member Central Committee, banning "extreme" rallies (even a hygiene lecture and a violin concert were canceled). `[00:30:14]`
- **The military confab** `[00:33:09]`: a week before the war, top US/British/Australian brass met in the Pacific — Gen. **Omar Bradley** and Defense Secretary **Louis Johnson** cryptically seeking "most accurate info" on the Soviet military position. I.F. Stone: "no stranger coincidence in this story of strange coincidences." `[00:33:09]`
- **The strange silence** `[00:33:49]`: despite all this concern, *no warning* was issued to deter the North — and after the South Korean defense minister's **May 10** invasion-warning press conference, the usually loud Seoul government went quiet ("no press dispatches, no statements, no speeches in Congress"). Stone asks: did Rhee "receive advice that it would be wiser to invite or provoke an attack"? When war broke out, Truman was in Missouri, Acheson on a country retreat, Kennan in a summer cottage — and the Pentagon had, in the week of **June 18**, distributed war plan **SL-17**, which *assumed* a NK invasion, retreat to a Busan perimeter, and an Inchon landing. `[00:33:49]`

## June 25 — the invasion `[00:36:33]`
- **Cumings** (interview): the suppression of people's committees, Jeju, the guerrilla war, and 1949 border fighting made the NK leadership decide "we're not going to put up with this anymore. Our brothers and sisters are being slaughtered in the south." `[00:35:48]`
- **The attack** `[00:36:33]`: before dawn in the rain, the NK army struck with tanks. Two divisions (20,000+ men, 80 tanks) drove down the **Uijeongbu corridor** toward Seoul. NYT/Herald Tribune reporter **Marguerite Higgins** arrived in Seoul before its capture: Col. Sterling Wright called the situation "fluid but hopeful," blamed a "pathological fear of tanks"; South Koreans hit Soviet tanks with inadequate bazookas, watched volleys bounce off, made suicidal grenade/Molotov charges; then "they've broken through — run for it." `[00:36:33]`
- **Who fired first?** `[00:40:00]`: Dulles said in Honolulu the attack "came sooner than expected" — "no one asked him *when* he expected it." Most historians reject the NK claim that the South launched a general invasion, but there's live debate over what Southern troops were doing at **Haeju** and Ongjin on June 25. Cumings: the South *did* take Haeju that day, but the decisive tell is in captured NK documents — the North **lifted its own landmines** north of Haeju and Kaesong 48 hours before, "implementing their plans for an invasion at the very last minute." `[00:40:00]`

## The US snaps into crisis `[00:42:02]`
- **Acheson** drove the response ("Prestige is the shadow cast by power… American credibility was at stake"); South Korea was essential to Japan's revival ("Great Crescent" strategy). The US put the **Seventh Fleet** between Taiwan and the mainland; the Joint Chiefs were *not* consulted. The CIA's *Baptism by Fire* and the record show **Acheson acted as Truman's empowered envoy** before the president even returned. `[00:42:02]`
- **The UN vote** `[00:47:34]`: the Security Council voted **9–0** to condemn North Korea — possible only because the **USSR was boycotting** over the exclusion of communist China, so couldn't veto. Thirteen officials met over fried chicken at Blair House; Bradley said Korea "offered as good an occasion for drawing the line as anywhere"; Dean Rusk called it "a dagger pointed at Japan." Acheson's overnight study paper minimized the Koreans' unification drive and framed it as a "Soviet move." `[00:44:48]`
- **World reaction** `[00:45:57]`: **Nehru** caught flak in India for calling the North an aggressor (critics said he'd taken sides in a civil war); **Egypt** noted the West's selective interest in "police actions" (cf. Palestine); even Canada urged "no hysterics, please." `[00:45:57]`
- **"Police action"** `[00:47:34]`: Seoul fell **June 28**, three days in. MacArthur (June 30) advised ground troops were needed; Truman, seizing a reporter's phrase, called it a **"police action"** — committing US soldiers with no congressional war resolution. The CIA judged Southern resistance wouldn't last 24 more hours. MacArthur cabled: "Be of good cheer. Momentous events are pending." `[00:47:34]`
- **The Han bridge** `[00:48:21]`: as people fled, ROK engineers **blew the main Han River bridge with no warning** — hundreds killed in the blast, many more drowned. The North freed thousands of leftists from Seodaemun prison; people's courts executed national policemen. The ROK army lost 44,000 of 98,000 men; Rhee's regime answered the collapse "the only way it knew how — methods of terror," disappearing villagers across the South. `[00:48:21]`

## MacArthur ascends `[00:50:02]`
- I.F. Stone found it "more extraordinary than the oversight itself" that MacArthur *admitted* being surprised — out of character — pointing again to the Dulles visit. MacArthur became **Commander-in-Chief Far East**; he had only ill-equipped, non-battle-ready divisions in Japan, and planned the US base at **Busan**. `[00:50:02]`
- **Truman vs. MacArthur begins** `[00:51:29]`: MacArthur requested ~30,000 reinforcements; Washington denied it ("amazed" him). Truman's instructions hinted at escalation (plans to "wipe out all Soviet air bases in the Far East" — *plans, not action*). Truman wanted containment; MacArthur was tallying its true cost. James Reston called MacArthur a "sovereign power." Key dates: **July 7** UN names MacArthur head of allied forces; **July 8** he authorizes Japan to grow its army to 100,000; **July 13** Nehru's peace notes to Stalin and Acheson (Stalin: reactivate the Security Council, i.e., admit Red China; Acheson: that would be "coercion and duress"). `[00:51:29]`
- **The blank check** `[00:54:35]`: Rhee declared a 3-year state of emergency; UN forces served under MacArthur's "unified command" — but he had no obligation to clear strategy with the UN. Stone: "the United Nations forces subject to MacArthur without making MacArthur subject to the United Nations." William Blum: "it was to be an American show." `[00:54:35]`
- **The Formosa gambit** `[00:55:58]`: against Truman's July 9 olive branch to China (no US designs on Formosa), MacArthur flew to **Formosa** in late July to meet Chiang and "change his boss's mind" — calling for a "common crusade of liberation against the new regime on the mainland." Stone: MacArthur "was pretty plainly trying to subvert any chance at a negotiated peace" — drowning out the quiet voices urging mediation, just as the USSR was "eating humble pie" and returning to the Security Council. MacArthur's VFW message (praising Truman's June 27 decision as "a lamp of hope throughout Asia") earned a presidential scolding. His plan: an **amphibious landing** behind enemy lines. `[00:55:58]`

## The summer rout and the massacres `[01:00:00]`
- The KPA swept south "with one humiliating defeat after another for American forces." The **First Marine Brigade** halted the advance in early August; the front froze at the **Busan perimeter**. Kim's plan was to win in one month — and by end of July "he had nearly done so." `[01:00:00]`
- **Rhee's death machine** `[01:00:41]`: forced evacuations and village burnings of any suspected leftist town; ROK officers executed their own (an AWOL man shot in the head, kicked into a grave). Rhee's **National Guidance Alliance** turned from "re-education" into mass extermination — of ~**330,000 rounded up, ~200,000 killed nationwide** (Su-kyoung Hwang), highest in Kyeongsang; victims targeted for *possible* "suspicious intent." On Jeju, 344 arrested and 218 executed; a NK pamphlet's photo corroborated the **Ochang massacre** (~1,000 "driven away like pigs" to an execution site). `[01:00:41]`
- **The North in Seoul** `[01:01:53]`: the city was *not* burned (discipline, or plans to make it the capital); people's committees and courts returned with real retribution against right-wingers, but Hwang cautions the "barbarism" reports were "hearsay" from a handful of fled personalities, "stretched to the extreme to incite fear" — echoing the exaggerated Batista-execution reports from S2's Cuba. `[01:01:53]`
- **No Gun Ri** `[01:04:46]`: late July, US troops evacuated villages; hundreds of refugees herded to **No Gun Ri** were stopped at a roadblock, then hit by a US airstrike and ground fire ("it looked like heaven crashed on us"). It fit Air Force/Navy policy: "strafe all civilian refugee parties approaching our positions," "attack any group of more than eight" — to root out infiltrators. Suppressed until the late 1990s. `[01:04:46]`
- **The Daejeon massacre** `[01:06:53]`: the largest — ~**7,000** executed by ROK officers near Daejeon in July before retreating, to deny the North support. Reported at the time only by NK's *Haebang Ilbo* and the British *Daily Worker* (correspondent **Alan Winnington**: "7,000 people horribly butchered… under the supervision of American officers… this is an American crime"). Britain seized Winnington's passport and considered treason charges; Acheson demanded an official denial; the US Embassy called it "atrocity fabrication." Yet US Army officers had *photographed* the killings ("grimly reminiscent of the Nazi Holocaust"); a classified report confirmed ~1,800+ executed over three days, "orders undoubtedly from the top level." The US instead issued a document blaming the deaths on the North — "and for 40 years, that is what people knew." `[01:06:53]`

## What the war revealed `[01:10:18]`
- **Rhee fled** by car and special train to the southern tip within a week (a beach home, then Mokpo); half the South Korean elite planned exile to Japan. Ordinary Koreans didn't know how far their president had run — or that **48 of 210 legislators** had pledged allegiance to Kim's DPRK. Masuda Hajimu: most people *stayed put* (workers, peasants, businessmen, intellectuals alike) — the opposite of what mass loyalty to Rhee would have produced. `[01:10:18]`
- **Gen. William Dean** `[01:12:12]`: the US general (and former military governor who'd rejected the Jeju peace deal and futilely protested the Daejeon massacre) was captured south of Daejeon, wandered a month, became a POW for years. His memoir notes the **Inmin'gun**'s discipline (paid farmers for peaches even with worthless currency) and the population's reception: "if the people of South Korea resented the northern invaders, they certainly weren't showing it… enthusiasm and passive acceptance." Children chanted "Choson-ul" (a united-Korea slogan) to his guard. Hanley/Cho/Mendoza: the Americans "did not understand that many South Koreans despised the government the US Army was defending." `[01:12:12]`
- **"Koreans invade Korea"** `[01:15:14]`: a few US senators dissented early (one Utah Democrat: "legally, this was a civil war and not an act of aggression" — what Stephen Casey calls "apostasy" quickly drowned out). A British official drew the US Civil War analogy: America would never have tolerated "an imaginary line" between North and South, nor foreign intervention for one side. Ex-State Department advisor **William Polk** (a JFK missile-crisis aide): the precipitating event was Rhee's **declaration of Southern independence**, which would have permanently blocked unification — "bluntly put, Rhee offered Kim an opportunity he could not refuse." Cumings closes: Kim crossed a five-year-old line, "not an international boundary," that "bisected a nation… going back to antiquity" — *Koreans invading Korea disrupts the received wisdom.* `[01:15:14]`
- **The pause** `[01:18:12]`: by late June the KPA paused south of Seoul for nearly a week (supply lines? cold feet? having only expected to reach Seoul?). MacArthur's words hang over the rest: "Be of good cheer. Momentous events are pending." `[01:18:12]`
