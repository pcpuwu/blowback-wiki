# Blowback S3E8 — "American Caesar"

> 📄 **[Read the full transcript](transcripts/s3e08-american-caesar.md)**


> **Hosts:** Brendan James & Noah Kulwin · **Runtime:** ~1:21:00
> **Thesis:** The Truman–MacArthur showdown reaches its climax. MacArthur, hungering for nuclear war with China, repeatedly goes over his president's head until Truman fires him (April 1951) — yet the "moderates" quietly shared his nuclear anxiety (A-bombs shipped to Guam/Okinawa). Meanwhile the war's two new fronts open: the POW camps (and "voluntary repatriation"), the brainwashing panic that birthed MK-Ultra, and the credible allegations of US biological warfare.

## Cold open: Christmas Eve 1950 `[00:00:10]`
- GIs were supposed to be home by now. Instead MacArthur cabled Washington for permission to use **nuclear weapons** on the Chinese border — claiming in his memoirs he'd planned a "modest" 30–50 atomic bombs. The command evacuated Rhee and others from Seoul to prepare for what Stone called "a phantom battle with a phantom foe." Stone's logic: if Manchuria was to be bombed, better get the troops out of Korea first — so MacArthur was acting as if the US would use nukes regardless of Truman's explicit sole authority. `[00:00:10]`
- Roadmap: China's counterattack pushes the UN back below the parallel; Truman declares a national emergency and the US plans a "nuclear solution"; MacArthur grows ever more daring to widen the war; the MacArthur faction (with McCarthy and Nixon) rips open the split with the "moderates" who wanted a Korean war, not a world war. And the rising figure of the **POW** as a propaganda weapon. `[00:02:30]`

## The Third Phase `[00:04:56]`
- The Sino-Korean **third phase** began New Year's Eve 1950; by **Jan 4** the UN abandoned Seoul, then Inchon. Tokyo HQ flooded the press with "hordes of red Chinese" — Stone noted the generals fought with "a sharp eye on public opinion, almost as if… engaged less in military actions than in gigantic advertising campaigns." Congress demanded a second front from Chiang and withdrawal from Korea; Truman pushed back, wanting the bloodbath "nice and neat and confined to the Korean Peninsula." MacArthur's forces **burned Seoul before any enemy approached** — AP flew over and "found a dead city. Where, everyone asked, was the enemy?" MacArthur invented the "**philosophy of if**" to justify the abandonment (Seoul left not under attack but on a calculation that *if* far-off forces *might* outflank it). `[00:04:56]`
- **Firing the bomb-talkers** `[00:08:18]`: with British urging, Truman fired the bomber-command general who'd talked of an "atomic offensive" ("obviously he doesn't speak for the Air Force"). Stone's Hitler comparison: just as Prussian aristocrats "swallowed rapidly and went along with Hitler," the White House, State, and Pentagon "all went along with MacArthur." `[00:08:18]`
- **General "Rosie" O'Donnell** `[00:10:11]`: Emmett O'Donnell, reassigned from Far East bombing command, told the NYT there was "nothing left to bomb unless the US was willing to bomb China or use the A-bomb" — 43,000 tons dropped, "not a single refinery left." He all but said the atomic bomb was an integral part of US armament — revealing the strategic bombing had been designed to "deliver the atomic offensive to the heart of the enemy." MacArthur had been "fighting to secure a pathway for nuclear bombs on the Chinese." But there were no hordes. By summer 1951 O'Donnell testified "the entire… Korean peninsula is a mess. Everything is destroyed." `[00:10:11]`

## Peng's exhausted army `[00:13:35]`
- **Peng Dehuai**'s memoir: the third phase took Seoul, crossed the Han, recovered Inchon — but he saw the Americans wising up, pulling back exactly the distance his foot-soldiers could advance in a night, "leading us into the trap of assaulting their well-defended positions." No air support, no anti-aircraft, raided daily; troops had lost "almost half their men" and "badly needed a rest." Bin Yu: 280,000 poorly-supplied, exhausted Chinese faced 230,000 well-equipped UN/ROK troops. `[00:13:35]`
- **The fateful pause** `[00:16:04]`: Peng insisted on a 3–4 month pause; Kim and Stalin didn't understand his caution. Mao, "thirsty for a final win," held parades in Beijing declaring Korea's impending liberation and urged a final sweep to Busan — reversing his own earlier warning about cornering the UN. They compromised on a two-month pause. Bin Yu: Chinese veterans later thought calling it a day here "might have won far better gains… in the long term." But would MacArthur have allowed that? `[00:16:04]`

## The diplomatic squeeze `[00:17:24]`
- Popular support had cratered: from 70% backing (June 1950) to 49% calling entry a mistake (six months later). The only active pressure on Truman was to *do more* — except from Britain. PM **Attlee** feared a wider nuclear war (a China blockade as prelude to a Chiang invasion); the British press had already pegged that as MacArthur's real aim. To reassert credibility the US sought the UN fig leaf — but the USSR had ended its boycott, so the US worked the **General Assembly** (no veto) instead of the Security Council. **Jan 13** two Joint Chiefs read MacArthur a personal letter from Truman so he couldn't "misread" it — yet Manchester says MacArthur "chose the interpretation which suited him," pretending a China blockade was now policy. `[00:17:24]`
- **UN Resolution 498** `[00:20:33]`: **Feb 1, 1951**, the US forced through the General Assembly a resolution branding **Red China the aggressor** — the first time the UN named an "aggressor state"; a factor in China boycotting the UN for 20 years (only Malik dissented). Nixon, the freshly-elected 38-year-old junior senator, demanded the UN "do more than merely brand" China — cut off all trade including British trade with Hong Kong. `[00:20:33]`
- **Ridgway rising** `[00:23:26]`: Gen. **Matthew Ridgway** (new 8th Army commander, after Walker died in a jeep crash) was winning on the ground, drawing the Joint Chiefs away from MacArthur — and a "creeping suspicion that MacArthur might be losing the plot," detached in Tokyo from actually running the war. `[00:23:26]`

## Censorship and the destruction of Wonsan `[00:23:52]`
- MacArthur imposed a censorship regime "far more severe than any known in WWII." Soldiers' letters were full of complaints (conditions, equipment, no rotation policy); all front information was now censored. A UP correspondent: "much of the truth about the Korean War… has been red-penciled" — be "particularly suspicious of all casualty counts." The British press mocked "Fairy tales from Korea." `[00:23:52]`
- **Wonsan** `[00:26:00]`: in February the UN began a **naval blockade of Wonsan** lasting over two years; a US admiral boasted of bombarding it for 41 straight days and nights — "the longest sustained naval or air bombardment of a city in history." "In Wonsan, you cannot walk the streets… It is a city of death." ~80% reduced to rubble; the 35,000 residents reduced to "suicide groups" living in caves. A page from LeMay's WWII playbook (his "death list" napalm of Japanese cities). `[00:26:00]`
- **The Solomon contrast** `[00:28:09]`: the London Times reported the communists *rejected* scorched earth, leaving the countryside intact — Stone's parallel to Solomon and the two mothers (the true one wouldn't see the child cut in half). A NYT correspondent: the communists "left their homes and schools standing in retreat," while UN troops "left only blackened spots where towns once stood." (Adjacent on the front page: the **Rosenbergs** and Morton Sobell convicted of atomic spying.) `[00:28:09]`

## March — MacArthur goes rogue `[00:29:47]`
- **March 1** Washington denied MacArthur permission to bomb Manchurian power plants. **March 7** he predicted an "inevitable military stalemate" without reinforcements and freedom to strike China, casting the lull as an enemy plot. **March 10** (Cumings) he asked for a "**D-Day atomic capability**" after intelligence suggested Soviet air divisions and bombers might move to Manchuria. **March 14**: Air Force Gen. Vandenberg — "believe everything set." `[00:29:47]`
- **The Joe Martin letter** `[00:32:18]`: GOP House Minority Leader **Joe Martin** wrote (March 8) seeking MacArthur's views and backing a "second Asiatic front" with Chiang. MacArthur replied (March 20), *not* asking for confidentiality: "the communist conspirators have elected to make their play for global conquest" in Asia; "if we lose the war to communism in Asia, the fall of Europe is inevitable… **There is no substitute for victory.**" `[00:32:18]`
- **The ultimatum** `[00:34:35]`: that same day Acheson was drafting a careful UN-approved peace statement (the Joint Chiefs telling MacArthur diplomacy should precede any advance north — "there very much was a substitute for victory… a line on a map"). MacArthur's response: demand no further restrictions. Then **March 24**, without informing Washington, he issued his **own ultimatum to China** — offering to personally meet the enemy commander to settle a truce (everything but a threat to crush China). His aim, per an advisor, was to stop "one of the most disgraceful plots in US history" — the campaign to negotiate peace. `[00:34:35]`
- Washington ordered: no public announcements of any kind. Acheson said mildly MacArthur had touched on political issues beyond his role; the joint UN statement was never revived. US/ROK troops crossed the parallel again (March 26); by April 7, nine divisions were in a new offensive. `[00:36:42]`

## The firing `[00:38:17]`
- The Joint Chiefs: "it would be perfectly obvious if it were anybody else… He would be relieved of his command." But MacArthur was "the most popular man in America"; an advisor called his statement "probably the most popular… anyone has ever made" (Schaller: it "promised victory, peace, and a quick exit"). The administration stayed quiet "for fear of getting on the wrong side of Mr. America." Then **April 5** Joe Martin read MacArthur's letter on the House floor — and Truman's diary shows this drove him over the edge: "The big general in the Far East must be recalled." `[00:38:17]`
- **The hidden nuclear deal** `[00:40:46]`: Truman had more on his mind. Acheson and Harriman had earlier vetoed sending LeMay ("Mr. Atom Bomb") to Korea lest he "excite people unduly." The very day Truman decided to fire MacArthur, the AEC learned the Joint Chiefs wanted **atomic bombs transferred to military control** and delivered to the Pacific, plus draft orders letting MacArthur strike China on major escalation. Bradley urged sending A-bombs to Guam; the AEC chair authorized transfer to **Guam and Okinawa** under LeMay's Strategic Air Command — concealed from MacArthur lest he act prematurely. (Days later, as cover for the firing, the AEC told Congress LeMay would *not* now control the nukes — a sop to Republicans that didn't work.) `[00:40:46]`
- **April 11** `[00:43:38]`: at 1 AM Truman's press secretary announced the firing (Ridgway to take command). MacArthur heard from his wife, who whispered it during a lunch with a visiting senator. But **Herbert Hoover** reached him first — Republican leaders wanting him "straight home… before Truman and Marshall and their crowd of propagandists can smear you." Hoover "offering Caesar his crown." `[00:43:38]`

## The POW front `[00:45:44]`
- The war kept growing unpopular (MacArthur's doom-mongering, rising costs) — and a new dominant figure emerged. Monica Kim: "the prisoner of war emerged to eclipse the citizen soldier." 4,428 US POWs were repatriated; 88% captured in the first year, many held nearly three years. `[00:45:44]`
- **The camps** `[00:47:38]`: the US held vast numbers of *Korean* POWs (1,145 in Aug 1950 → 137,000 by Dec 1950). UN-camp conditions were brutal — 66% of 7,614 recorded POW deaths from infectious disease (TB, dysentery), 600/month in the winter 1950–51 dysentery epidemic. The largest camp: **Koje Island** (Jan 1951), built partly by prisoners' forced labor, ~170,000 people — designated a "leftist territory" because its peasants backed land reform. `[00:47:38]`
- **Voluntary repatriation** `[00:49:47]`: Truman's state of emergency let the US handle a new reality. POWs protested being forced into **red uniforms** (echoing Japanese colonial death-row dress) — guards killed three, an incident ruled "justified." After MacArthur's firing the new **Psychological Strategy Board (PSB)** pushed "voluntary repatriation" — a departure from the Geneva standard of returning POWs to their country of origin. Kim: its real aim was "the continuing de-recognition of North Korea as a sovereign state," forcing each prisoner to "choose freedom or communism" — making non-political actors into political ones. Koje saw 102 incidents in its first year (28 with injury/death), most provocations from *non-communist* prisoners and guards; tensions would explode by mid-1952. `[00:49:47]`

## Brainwashing `[00:55:09]`
- **Ronald Reagan** narrated a post-war documentary on American POWs, framing them as having "weaknesses in the American character" (can't think independently, opportunist) that the Chinese exploited. American interrogators of Koreans had no language skills (leaning on resented Japanese colleagues); the Chinese interrogated Americans fluently, with an all-day, seven-day "educational program." Monica Kim: the POWs were working-class kids, immigrants' children, racial minorities (Puerto Rico, the Philippines, Louisiana, Ohio, Texas) — and North Korean interrogators, many US-college-educated, were "eager to talk with POWs from working class and racial minority backgrounds." The Reagan film cast them as "rubes, weak-willed and easily manipulated." `[00:55:09]`
- **The "circle"** `[00:58:42]`: white American and South Korean prisoners formed "KKK similar groups" in the camps — the "circle" named for surrounding and beating a prisoner who wrote pro-communist articles. The brainwashing narrative held that Sino-Soviet mind manipulation had subverted clean American consciences — and that returnees came home "strangers to one another." (Spliced with *Invasion of the Body Snatchers* dialogue.) `[00:58:42]`

## MK-Ultra and the mind-control programs `[01:01:03]`
- John Marks's *The Search for the Manchurian Candidate* (1979) exposed **MK-Ultra** (formalized 1953), successor to programs like **Artichoke** and **Bluebird**. The behavior-control program got going after Hungary's 1949 trial of Cardinal **Mindszenty** — the CIA believing some "unknown force" had been used (despite later concluding none had). **Operation Bluebird** was approved **April 20, 1950**; three months later a team went to Tokyo to try behavioral techniques on human subjects (suspected double agents); ~October 1950 they used "advanced techniques" on 25 subjects, apparently North Korean POWs. `[01:01:03]`
- **The word "brainwashing"** `[01:04:01]`: coined for Americans in a **Sept 24, 1950** Miami Daily News exclusive by **Edward Hunter** ("brainwashing… the principal activity on the Chinese mainland") — Hunter being a former OSS man who'd worked for the CIA. His piece presented communist conversion as self-evident and nefarious ("brain reform," "brain-changing"), name-dropping Mindszenti again. `[01:04:01]`

## Biological warfare `[01:05:37]`
- A North Korean propaganda reel features a captured US pilot (shot down 1952) confessing to dropping **germ bombs** (fuseless "duds") on Jan 4 and 11. China, the USSR, and Korea knew such weapons from fighting Japan's **Unit 731** (Shiro Ishii) — whose exfiltration into the Western fold the show covered earlier. As the CIA fixated on the drugged-Cardinal story, the USSR ran the 1949 **Khabarovsk trials** of 12 Japanese bio-warfare officials — noting Ishii and colleagues were "sheltering in Tokyo… under the reactionary wing." `[01:05:37]`
- **The allegations** `[01:08:06]`: **May 8, 1951**, NK foreign minister **Pak Hon-yong** made the first public accusation that the US used biological weapons (Dec 1950–Jan 1951). Thomas Powell: disease-infected **chicken feathers** (Ishii's "pet invention") were scattered in villagers' homes during the UN retreat; smallpox broke out ~two weeks later (~3,500 cases, 10% mortality). **Wilfred Burchett** reported four widely-separated cities infected with smallpox simultaneously 6–7 days after US troops withdrew — incompatible with natural transmission, the bacteria "of artificial culture." `[01:08:06]`
- **The plague ship** `[01:12:49]`: March 1951 — Gen. Crawford Sams personally led a mission behind enemy lines (stated intent: kidnap a North Korean plague victim) aboard a craft Newsweek dubbed the "Bubonic Plague Ship" at Wonsan. Powell's father, journalist John Powell, was federally prosecuted for reporting the ship was "actually loaded with bacteriological installations… used for testing germ weapons" on prisoners. `[01:12:49]`
- **The pilots' confessions & the Needham report** `[01:14:02]`: the most famous allegations came from two US pilots over Beijing radio (May 1952) — the US calling them forced/fabricated confessions, but Powell argues the technical detail (BW bomb mechanisms, loading protocols, sortie dates, briefing-officer names) "precludes the possibility of invented information." Jeffrey Kaye finds the allegations convincing. An **Independent Scientific Commission** led by the eminent British scientist **Joseph Needham** issued a unanimous 1952 report endorsing the BW claims; Barenblatt notes the delivery systems were "identical to methods confirmed to have been used by the Japanese against China" (per Khabarovsk). `[01:14:02]`
- **Plausible deniability** `[01:16:57]`: the US couldn't be definitively pinned partly because the Joint Chiefs "conveniently lost" decades of meeting minutes before FOIA (1974) — no paper trail. The US never refuted the actual argument; it "pirouetted," both denying germ warfare and calling the allegations a communist trick. And among those evaluating the returned flyers: MK-Ultra figure **Dr. Louis "Jolly" West** (later examined Jack Ruby and Patty Hearst), with the returnees interrogated by Army Counterintelligence and the CIA. `[01:16:57]`
- **The payoff** `[01:19:00]`: "brainwashing" solved problems — it explained the 21 American soldiers who chose to stay in Korea, and justified the aggressive US propaganda effort. The Cold War would go on "not only to prevent the communists from taking over Korea, but to prevent them from taking over your mind." The Reagan film closes by warning the same "moral fallout" threatens children "promoted in school though failing" and citizens who "vote uninformed" — "the ultimate weapon for this battle is the mind." `[01:19:00]`
